# Chapter 8.3 ‚Äî Project: the welcome email send path: pedagogical approach

## Concept 1 ‚Äî The cuts panel: what 8.3 ships and what later units own

**Why it's hard.** The student opens the project brief and the reflex is to ship "the email layer" ‚Äî bounce-webhook, batch sends, audit log, rate limiter, marketing list, multi-tenant custom domains, all reachable from where the chapter ends. The senior move is the inverse: ship the canonical send path ‚Äî one wrapper, one template, one action, one verified domain ‚Äî and *nothing else*, with every later layer named and assigned to the unit that owns it. Without the cut named explicitly, the student either pads the scope (loses the deadline, blurs the discipline) or shortcuts the disciplines the cut is designed to install (skips the suppression read, makes the idempotency key optional, wraps Resend in a generic adapter). The cut *is* the lesson, and the unit graph the cuts point at is what the chapter cashes in for in Units 9‚Äì14.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **cuts-and-carry-forwards table** in the brief lesson, echoing 6.6 and 7.6's project-chapter framing so the student recognizes the shape across the curriculum. Two columns: *In scope* (one verified domain, one wrapper, one suppression read, one template, one Server Action, one inspector page, the six "Done when" clauses) and *Cut, with the unit that owns it* (bounce/complaint webhook ‚Üí 12.1.5, batch sends ‚Üí 14, marketing email ‚Üí out of curriculum, per-tenant custom-domain sending ‚Üí out of curriculum, rate-limiter on the action ‚Üí 15.4, audit-log on the send ‚Üí 10/17, real auth replacing the stub ‚Üí 9, React Hook Form ‚Üí 7.4 owns the threshold and the project doesn't trip it). A third row at the bottom: *forward-loaded disciplines* ‚Äî `lib/email.ts` is the chokepoint Unit 9's verification email and Unit 10's invitation email both call into; the `email_suppressions` table is the read this chapter installs and the webhook in 12.1.5 will populate; the `Result<{id}>` shape extends the 7.2 union with one new `'suppressed'` code that every later send inherits; the `getActiveContext()` stub call site is shaped exactly the way Unit 9's session reader will drop in. The senior posture lands inline: the cut items are not missing ‚Äî they are owned by their owning units, and this chapter's job is the canonical send-path shape they all extend.

**Engagement.** A `Buckets` sort: twelve feature descriptions ("a `sendEmail` wrapper that reads `email_suppressions` before calling Resend", "a webhook handler that writes to `email_suppressions` from Resend bounce events", "a `<WelcomeEmail />` template with `PreviewProps`", "a marketing-broadcast UI with audience segments", "an idempotency key on every transactional send", "a per-IP rate limiter on the inspector's send button", "an `org_audit_log` row for every send", "a Server Action that posts the form to `sendEmail`", "a Better Auth verification email that re-uses `lib/email.ts`", "a Trigger.dev task that calls `sendEmail` from a durable job body", "a React Hook Form-controlled email input with `Controller`", "an MX-record probe on the recipient before sending") into "in scope for 8.3" or "owned by a later chapter (name it)." Wrong-answer feedback names which discipline the cut protects.

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-coded HTML table (in-scope / cut-and-owned-by / forward-loaded) for the cuts-and-carries panel. Single-use static composition; the same shape 6.6 Concept 1 and 7.6 Concept 1 use, recurring across project chapters.
- `Buckets` for the in-scope-vs-owned-elsewhere sort.
- `Aside` (`tip`) below the panel: "the cut items are not missing ‚Äî they are owned by their owning units; this chapter's job is the canonical send-path shape they all extend."

**Project link.** This concept *is* lesson 8.3.1's framing ‚Äî the student returns to the panel at the end of every build lesson to confirm they're still on the inside of the cut, and at 8.3.5 to confirm the forward-loaded disciplines paid off.

## Concept 2 ‚Äî The cheap-real-domain prerequisite, and the prohibition of `onboarding@resend.dev`

**Why it's hard.** Two reflexes compete and both fail the chapter. (1) "The sandbox sender is what beginners use ‚Äî `onboarding@resend.dev` is fine for a course project." It isn't: Resend's shared sandbox sender is widely deny-listed, the chapter's "Done when" clauses include DKIM-pass on the *student's domain* (the sandbox doesn't satisfy that), and the muscle memory the project installs depends on the student walking the verified-domain ceremony with hands on their own DNS panel. (2) "I don't have a domain ‚Äî I'll skip the chapter or fake it." The senior framing says: a `.com` is $8‚Äì12/year at Namecheap, Porkbun, or Cloudflare Registrar, the setup is one-time, every later unit (auth verification, invites, billing receipts) re-uses the same domain. The prerequisite is a one-paragraph senior call, but landing it as *non-negotiable* before lesson 2 starts is the whole point ‚Äî students who try the chapter on the sandbox get to 8.3.5 and can't verify a single Done-when clause.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Decision archetype delivered as a **two-path verdict panel** rendered as a `Figure`-wrapped hand SVG. Two paths drawn side by side: the *sandbox path* (left lane) starts at "use `onboarding@resend.dev`" and ends at five red X marks, one per Done-when clause that fails ‚Äî DKIM-pass on student domain (sandbox isn't your domain), inbox arrival (sandbox lands in spam at most providers), reply-to to a monitored mailbox (sandbox controls reply-to), professional from address (sandbox shows `onboarding@resend.dev`), reputation isolation (shared with everyone else's test sends). The *real-domain path* (right lane) starts at "buy a $10/year `.com` and verify a `send.<domain>` subdomain" and ends at five green checks ‚Äî every clause becomes verifiable. Below the lanes, a small reference panel: three registrar options with cost, the "use a subdomain on your existing portfolio domain" carve-out for students who already own one, and the one-line rule ‚Äî *every later transactional unit reuses this domain and key, the cost amortizes across half the curriculum*. The artifact is a pre-flight check that gates entry to the chapter.

**Engagement.** A `MultipleChoice` round of three scenarios: *"I already own a portfolio domain at `myname.dev`"* ‚Üí use `send.myname.dev` as the sending subdomain, no second purchase needed; *"I want to skip the cost and use the sandbox sender for now"* ‚Üí blocked, the chapter's verification can't pass on the sandbox, the chapter's discipline doesn't install without the real DNS walk; *"I'll use my Gmail address as the from"* ‚Üí blocked, you can't authenticate `gmail.com` you don't control, this is the SPF/DKIM ownership rule from 8.1.2 made operational. Wrong-answer feedback names the Done-when clause that breaks for each.

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand SVG of the two-path verdict panel (sandbox vs. real domain) with five-clause check/X grids per lane and the registrar reference panel below. Single-use static composition; the picture is the gate.
- `MultipleChoice` for the three-scenario round.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below: "the sandbox sender is for SDK smoke tests, not for shipping a transactional surface; the chapter's verification fails on it by design."

**Project link.** This concept gates entry to lesson 8.3.2 ‚Äî the student arrives at the verified-domain ceremony having already accepted the cost and chosen the registrar, with no friction left in the path.

## Concept 3 ‚Äî The verified-domain ceremony at the student's own keyboard

**Why it's hard.** Lesson 8.1's chapter taught the ceremony in the abstract ‚Äî Resend issues records, the registrar publishes them, verification flips. The project chapter is where the student does it on their own DNS panel against their own domain, and the failures are all hands-on: the registrar UI takes the DKIM host as `resend._domainkey` on Cloudflare but `resend._domainkey.send` on Namecheap (relative vs. absolute conventions), the long DKIM TXT value gets truncated by some registrars' input fields, the DMARC record at `_dmarc.send.<domain>` covers only the subdomain and leaves the apex unprotected. The student who reads 8.1 and never touches DNS hits these the first time at 8.3.2 and either spends an hour fighting them or skips the verification entirely. The chapter has to walk the registrar-specific differences, the failure-mode triage (`dig` to confirm what was actually published), and the DMARC-at-apex inheritance rule ‚Äî without those, the verification stalls past the 30-minute mark and the student bounces.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Setup archetype delivered as a **registrar-aware ceremony walkthrough** in `Steps`, paired with a small **failure-triage decision tree** in `Figure`. The `Steps` walks the canonical seven-step flow: (1) Resend dashboard ‚Üí "Add domain" ‚Üí enter `send.<your-domain>`; (2) Resend issues SPF TXT, DKIM TXT (selector `resend._domainkey`), optional MX; (3) at the registrar, add the records ‚Äî with three TabItem-style sub-paths for Cloudflare, Namecheap, and Porkbun showing each registrar's host-field convention so the student picks their lane and skips the others; (4) wait for verification (typically minutes, up to 24h), the dashboard flips to `Verified`; (5) publish DMARC at `_dmarc.<apex>` (not at the subdomain ‚Äî covers subdomains by inheritance) with `v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:...`; (6) fire a test send from Resend's dashboard to a personal Gmail; (7) "Show original" ‚Üí confirm SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass. The paired `Figure` carries a small triage flowchart: *verification stalled past 30 min?* ‚Üí run `dig TXT resend._domainkey.send.<domain> +short`, compare against Resend's issued value, three branches (no record returned ‚Üí registrar didn't publish or used wrong host; record returned but truncated ‚Üí registrar's input field truncated, re-paste from Resend; record matches ‚Üí wait longer, propagation can take 24h). The triage tree turns the unblocking gate into a 5-minute diagnostic instead of an unbounded wait.

**Engagement.** Because the artifact is a real-world DNS walk, the assessment is the verification itself ‚Äî the student's Resend dashboard shows `Verified` and a test send arrives in their inbox with SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass lines. Confirm with a short `MultipleChoice` round of two: *"the dashboard still shows Pending after 45 minutes ‚Äî what's the next move?"* ‚Üí run `dig` against the published host and compare to Resend's value, the most common cause is a host-field convention mismatch or a value truncation, not propagation; *"DMARC published at `_dmarc.send.<domain>` instead of `_dmarc.<apex>` ‚Äî what's the failure mode?"* ‚Üí the apex is unprotected, spammers can spoof `From: <user>@<apex>` because no DMARC record applies; the apex DMARC inherits to subdomains, the inverse doesn't hold.

**Components.**
- `Steps` for the seven-step canonical ceremony, with three `TabItem` panels at step 3 for the three common registrars' host-field conventions.
- `Figure` wrapping a hand SVG of the failure-triage flowchart (stall ‚Üí `dig` ‚Üí three branches). Single-use static composition; the diagnostic *is* the picture.
- `MultipleChoice` for the two-scenario triage confirmation.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below the triage: "DMARC at the apex covers subdomains by inheritance; the inverse doesn't hold ‚Äî publish at `_dmarc.<apex>` to protect both."

**Project link.** This concept *is* lesson 8.3.2's main beat ‚Äî the student leaves the lesson with a `Verified` domain in the Resend dashboard and a `RESEND_API_KEY` ready for `.env.local`, which is the unblocking gate for every later beat in the chapter.

## Concept 4 ‚Äî The starter as a contract: the inspector page is the spec, the action earns its right to exist by satisfying it

**Why it's hard.** The student opens `app/inspector/send-welcome/page.tsx`, sees a Server Component shell that imports `<SendWelcomeForm />`, and reaches for the keyboard to write the action however feels right. The provided page is *not* a sketch ‚Äî it's the contract: the form posts to `sendWelcomeEmail` by exact import path, it expects the action's `Result<{id}>` shape with three branchable failure codes (`'validation'`, `'suppressed'`, `'internal'`), and the result cards are wired against those exact branches. The senior reflex is to read the call site *first* and treat the action's signature, return shape, and import path as the spec the implementation satisfies. The junior reflex is the opposite ‚Äî write the action, then "wire it up" ‚Äî and the wiring breaks because the page already wrote it and was waiting for the action to arrive. The chapter's discipline is the same one 6.6.2 and 7.6.2 install: the page is the contract, the implementation satisfies it.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **call-site trace** through the three provided files in `AnnotatedCode`. The student walks (1) `app/inspector/send-welcome/page.tsx` ‚Äî Server Component renders the form, no props beyond the seeded suppressed-recipient hint; (2) `send-welcome-form.tsx` ‚Äî Client Component, `useActionState(sendWelcomeEmail, null)`, the action import line names the file path the student must match; (3) the result-card switch on `state` ‚Äî the success branch reads `state.data.id` (the Resend send ID), the suppression branch reads `state.error.code === 'suppressed'`, the validation branch reads `state.error.code === 'validation'` plus `state.error.fieldErrors`. Three annotation tracks per file: (a) the exact import path and exported name the student-written file must match; (b) the prop or return shape the page consumes; (c) what would break in the page if the student renamed the action, returned a different shape, or collapsed the failure codes into one. A second short beat lingers on `lib/result.ts`, `lib/auth-stub.ts`, and `emails/components/EmailLayout.tsx` ‚Äî provided code the student reads but doesn't touch, with the boundary line drawn explicitly so the student stops trying to rebuild what the starter already ships.

**Engagement.** A `Dropdowns` exercise on the action's file header ‚Äî given the form's import line `import { sendWelcomeEmail } from '@/app/actions/send-welcome'`, the student fills in the file path, the exported name, and the return type signature for the action. Wrong picks surface the wiring break each one would produce ("the form imports `sendWelcomeEmail`, your file exports `sendWelcome` ‚Äî the form renders `undefined`"). Confirm with a `MultipleChoice`: "a teammate's PR collapses the action's failure codes into a single `error: string` field for simpler card rendering. What does the senior reviewer ask for?" ‚Üí keep the discriminated `code` union; the suppression card's "Suppression path hit ‚Äî Resend was NOT called" banner depends on `code === 'suppressed'` being distinguishable from a generic error; collapsing the union loses the diagnostic surface the inspector exists to provide.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` walking the three call sites (page, form, result-card switch) with the three annotation tracks per file.
- `Dropdowns` for the action's file-header signature exercise.
- `MultipleChoice` for the failure-code-collapse PR-review.
- `Aside` (`note`) below: "the page is the contract; the action earns its right to exist by satisfying it. The result cards exist to surface the failure taxonomy ‚Äî collapsing the codes hides what the inspector is for."

**Project link.** This concept *is* lesson 8.3.2's first half ‚Äî the student leaves knowing exactly what they have to write for the action and the wrapper, before they write a line, with the inspector page's branches as the four-corner constraint.

## Concept 5 ‚Äî Env at boot, fail-closed: the three new entries and the missing-key rehearsal

**Why it's hard.** The student adds `RESEND_API_KEY=re_xxx` to `.env.local`, types `process.env.RESEND_API_KEY!` (or worse, `as string`) inside `lib/email.ts`, and ships an app that boots fine in dev but ships to production with the env variable missing ‚Äî the first transactional send returns a Resend authentication error at *runtime*, not at build time. The senior posture inverts this: every secret the app needs is declared in the `@t3-oss/env-nextjs` schema's `server` block, the schema runs at boot, missing or malformed values fail the build with a Zod error, the production deploy can't ship without them. The discipline is one schema edit and one rehearsal: comment out `RESEND_API_KEY`, restart `pnpm dev`, watch the boot fail, restore. The rehearsal is the load-bearing moment ‚Äî without it, the student adds the entries and never feels the fail-closed property the schema buys.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Mechanics archetype delivered as a **schema-edit walk plus the rehearsal** in `AnnotatedCode`. The student watches the three new entries land in the existing `server` block of `lib/env.ts` with four annotations: (1) `RESEND_API_KEY: z.string().min(1)` ‚Äî the key shape, `min(1)` rejects empty strings (a common deployment-config bug where an env var exists but its value got blanked); (2) `EMAIL_FROM: z.string().min(1)` ‚Äî the full `Display Name <local-part@send.domain.tld>` format; the looser `z.string().min(1)` ships in the chapter, with a side annotation naming the regex tightening as the production reach the team grows into; (3) `EMAIL_REPLY_TO: z.email()` ‚Äî uses Zod 4's top-level format builder; the apex monitored mailbox; (4) `NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_NAME: z.string().min(1)` lands in the *client* block (the only `NEXT_PUBLIC_` entry the chapter adds), so `EmailLayout` can read it. Below the schema walk, a *rehearsal beat* in `Steps`: comment out `RESEND_API_KEY` in `.env.local` ‚Üí run `pnpm dev` ‚Üí see the Zod error message pointing at the missing variable ‚Üí restore ‚Üí boot succeeds. The rehearsal is the assessment because the student has to *watch the boot fail* to internalize that the schema is the boundary.

**Engagement.** The rehearsal carries the assessment ‚Äî comment, watch, restore. Confirm with a `MultipleChoice` round of two: (a) "a teammate writes `process.env.RESEND_API_KEY!` directly inside `lib/email.ts` and skips the schema entry. What's the failure mode?" ‚Üí the missing variable surfaces at the first send attempt in production, not at build time; the `!` non-null assertion lies to TypeScript; the schema is the boundary that fails closed; (b) "a teammate adds `RESEND_API_KEY: z.string().optional()` to make local dev easier without setting up Resend. What's the smell?" ‚Üí the wrapper assumes the key exists at module scope (`new Resend(env.RESEND_API_KEY)`); making it optional pushes the failure to runtime and breaks the fail-closed property; the right answer is to set the key for local dev too, not to weaken the schema.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` walking the four-annotation env-block edit.
- `Steps` for the comment-restart-restore rehearsal.
- `MultipleChoice` for the two-scenario PR-review confirmation.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below: "the schema is the boundary; a missing variable fails the build, not the production send. The `!` non-null assertion lies to TypeScript and ships the leak."

**Project link.** This concept *is* the first beat of lesson 8.3.3 ‚Äî the student edits `lib/env.ts` once and runs the rehearsal, and 8.3.5's "env validation fails closed" Done-when clause confirms the property end-to-end with a second rehearsal at the verify stage.

## Concept 6 ‚Äî `lib/suppressions.ts`: the read helper, normalize-on-read, and the reason-aware bypass

**Why it's hard.** Three structural facts collapse into one helper signature the student stitches wrong on first read. (1) **Normalize on read.** Email addresses arrive at the helper from form input (the recipient the user typed, possibly with capitalization or whitespace) and the table stores them lowercased and trimmed (the 8.1.4 contract). The helper has to apply `email.trim().toLowerCase()` *before* the index lookup, every time, no exceptions ‚Äî the student who skips it ships a suppression hole on the first user who types `Suppressed@Foo.com`. (2) **The single index lookup.** The unique-on-email index from 8.1.4 makes the read O(1); the helper is one query, not a scan. (3) **The reason-aware bypass.** Transactional sends bypass `manual_unsubscribe` (the user can't opt out of password resets), but not `hard_bounce` or `complaint`. The helper's `kind` parameter (`'transactional' | 'marketing'`) drives the bypass branch ‚Äî without the kind-aware logic, the wrapper either over-blocks (transactional sends fail to manually-unsubscribed users who need their password reset) or under-blocks (marketing sends slip past the unsubscribe flag). The trio has to land in one focused walk; the student leaves with a typed signature, a normalize step, a single query, and a small branch that distinguishes the two send kinds.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **decision-walked helper authoring beat** in `AnnotatedCode`. The student watches `isSuppressed` get authored line by line in seven annotations: (1) `import 'server-only'` at the top ‚Äî never bundle into a client component, the table contains PII; (2) the typed signature `isSuppressed(email: string, opts: { kind: 'transactional' | 'marketing' }): Promise<{ suppressed: boolean; reason?: string; bypassUntil?: Date }>` ‚Äî the structured return shape the wrapper unpacks (not a bare boolean ‚Äî the reason and the bypass window matter for logging); (3) `const normalizedEmail = email.trim().toLowerCase()` ‚Äî the normalize-on-read rule, applied unconditionally, the table and the input meet at the same shape; (4) the single Drizzle query ‚Äî `db.query.emailSuppressions.findFirst({ where: eq(emailSuppressions.email, normalizedEmail) })` ‚Äî one index lookup, the unique-on-email index from 8.1.4 makes it O(1); (5) the no-row branch ‚Äî `if (!row) return { suppressed: false }` ‚Äî the happy path, every send not on the list goes through; (6) the bypass-window branch ‚Äî `if (row.bypassUntil && row.bypassUntil > new Date()) return { suppressed: false, bypassUntil: row.bypassUntil }` ‚Äî the explicit carve-out from 8.1.4, time-scoped and auditable; (7) the reason-aware bypass ‚Äî `if (row.reason === 'manual_unsubscribe' && opts.kind === 'transactional') return { suppressed: false, reason: 'manual_unsubscribe' }` ‚Äî the password-reset carve-out, reason-aware, the marketing kind doesn't bypass. The closing line otherwise ‚Äî `return { suppressed: true, reason: row.reason }`. The walk lands the helper as one focused function with three short branches; nothing implicit, nothing magic.

**Engagement.** A `MultipleChoice` round of three traps: (a) "a teammate's PR reads `email_suppressions` without normalizing ‚Äî `findFirst({ where: eq(...email, email) })`. What's the failure mode?" ‚Üí the user typing `Suppressed@Foo.com` writes a row at `suppressed@foo.com` (the table-write path lowercases) but the read finds nothing because the input wasn't normalized; the suppression hole opens on every capitalization variant; (b) "a teammate's PR drops the `kind` parameter and always bypasses `manual_unsubscribe`. What breaks?" ‚Üí marketing sends slip past the unsubscribe flag, the user gets the newsletter they explicitly opted out of, the next complaint poisons the marketing reputation; the kind-aware logic is the reason-aware bypass made structural; (c) "a teammate's PR returns a bare `boolean` instead of the structured object. What's lost?" ‚Üí the wrapper can't log the reason, can't surface the `bypassUntil` window in the inspector's diagnostic card; the structured return is what makes the disposition observable.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` walking the seven-annotation `isSuppressed` authoring beat.
- `MultipleChoice` reused three times for the trap round.
- `Aside` (`tip`) below: "normalize on read, one index lookup, reason-aware bypass. The structured return is what makes the disposition observable in the wrapper's logs and the inspector's diagnostic surface."

**Project link.** This concept *is* the second beat of lesson 8.3.3 ‚Äî the student writes `lib/suppressions.ts` from this walk, tests it directly against the seeded suppressed row via `pnpm tsx`, and 8.3.4's wrapper calls it as the chokepoint's first action.

## Concept 7 ‚Äî `lib/email.ts` as the single send chokepoint: required idempotency, default `from`, no-Resend on suppress

**Why it's hard.** The wrapper is small (~25 lines) but it carries four non-negotiables that are easy to weaken individually and catastrophic together. (1) **`idempotencyKey` is a required parameter** ‚Äî not optional, not defaulted to a random UUID, not computed inside the wrapper. The senior call from 8.1.1 forces the caller to think about replay safety; making it optional lets the next caller skip it "for ad-hoc sends" and the next retry doubles the email. (2) **The `from` defaults to `env.EMAIL_FROM` and never accepts a per-call override** ‚Äî allowing per-call `from` is how multi-tenant sends accidentally land on the wrong subdomain. (3) **The suppression check happens *before* the Resend call, and the suppressed branch returns *without* calling Resend** ‚Äî the temptation is to "log it and send anyway"; the chapter's discipline is to short-circuit, log the disposition, and never make the API call. (4) **The wrapper returns `Result<{id}>`, never throws on expected failures** ‚Äî the action layer reads the `ok` boolean and branches; reaching for try/catch around the wrapper collapses the failure surface and the inspector loses the diagnostic codes. The four facts together *are* the chokepoint; weakening any one of them means the next caller either retries-into-double-send, sends from the wrong domain, sends to the suppressed list, or surfaces a generic 500 instead of a typed code.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **wrapper-anatomy walk** in `AnnotatedCode`, paired with a **call-site contrast** in `CodeVariants`. The walk authors `sendEmail` line by line in eight annotations: (1) `import 'server-only'` at the top, then `const resend = new Resend(env.RESEND_API_KEY)` at module scope (singleton, not per-call); (2) the `SendInput` type ‚Äî `to`, `subject`, `react`, `idempotencyKey` (required), `replyTo?`, `bypassSuppression?` ‚Äî the required idempotency key is the keyboard-level enforcement; (3) `const normalizedTo = to.trim().toLowerCase()` ‚Äî the same normalize rule as the suppressions helper, every send obeys it; (4) `const check = await isSuppressed(normalizedTo, { kind: 'transactional' })` ‚Äî the wrapper hard-codes `kind: 'transactional'` because the chapter ships only the transactional path; (5) the suppression short-circuit ‚Äî `if (check.suppressed && !bypassSuppression) { console.info('[email] suppressed', { to: normalizedTo, reason: check.reason }); return err('suppressed', 'This recipient is on the suppression list.') }` ‚Äî log the disposition, return the typed `Result`, never call Resend; (6) the Resend call ‚Äî `const { data, error } = await resend.emails.send({ from: env.EMAIL_FROM, to: normalizedTo, replyTo: replyTo ?? env.EMAIL_REPLY_TO, subject, react }, { idempotencyKey })` ‚Äî the from is locked to the env, the reply-to defaults to env, the idempotency key passes through to the SDK; (7) the error branch ‚Äî `if (error) { console.error('[email] failed', { to: normalizedTo, error }); return err('internal', 'Email send failed.') }`; (8) the success branch ‚Äî `return ok({ id: data.id })`. Below the walk, a `CodeVariants` two-tab contrast: tab one shows three Server Actions each calling `resend.emails.send` directly (suppression check missing in two, idempotency key missing in one, from-address inconsistent across all three ‚Äî annotated as the wrong shape Architectural Principle #3 names); tab two shows the same three actions calling `sendEmail` (one line each, every policy enforced by the wrapper). The closing rule names the discrimination ‚Äî this is *not* an adapter that lets you swap Resend for SES tomorrow (a swap that won't happen and would tax every send forever); this is a chokepoint where the policies that must hold for every send live in *one* place. Re-states 8.1 Concept 4 at the project's keyboard.

**Engagement.** A `MultipleChoice` PR-review round of four scenarios: (a) "a teammate makes `idempotencyKey` optional with a `crypto.randomUUID()` default. What breaks?" ‚Üí every retry mints a fresh key, dedup never matches, the user gets two welcome emails on a Vercel cold-start retry; the required-parameter shape forces the caller to think about replay safety; (b) "a teammate adds a `from?: string` parameter to the wrapper for 'flexibility'. What's the smell?" ‚Üí per-call `from` overrides are how multi-tenant sends accidentally land on the wrong subdomain; the wrapper owns the sender identity, the call site asks for *who to send to*, not *who to send as*; (c) "a teammate moves the suppression check to the action body 'so the wrapper feels less magic'. What's the failure mode?" ‚Üí one forgotten action call site is a reputation incident; the chokepoint is the chokepoint, double-checking is the smell, trust the boundary; (d) "a teammate wraps the `sendEmail` call in `try { ... } catch (e) { return err('internal', e.message) }`. What's lost?" ‚Üí the wrapper already returns `Result`; the try/catch collapses the typed error codes (`'suppressed'` becomes `'internal'`), the inspector loses the diagnostic surface, the discriminated union is the API.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` for the eight-annotation `sendEmail` wrapper walk.
- `CodeVariants` with two tabs for the call-site contrast (direct Resend calls vs wrapper calls). Re-uses the 8.1 Concept 4 contrast shape ‚Äî confirms the forward-link.
- `MultipleChoice` reused four times for the PR-review round.

## Concept 9 ‚Äî The five-seam action shape, applied to the send path with `'suppressed'` as the new failure code

**Why it's hard.** The student arrives with the five-seam stencil installed (7.2 Concept 4, exercised three times in 7.6 Concept 4 ‚Äî parse, authorize, mutate, revalidate, return) and the welcome action looks like a fourth instance ‚Äî except the seams substitute differently. (1) The "mutate" seam is *the wrapper call*, not a database write. (2) `revalidatePath` is *not used* ‚Äî there's no list to revalidate; the inspector's result card renders from `useActionState`, no cache invalidation needed. (3) The `Result` channel inherits a *new failure code* ‚Äî `'suppressed'` ‚Äî that the wrapper returns and the action passes through unchanged; reaching to re-shape it ("if suppressed, return validation with a custom message") collapses the failure surface. (4) The idempotency key is computed *inside* the action from stable inputs (`welcome:${userId}:${recipientEmail}`), not generated freshly per call ‚Äî this is the 8.1.1 senior call from Concept 7 made operational at the action site. The student who treats the welcome action as "just another five-seam action" misses the four substitutions and either re-shapes the suppression result, generates a fresh UUID for the key, or reaches for `revalidatePath` out of habit.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **stencil-and-substitute walkthrough** in `AnnotatedCode`, lifting the 7.2 Concept 4 / 7.6 Concept 4 five-seam stencil and applying it to `sendWelcomeEmail` with the four substitutions called out per seam. The student sees the action body in five labeled regions with annotations at each: (1) **Parse** ‚Äî `safeParse({ recipientEmail: z.email(), firstName: z.string().min(1).max(80) })` over `Object.fromEntries(formData)`; on failure, return `err('validation', 'Check the highlighted fields.', z.treeifyError(parsed.error).properties)` ‚Äî same shape as 7.6, no substitution; (2) **Authorize (stub)** ‚Äî `const { userId } = await getActiveContext()` ‚Äî same stub call site as 7.6; Unit 9 swaps it; no `authedAction` wrapper yet (Unit 10); (3) **Compute the idempotency key** ‚Äî `const idempotencyKey = \`welcome:${userId}:${parsed.data.recipientEmail.trim().toLowerCase()}\`` ‚Äî *substitution one*: the key derives from stable inputs (the user ID and the normalized recipient), not from a random UUID at the call site; the same retry produces the same key, Resend dedups; (4) **Compute the verify URL** ‚Äî placeholder for this chapter, with a `// TODO Unit 9` comment naming the swap to a signed token; *substitution two*: the URL is computed in the action, never inside the template; (5) **Send** ‚Äî `const result = await sendEmail({ to: parsed.data.recipientEmail, subject: \`Welcome to ${env.NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_NAME}\`, react: <WelcomeEmail firstName={parsed.data.firstName} verifyUrl={verifyUrl} />, idempotencyKey })` ‚Äî *substitution three*: the "mutate" seam is the wrapper call, the JSX is constructed in the action and passed to the wrapper as the `react` prop; (6) **Return** ‚Äî `return result` ‚Äî *substitution four*: pass the wrapper's `Result` through *unchanged*; the action is a thin orchestrator, the wrapper owns the failure taxonomy, including the new `'suppressed'` code that the inspector reads. *No `revalidatePath`* ‚Äî the absence is annotated explicitly: there's no list to revalidate, the inspector's `useActionState` re-renders the result card on its own. A small side panel surfaces the `.tsx` extension watch-out ‚Äî the action file is `.tsx` (not `.ts`) because the JSX in the body needs the JSX-aware tsconfig settings; ESLint configs that flag JSX in `.ts` files complain otherwise.

**Engagement.** A `Sequence` drag-to-order drill: the student is shown seven shuffled lines (`safeParse`, `getActiveContext`, the idempotency-key computation, the verify-URL computation, the `sendEmail` call, the return statement, and a distractor `revalidatePath('/inspector/send-welcome')`) and orders them into the five seams. Wrong placements produce targeted feedback ("`getActiveContext` before `safeParse` runs the auth read on bad input ‚Äî wasted session lookup once Unit 9's session reader replaces the stub"; "`revalidatePath` doesn't belong here ‚Äî there's no list to revalidate, the inspector re-renders from `useActionState`, the call is dead weight that confuses the next reader"; "the idempotency key computed *after* the `sendEmail` call can never be passed to it ‚Äî the order matters"). Confirm with a `MultipleChoice`: "a teammate's PR re-shapes the `'suppressed'` result into `err('validation', 'This email cannot be sent')` so the inspector's error card renders 'cleanly'. What does the senior reviewer ask for?" ‚Üí pass the wrapper's `Result` through unchanged; the inspector's suppression card *exists specifically* to surface the `'suppressed'` code as a distinct diagnostic; collapsing it loses the chapter's verification surface.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` walking the five-seam action body with the four substitutions called out. Reuses 7.2 Concept 4 / 7.6 Concept 4's stencil ‚Äî confirms the forward-link.
- `Sequence` for the seven-line drag-to-order drill with the `revalidatePath` distractor.
- `MultipleChoice` for the result-reshape PR-review.
- `Aside` (`note`) below: "five seams, this order. The substitutions per send-path action: the mutate seam is the wrapper call, no revalidate (no list), the wrapper's `Result` passes through unchanged with the new `'suppressed'` code."

## Concept 10 ‚Äî The verify lesson as failure-rehearsal pass

**Why it's hard.** Verification reads as bureaucracy ‚Äî a checklist the student ticks to ship ‚Äî until the student understands what each Done-when clause is *for*. Each clause corresponds to a discipline that has a failure mode if skipped: real-inbox arrival fails when the verified-domain ceremony was sandbox-shortcut; DKIM-pass fails when the registrar truncated the TXT value; the template renders wrong when the props leaked env reads or the `<Preview>` is missing; the plain-text fallback is missing when the call site hand-maintained a `text` field instead of letting `render({ plainText: true })` do it; the suppression path calls Resend when the wrapper's chokepoint is bypassed at the caller; the idempotency key fails to dedup when the action minted a fresh UUID instead of deriving from stable inputs; env validation passes silently when the schema was weakened to optional. The verify lesson's job is not to *check the boxes* but to *rehearse each failure* ‚Äî to make the student feel what would have shipped if the discipline weren't installed. Without the rehearsal, the verify lesson is a checklist; with it, the student leaves the chapter knowing exactly what a senior transactional surface guards against and which earlier concept owns each guard.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **failure-rehearsal pass** through the seven Done-when clauses, each as a small three-beat scrub in `DiagramSequence` (or, if the chapter's scrub model needs simplification, a `Steps`-shaped vertical list with one expandable row per clause).
