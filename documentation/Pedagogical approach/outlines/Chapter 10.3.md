# Chapter 10.3 — Invitations and the seat-handoff lifecycle: pedagogical approach

## Concept 1 — The invitation row as a seat reservation that outlives the request

**Why it's hard.** The student arrives with the request/response shape as their default frame for "thing happens, thing finishes." An invitation breaks that shape — the admin clicks send at 10am Monday, the row is born, the human accepts (or doesn't) on Thursday afternoon, and the audit obligation lasts forever. Three sub-misconceptions ride this. *Misconception A — bind the invite to a `user.id`:* the invitee may not have an account yet; binding to `userId` is structurally wrong on the most common shape. *Misconception B — delete the row on accept (or on revoke) because "the invite's done":* loses the audit trail; the admin question "did Bob ever get invited, and when did he accept?" has no answer. *Misconception C — write a background job that flips `pending` to `expired`:* invents a cron for a thing the read filter already knows. The senior reading is that the invitation row is a *seat reservation between two humans across time* — its identity is `email` (not `userId`), its status machine is `pending → accepted | canceled | rejected`, expired is *computed at read time* (`expiresAt < now()`), and the row never gets deleted in normal flow (retention is its own batch job, named once, owned by Chapter 17).

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Concept archetype delivered as a **timeline-stretched lifecycle diagram with three failure-frame ambushes**. A hand-SVG inside `Figure` draws a horizontal time axis from "Monday 10:00 — admin clicks send" to "Thursday 16:00 — Bob accepts" (about a 78-hour span), with the `invitation` row visualized as a labeled card that persists across the entire span. The card's status badge changes color across four marked moments — *born `pending`*, *email sent* (a small envelope icon hangs off the timeline), *Bob clicks the link* (verify gate), *Bob clicks Accept* (status transitions to `accepted`, `acceptedAt = now()`, `member` row spawns adjacent). Around the timeline, three small annotated callouts ambush the misconceptions: *(1)* a struck-through "row carries `userId`" callout pointing at the row's identity field, replaced with "row carries `email` — Bob has no account yet"; *(2)* a struck-through "DELETE on accept" callout pointing at the Thursday transition, replaced with "UPDATE `status = 'accepted'`, row stays — audit"; *(3)* a struck-through "expiry cron flips `pending → expired`" callout pointing somewhere along the middle of the timeline, replaced with "no row write; the read filter `expiresAt > now()` is the source of truth." Below the timeline, a small state-machine inset — three solid arrows (`pending → accepted`, `pending → canceled`, `pending → rejected`) and one dashed *computed* arrow (`pending --[time]--> expired`) — installs the four-state vocabulary as a single picture. A closing one-liner under the artifact: *the row is what the org promised; the email is a delivery; the member is the outcome. Three artifacts, three lifetimes, never collapsed.*

**Engagement.** A `Buckets` sort on eight invitation-shape decisions into two columns — *belongs on the `invitation` row* / *belongs elsewhere (and where)*. Items: *the invited email* (invitation, lowercased), *the role the inviter chose* (invitation), *the token's SHA-256 hash* (invitation), *the raw token* (the email's URL only — nowhere else), *the inviter's user id* (invitation, FK), *the invitee's `user.id` once they accept* (the new `member` row, not the invitation), *the org's seat count remaining* (entitlements layer — Chapter 12.2; not on `invitation`), *the inviter's name as it should appear in the email body* (read from `user.name` at send time, not stored on `invitation`). Wrong-answer feedback names the misframe each misplacement enables. Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice`: *"a teammate proposes adding a nightly cron that sets `status = 'expired'` on rows where `expiresAt < now()` 'so the list query is simpler.'"* — the right answer names the cron as redundant against the read filter, names the side-effect (every accepted row's `acceptedAt` semantics blur with `expiresAt`-then-`expired` writes), names the actual cleanup mechanism (a retention job that *deletes* old canceled/expired rows on a 90-day horizon, named once, owned by Chapter 17).

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-SVG horizontal-timeline lifecycle with three struck-through misconception callouts and the four-state-machine inset.
- `Buckets` for the eight-decision sort.
- `MultipleChoice` for the cron-vs-read-filter diagnosis.
- `Aside` (`note`) below the timeline: "the invitation row is a *promise* the org made; it stays around because the audit log references it. Expired is computed; canceled and accepted are written; deletes happen on the retention horizon, not in the request path."

**Project link.** Lesson 10.4.3 of the project declares the `invitation` table with `tokenHash` and `acceptedAt` `additionalFields` and the partial unique index; the timeline artifact is the *why* the project's schema file leads with comments naming `email` as the identity column and explicitly *not* `userId`, and the *why* the project's verification step (10.4.8) confirms expired rows are surfaced by query filter rather than by a stored status.

## Concept 2 — The partial unique index as the structural defense against duplicate pending invites

**Why it's hard.** The student's instinct for "at most one pending invite per (org, email)" is to pre-check with a `SELECT` before the `INSERT`. Two failure modes ride this. *(1) The race window*: between SELECT and INSERT, a second admin (or the same admin double-clicking) slips in and both INSERTs succeed; the table now has two pending rows for the same email; the accept flow becomes nondeterministic and the audit log has a phantom. *(2) The case-sensitivity miss*: a naive `WHERE email = ?` against a raw-cased column lets `Bob@Acme.com` and `bob@acme.com` coexist as separate "pending" invites; both succeed, the user gets two emails, the seat-count discussion gets sticky. The senior reflex is to encode the business rule at the *database layer* — `CREATE UNIQUE INDEX invitation_pending_unique ON invitation(organization_id, lower(email)) WHERE status = 'pending'` — and let the INSERT fail loudly on collision. The action's job becomes catching and translating the constraint error into a structured `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`. The misframe to break is "validate in the action body"; the senior shape is "validate in the schema, translate in the action."

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **wrong-then-right race-condition replay** in `CodeVariants`. *Tab 1 — the broken pre-check shape.* The action body shows the naive sequence — `const existing = await tenantDb(orgId).query.invitation.findFirst({ where: and(eq(invitation.organizationId, orgId), eq(invitation.email, email), eq(invitation.status, 'pending')) }); if (existing) return Result.error('already-invited'); await tenantDb(orgId).insert(invitation).values({ ... })`. A small two-track timeline below the code (rendered as hand-SVG inside `Figure`) shows two admin requests `A` and `B` arriving 80ms apart. Frame 1: A's SELECT returns nothing. Frame 2: B's SELECT returns nothing (A hasn't committed yet). Frame 3: A inserts. Frame 4: B inserts (no constraint, no protection — both succeed). Frame 5: the table has two pending rows for `bob@acme.com`; the email sends twice; the partial-unique-index is missing; the bug is structurally encoded in "trust the application layer." Annotation: *the read-then-write pattern is correct against a unique constraint; without the constraint it's a race condition, full stop.* *Tab 2 — the senior baseline.* The schema declaration shows the partial unique index — `index('invitation_pending_unique').on(invitation.organizationId, sql\`lower(${invitation.email})\`).where(sql\`status = 'pending'\`)` — followed by the action body: no pre-SELECT, INSERT directly, catch the unique-violation error code (`'23505'` from Postgres), translate to `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })` by looking up the existing row in the catch block. The same two-track timeline replays under tab 2: frames 1–3 unchanged (A inserts), frame 4 — B's INSERT fails with `23505`, B's action returns the structured error, the UI surfaces the actionable "resend or revoke" branch. Annotation: *the constraint enforces the rule even when the application forgets; the action's job is translation, not gatekeeping.* Closing rule under the variants: *business rules that must hold across concurrent writers belong in the schema; the action body translates the database's `no` into the user's `try again differently`.*

**Engagement.** A `DrizzleSchemaCoding` exercise: the student declares the `invitation` table with the partial unique index keyed on `(organizationId, lower(email))` filtered by `status = 'pending'`; the grader's SQL probe inserts two pending rows for `bob@acme.com` (one as `bob@acme.com`, one as `BOB@ACME.COM`) and asserts the second one fails with `23505`, then inserts a third row with `status = 'canceled'` (a fourth admin attempt that was previously revoked) and asserts it succeeds (the partial index doesn't constrain non-pending rows). Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice`: *"the team's PR catches the unique-violation error and returns a generic `'something went wrong'` toast. What's the senior reading?"* — the right answer names the wasted constraint signal (the database told the action exactly which row caused the collision; the action should look it up and pass `{ existingInvitationId }` to the UI so the toast surfaces "resend or revoke" with both actions one click away).

**Components.**
- `CodeVariants` for the broken-pre-check vs partial-unique-index pair, with the two-track race-condition replay rendered as a hand-SVG inside `Figure` inside each tab.
- `DrizzleSchemaCoding` for the partial-unique-index declaration with the three SQL probe cases.
- `MultipleChoice` for the wasted-signal diagnosis.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below the variants: "pre-check then insert is a race condition without a constraint. The partial unique index is the structural defense; the action body's job is to translate `23505` into an actionable error, not to prevent the duplicate by hand."

**Project link.** Lesson 10.4.6 of the project ships exactly this pattern — the partial unique index on `(organization_id, lower(email)) WHERE status = 'pending'` lives in the schema, and the `sendInvitationAction` catches the unique-violation error and returns `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`; the wrong-then-right replay is the *frame* the project's verification step uses to confirm that two simultaneous invite submissions resolve to one row plus one structured error, not two rows.

## Concept 3 — Raw in the URL, hashed at rest: the invitation token's two-substrate discipline

**Why it's hard.** The token-hashing discipline is installed once in 9.3 against verify-email and password-reset; invitations are the third instance, and the temptation to skip the re-teaching is exactly where the bug ships. Three sub-misconceptions cluster. *Misconception A — use `crypto.randomUUID()` as the token:* 122 bits is fine for entropy, but the senior shape is 32 bytes from `crypto.getRandomValues` because the URL's namespace mixes the random value with the invitation id, and the wider random is the standard. *Misconception B — store the raw token alongside the hash for "easier debugging":* defeats the threat model in one line; a DB-read attacker now has the raw token and can forge accept URLs. *Misconception C — reach for bcrypt because "hashing is what you did for passwords":* bcrypt's slowness is the password threat model (offline brute-force against a leaked hash); invitations have 7-day windows and high entropy, SHA-256 is the right primitive, bcrypt is cargo. The senior reading is *raw in the URL (the email is a credential delivery; the inbox is the trust boundary), hashed at rest (lookups go by `sha256(tokenRaw)` against an indexed column, the raw value never lands in the database or in logs)* — same primitive as session tokens in 9.1 and the verify-email tokens in 9.3, third time the student has seen the shape.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Concept archetype delivered as a **two-substrate token-lifecycle diagram with a redaction overlay**. A hand-SVG inside `Figure` draws two parallel substrates as horizontal lanes — *the email inbox* (top, labeled "trust boundary: the invitee's mailbox") and *the database* (bottom, labeled "trust boundary: the application's privileged access"). A vertical "token mint" arrow at the left fires both lanes simultaneously. *Top lane:* the raw 32-byte base64url string travels into a small `InviteEmail` envelope sketch, the envelope crosses to "Bob's inbox," and the raw token survives there until Bob clicks. *Bottom lane:* the same mint arrow produces a `sha256(tokenRaw)` value that lands in the `invitation.tokenHash` column; the raw value gets a `delete from memory after send` annotation; the database lane shows only the hash, never the raw. A third small lane below the database — *the application logs* — is overlaid with a red redaction band: the URL's `?token=` and `?sig=` query params are crossed out, with a callout *"server logs are not the same trust boundary as an email inbox; redact at the logger."* At the right edge, an annotated *accept* moment: Bob's click reads `?token=` from the URL, the verify step computes `sha256(clickedToken)`, looks it up against the indexed `tokenHash` column with a *timing-safe compare* (named, not built — 3.7.1 owns), the row is found, the flow proceeds. A closing one-liner under the artifact: *the email is the only place the raw token exists outside server memory; the database is a sealed envelope; the logs are not.*

**Engagement.** A `Tokens` exercise on the canonical token-mint code block — `const raw = crypto.getRandomValues(new Uint8Array(32)); const tokenRaw = Buffer.from(raw).toString('base64url'); const tokenHash = await sha256(tokenRaw); await tenantDb(orgId).insert(invitation).values({ tokenHash, ... }); const url = signedInviteUrl(invitationId, tokenRaw);` — with five clickable load-bearing tokens: `getRandomValues` (the senior primitive over `Math.random` or `randomUUID`), `new Uint8Array(32)` (the 32-byte width), `tokenHash` (the column stores the hash, not the raw), `tokenRaw` (passed only to the URL builder, never to the DB insert), and the *absence* of `console.log(url)` as a clickable-by-shape decoy (the student clicks it expecting praise; feedback names it as a credential leak in dev logs). Each click reveals the bug class the choice closes. Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice`: *"a teammate stores the raw token in a `tokenDebug` column 'so we can replay invites in staging without minting new ones.'"* — the right answer names the threat-model collapse (a DB-read attacker now has every pending invite's raw token; replay capability is exactly what the hash discipline prevents), names the alternative (a dev-only "resurface invite URL" button in the admin UI gated on `NODE_ENV !== 'production'`, which surfaces the URL once at send time and never persists it).

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-SVG two-substrate lifecycle diagram with the email/database/logs lanes and the redaction overlay.
- `Tokens` on the canonical token-mint code block with five clickable load-bearing positions including a decoy.
- `MultipleChoice` for the raw-token-storage diagnosis.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below the diagram: "raw in the URL, hashed in the database. Same primitive as session tokens (9.1) and verify-email tokens (9.3) — third instance, same discipline. The dev-mode URL surface in the admin UI is the only debugging affordance; the database is sealed."

**Project link.** Lesson 10.4.6 of the project ships the 32-byte token mint with `getRandomValues`, the SHA-256 at rest, and the dev-mode URL surface in the admin UI; the two-substrate diagram is the *frame* the project's verification step uses to grep the codebase and confirm zero instances of `console.log(url)` and zero columns other than `tokenHash` reference the token at rest.

## Concept 4 — The HMAC signature as defense-in-depth on top of the random token

**Why it's hard.** This is the chapter's most novel piece — every prior token flow (verify-email, password-reset, session) used a hashed-token-only design with no URL signature. The student's first reaction is "the random token is already 32 bytes; what does the signature add?" The answer is subtle and worth foregrounding: the signature is *not* the primary authentication (the random token's lookup is); the signature is a *fast pre-check* that rejects tampered URLs *before the database round-trip*, and it adds a separate cryptographic dimension — a DB-read attacker who somehow got hold of `tokenHash` *still* can't forge a valid URL because they don't have the HMAC secret. Three sub-misconceptions cluster. *Misconception A — "the signature is the auth":* a hostile actor with the secret could mint URLs without DB write access; the *random token verified by hash lookup* is the authoritative check. *Misconception B — "reuse the auth session secret for the HMAC":* two cryptographic uses on one secret entangle rotation cadences; the senior reflex is one secret per cryptographic purpose. *Misconception C — "compare the signature with `===`":* timing-leak in the request path; constant-time compare via `crypto.subtle.verify` is the discipline (3.7.1 owns the why). The senior reading is *defense-in-depth on two independent dimensions* — random-token-hash-lookup (which protects against URL tampering by entropy) and HMAC-signature-verify (which protects against DB-read attackers and short-circuits tampered URLs before the DB load).

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Decision + Pattern composite delivered in two beats. *Beat one — the URL anatomy, annotated:* an `AnnotatedCode` block on the canonical signed URL — `${BASE_URL}/accept-invite?id=${invitationId}&token=${tokenRaw}&sig=${hmacBase64Url}` — with three highlighted spans. *Span 1 — `id`*: the invitation row's primary key; the lookup key alongside the hash; included because the verify needs to know which row's hash to check against. *Span 2 — `token`*: the 32-byte base64url random; the *authenticator*; the value the verify hashes and looks up. *Span 3 — `sig`*: HMAC-SHA-256 of `${id}.${tokenRaw}` keyed on `INVITATION_SIGNING_SECRET`; the *fast pre-check*; rejected before any DB call. Annotation under each span calls out what each piece carries: id = pointer, token = secret, sig = tamper-evidence. *Beat two — the layered-defense ledger.* A hand-coded HTML table inside `Figure` with two columns — *random-token-hash-lookup (Layer 1)* / *HMAC signature (Layer 2)* — and four rows. *What attack it stops*: Layer 1 stops URL-guessing by entropy (32 bytes is computationally infeasible); Layer 2 stops URL-tampering by signature verification (any byte changed without the secret invalidates the sig). *What happens if the other layer fails*: if the HMAC is removed or its secret leaks, Layer 1 still gates the accept (the attacker needs the raw token to pass hash lookup); if the random-token discipline is removed (or `tokenHash` is leaked from the DB), Layer 2 still gates because forging a sig requires the secret. *Where the layer lives*: Layer 1 lives in the database schema and the lookup-by-hash call; Layer 2 lives in the URL builder and the verify step before the DB call. *Operational cost*: Layer 1 is a column and an index; Layer 2 is one env var, one `signedInviteUrl` helper, one verify call. A small closing one-liner under the ledger: *the random token is the lock; the HMAC is the deadbolt. One alone protects against most attacks; both together fail only when both are broken — which is the defense-in-depth contract.*

**Engagement.** A `MultipleChoice` round of three scenarios. *"A teammate proposes dropping the HMAC signature: 'we already have 32 bytes of entropy in the token; the signature is redundant.'"* — the right answer names the layer-by-purpose distinction (the HMAC is the fast pre-check that rejects before DB load, *and* the second cryptographic dimension that closes the DB-read attacker scenario), names the cost-benefit (one env var, one helper, one verify call — cheap), names the threat model (defense-in-depth's value is *both layers must fail* for the attack to succeed). *"A teammate reuses `BETTER_AUTH_SECRET` for the HMAC: 'we already have a secret in env; rotate them together.'"* — the right answer names the secret-reuse coupling (rotating session-auth invalidates every pending invite link; debugging a sig mismatch requires reasoning about two cryptographic uses on one secret), names the senior alternative (a separate `INVITATION_SIGNING_SECRET` env var with its own rotation cadence and blast radius). *"The verify step uses `if (computedSig === providedSig)` — what's wrong?"* — the right answer names the timing-leak (string comparison short-circuits on first mismatch, leaking the position of the first wrong byte), names the fix (`crypto.subtle.verify('HMAC', key, sigBytes, data)` returns a boolean in constant time), points back to 3.7.1 as the owner of the timing-safe-compare discipline. Confirmed by a live-coding `ScriptCoding` exercise: implement `signedInviteUrl(invitationId, tokenRaw)` and its `verifyInviteSig(invitationId, tokenRaw, sig)` twin against a small fixture; the grader runs four cases — valid sig passes, byte-flipped sig fails, byte-flipped token fails, sig minted with wrong secret fails — and asserts the verify uses `crypto.subtle.verify` (not `===`) by checking for the call shape in the source.

**Components.**
- `AnnotatedCode` for the URL anatomy with three highlighted spans (`id`, `token`, `sig`).
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-coded HTML two-column layered-defense ledger across four rows.
- `MultipleChoice` for the three-scenario round (drop-the-sig / reuse-the-secret / `===`-compare).
- `ScriptCoding` for the `signedInviteUrl` + `verifyInviteSig` live-coding exercise with four fixture cases.
- `Aside` (`note`) below the ledger: "the random token is the lock, the HMAC is the deadbolt. One secret per cryptographic purpose. Always `crypto.subtle.verify` — never `===` — for signature compare (3.7.1)."

**Project link.** Lesson 10.4.6 of the project ships `signedInviteUrl` and its verify twin keyed on `INVITATION_SIGNING_SECRET`; the layered-defense ledger is the *frame* the project's verification step uses to confirm — via a tampered-URL probe — that the verify rejects before the DB call, and a corresponding cross-secret probe (using a wrong secret to mint a sig) returns the same generic refusal.

## Concept 5 — The eight-step send action and the DB-commit-then-email transaction boundary

**Why it's hard.** The `sendInvitationAction` is the chapter's first real composition of every primitive the unit has shipped so far — `authedAction('admin', ...)`, `tenantDb(orgId)`, `withTenant` (if the audit insert needs the RLS scope), `logAudit(tx, event)`, the Resend `sendEmail` wrapper — plus the new ones (token mint, signed URL). The student arrives able to write each primitive but not yet able to *order* them, and the ordering carries one real load-bearing decision: *the email send happens after the DB transaction commits, not inside it*. The misframe to break is "wrap everything in one big transaction for atomicity." Wrapping the email send inside the transaction ships a worse failure mode than the senior alternative — *a rollback after the email is queued leaves an orphan email referencing a row that no longer exists*; the invitee clicks a link, the lookup returns nothing, the generic refusal lands, the user is confused. The senior order is *(1) inputs validated, (2) entitlement check, (3) collision check (database, not application — Concept 2), (4) token mint, (5) row write + audit-log write in one transaction, (6) commit, (7) email send (resend on failure), (8) revalidate + return*. The transaction's job is to make "row + audit" atomic; the email's job is to deliver the URL, and the row is the source of truth that survives a send failure.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as an **eight-step sequence diagram with a wrong-then-right transaction-boundary fork**. A hand-SVG inside `Figure` draws the canonical action body as eight numbered nodes on a vertical track, with arrows between them and a green "transaction boundary" bracket grouping steps 5 and 6 only. Each node carries a one-line label and the primitive it reaches for: *(1) `authedAction('admin', schema, fn)` parses inputs and enforces role*; *(2) `entitlements.canInviteMember(orgId)` — named, deferred to 12.2 (the placeholder line is in the diagram)*; *(3) INSERT runs into the partial unique index on collision → catch `23505` → `Result.error('already-invited')`*; *(4) `tokenRaw = base64url(getRandomValues(32))`, `tokenHash = sha256(tokenRaw)`*; *(5) inside `db.transaction(async (tx) => { ... })`: insert row with `tokenHash`, expiresAt, role, inviterId; log `'invitation.sent'` audit row*; *(6) commit*; *(7) `await sendEmail({ to, subject, react: <InviteEmail acceptUrl={signedInviteUrl(invitationId, tokenRaw)} ... /> })` — outside the transaction*; *(8) `revalidatePath('/settings/members')`, return `Result.ok({ invitationId })`*. To the right of the eight-node track, a *fork annotation* shows two alternative transaction-boundary brackets in red and green. *Red bracket — broken, the "everything atomic" reflex:* brackets steps 5, 6, 7 — the email is sent inside the transaction. Annotated below the red bracket: *if the transaction rolls back (DB error during audit-log insert, network blip on commit), the email has already been queued in Resend's API; the row no longer exists; the invitee clicks an orphan link.* *Green bracket — senior baseline:* brackets steps 5 and 6 only — the email is sent after commit. Annotated below the green bracket: *if Resend is down at step 7, the row exists with `'invitation.sent'` audit; the action returns `Result.error('email-send-failed', { invitationId })`; the admin sees a "resend email" button in the pending-invites list (10.3.4); the row is the source of truth, the email is a delivery, the resend closes the gap.* A small closing one-liner under the diagram: *atomicity covers what must agree (row + audit); delivery is its own dimension; the resend affordance closes the rare send-failure case without compromising atomicity in the common case.*

**Engagement.** A `Sequence` ordering exercise on the eight steps, scrambled, with two distractors: *"set `app.org_id` via `SET LOCAL` for RLS"* (only needed if the audit insert touches an RLS-protected table — depends on the project; named conditional) and *"call `revalidatePath('/settings/members')` before the email send"* (it can land before or after; the senior call is after the email send returns, because a send failure changes what the list should show). Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice` round of two scenarios. *"The CI starts failing intermittently: 'orphan invitations referenced by stale emails.' The action wraps the email send inside the transaction. What's the senior fix?"* — the right answer names the transaction-boundary move (email after commit), names the resend affordance as the rare-case recovery, *not* "retry the transaction" or "add idempotency keys to Resend." *"A teammate adds a try/catch around the email send that retries three times on network failure, all still inside the transaction."* — the right answer names this as wrapping the wrong direction (retries don't fix the orphan-on-rollback failure; they extend the transaction's hold on the connection, blocking other writers); names the senior shape (the structured `Result.error('email-send-failed', ...)` plus the visible resend button is the recovery surface).

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-SVG eight-node action-body sequence diagram with the broken/senior transaction-boundary fork annotation.
- `Sequence` for the eight-step ordering exercise with two conditional distractors.
- `MultipleChoice` for the orphan-on-rollback and retry-inside-transaction diagnoses.
- `Aside` (`caution`) below the diagram: "atomicity covers the row and the audit; the email is delivery. Send after commit; the row is the source of truth; the resend button is the recovery affordance."

## Concept 6 — The verify order and the unified generic refusal

**Why it's hard.** The accept route has five gates the request must pass before any state change is allowed — *(1) HMAC signature valid*, *(2) row exists by `id`*, *(3) `tokenHash` matches `sha256(tokenRaw)` under timing-safe compare*, *(4) `expiresAt > now()`*, *(5) `status === 'pending'`*. Two things ride this. *(a) The order matters for cost and for information leakage*: signature verification is cheap and rejects without a DB call; the DB lookup is the next gate; the timing-safe hash compare is third; the time and status checks are read-side filters on the loaded row. Reversing the order (looking up the row first, then verifying the sig) leaks a one-bit "this id exists" signal to a tampering attacker. *(b) The refusal surface is uniform across the first three failures* — bad sig, missing row, hash mismatch all map to the same user-facing screen ("this invitation link isn't valid or has expired"); differentiating publicly between "tampered" and "missing" leaks information without adding user value, and the recovery action is the same regardless (ask the inviter for a fresh invite). Expired and revoked get their own copy because the recovery diverges — expired says "ask for a new one," revoked is friendlier ("contact the org admin"), accepted short-circuits to the org's dashboard. The student needs the order as a *rule with a reason* (cost + leakage), not as ceremony, and the refusal as a *deliberate uniformity* (not a copy-laziness).

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Decision archetype delivered as a **five-gate verify pipeline diagram with paired branching outcomes**. A hand-SVG inside `Figure` draws the verify pipeline as a vertical chain of five diamond nodes, each labeled with the check and a cost annotation. *Diamond 1 — `verifyInviteSig`* (cost: HMAC compute, no DB): pass → continue; fail → generic refusal. *Diamond 2 — `tenantDb.invitation.findFirst({ where: eq(id, invitationId) })`* (cost: indexed SELECT): hit → continue; miss → generic refusal. *Diamond 3 — `timingSafeEqual(sha256(tokenRaw), row.tokenHash)`* (cost: hash + constant-time compare, no DB): match → continue; mismatch → generic refusal. *Diamond 4 — `row.expiresAt > now()`* (no extra cost): not expired → continue; expired → *"this invitation expired"* with a "ask for a new one" hint. *Diamond 5 — `row.status === 'pending'`*: pending → render Accept screen; `'accepted'` → *"you're already a member of Acme"* with a dashboard link (idempotent friendly response); `'canceled'` → *"this invitation was revoked"* with a hint to contact the admin; `'rejected'` → also generic refusal (rare). To the right of the pipeline, a small *information-leakage* annotation calls out the order's payoff: *reversing diamonds 1 and 2 leaks a "this id exists" signal to a tampering attacker; the order is cost-then-cryptographic-strength, not arbitrary.* Below the pipeline, a small *outcome ledger* shows the four refusal screens side by side — generic-refusal, expired, revoked, already-accepted — with one-line copy and the recovery action each lands. A closing one-liner under the ledger: *uniform refusal where the recovery is the same; differentiated copy where the recovery diverges. The order is cost-then-secret; the surface is recovery-aligned.*

What's the senior reading?"* — the right answer names the cost argument (the DB hit is wasted on every tampered URL — possibly an abuse vector for `/accept-invite?id=...&sig=garbage` requests at scale) *and* the leakage argument (the response time differential — fast for missing id, slower for found id — reveals which invitation ids exist). *"A teammate proposes differentiating the refusal copy: 'tampered link,' 'expired,' 'revoked,' 'never existed' — 'so users know what to do.'"* — the right answer names the leakage-without-value tradeoff (the recovery for tampered, missing, and hash-mismatch is identical — ask for a fresh invite; surfacing the difference helps no one and gives an enumeration attacker information), names the senior shape (uniform refusal where recovery is the same; differentiated copy where recovery diverges — expired and revoked qualify; tampered and missing do not).

## Concept 7 — Four arrival shapes on one accept URL

**Why it's hard.** This is the chapter's load-bearing senior-mindset concept. Most students have only ever built invite flows with one assumed user state — usually "signed in already, same email." Real production traffic is four shapes, and *the page is one route that branches on session + invitation row*, not four routes. *(A) Signed in, session email matches `invitation.email`*: render Accept button. *(B) Signed in, session email differs*: render the email-mismatch refusal (strict — Concept 10 owns the *why*). *(C) Signed out, account exists on `invitation.email`*: render a sign-in form prefilled with the invited email, return to the same accept URL on success. *(D) Signed out, no account exists*: render a sign-up form prefilled with the email; after sign-up, re-render now in shape A and present the Accept button. Two sub-misconceptions are common. *Misconception 1 — auto-accept on the GET:* clicking the link in an email-scanning crawler or a corporate URL-rewriter would silently consume the invite; the Accept button is the *consent gate* between "URL fetched" and "member row written," and corporate proxies (Outlook Safe Links, ATP scanners, link-prefetchers) make this the most-real failure mode in B2B. *Misconception 2 — the action trusts the page's verification:* the page is one request, the action is a separate request; the action must re-verify the sig, the row, the hash, the expiry, the status, and the session email-match — *all* the gates from Concept 6 — because nothing about the previous render is authoritative at action time.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Concept + Pattern composite delivered in two beats. *Beat one — the four-state branch matrix.* A `TabbedContent` with four tabs (A / B / C / D), each tab containing three small panels stacked: *(i)* the session shape entering the page (signed-in vs signed-out, email matches vs differs, account exists vs not), *(ii)* a hand-SVG inside `Figure` of the page render — wireframe-style: header "You're invited to Acme as `member`," a small body section (the Accept button for A, the mismatch refusal for B, the prefilled sign-in form for C, the prefilled sign-up form for D), *(iii)* the post-action flow — what the action does, what the redirect is. Each tab carries a small ledger underneath naming the senior reflex specific to that shape: A → "the Accept button is the consent gate, the action re-verifies"; B → "strict refusal — no 'accept anyway' affordance, ever (Concept 10)"; C → "the sign-in form returns to the same accept URL; on success the page re-renders now in shape A"; D → "the sign-up form returns to the same accept URL; after sign-up the page re-renders now in shape A; auto-`emailVerified` on the new user (Concept 8) prevents the verify-loop." *Beat two — the consent-gate wrong-then-right.* A `CodeVariants` with two tabs on the accept-page server-component body. *Tab 1 (broken — auto-accept on GET):* the page calls `acceptInvitationAction({ invitationId, tokenRaw })` inside the server component's render, before returning JSX; the side effect of corporate URL scanners or email prefetchers fetching the link is the row gets accepted before the human ever sees the page. Annotated below: *Outlook Safe Links, Microsoft ATP, Gmail's image proxy, and corporate Mimecast all fetch the URL once; auto-accept on GET makes every invite a single-use scanner-triggered no-op for ~30% of corporate B2B inboxes.* *Tab 2 (senior baseline — explicit consent button):* the page renders the Accept button as a form whose `action` is the Server Action; the action runs only on submit (the human's deliberate click); the action re-verifies the entire gate chain from Concept 6 plus the session email-match before writing. Annotated below: *the button is the consent gate; the form submission is a separate request; the action's re-verification is mandatory because nothing about the GET's prior verification is authoritative at action time.*

**Engagement.** The `TabbedContent` four-shape exploration is *discovery*, not assessment. The assessment lands as a `Matching` exercise — six "user state" cards on the left (e.g., *"signed in as bob@personal.com, invite to bob@acme.com"*, *"signed in as bob@acme.com, invite to bob@acme.com"*, *"signed out, has account on bob@acme.com"*, *"signed out, no account anywhere"*, *"signed in as bob@acme.com, invite to bob@acme.com, this is Bob's second click"*, *"signed out, has account on bob@acme.com, but session-cookie was wiped mid-flow"*) matched to four "page render" cards on the right (Accept button / Mismatch refusal / Prefilled sign-in / Prefilled sign-up). The trap cases (the fifth and sixth) test whether the student recognizes that the second-click case is *still* shape A (because the page is idempotent and routes by the same matrix on every render — and shape A's action layer handles the "already accepted" outcome by short-circuiting to dashboard), and the wiped-session case is *still* shape C (the cookie's absence is what the matrix reads, not what the user remembers about being signed in earlier). Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice` round of two scenarios. *"After deploying invites, support starts getting tickets from B2B users saying 'the link doesn't work — when I click it, the page says you're already a member.' What's happening?"* — the right answer names the corporate-URL-scanner pre-fetch (the scanner's GET against an auto-accept-on-GET implementation consumed the invite; the human's click hits an already-accepted row), names the structural fix (the explicit Accept button consent gate). *"A teammate proposes 'we'll skip the Accept button for shape D, since the user just signed up — they obviously want to accept.'"* — the right answer names the consent argument (the human should see "you're about to join Acme" once explicitly, even after sign-up; the click is the moment of recorded intent and is what the audit log captures correctly), names the year-1 default (one extra click is the right tradeoff for clarity).

## Concept 8 — Post-accept session reflexes: active-org switch and auto-`emailVerified`

**Why it's hard.** Two state-modifying side effects ride the accept transaction and the student tends to miss both, because each one is the kind of "obvious in hindsight" detail that only shows up as a failure mode in support tickets. *(1) The active-org switch.* Bob accepts an Acme invite; if the action doesn't call `setActiveOrganization(invitation.organizationId)`, the post-redirect lands Bob in *Personal* (or whatever his prior active org was), the dashboard shows no Acme data, Bob assumes the accept didn't work, the support ticket follows. The senior reflex is "the click signals intent — switch the active org as part of accept; the post-redirect to `/dashboard` resolves in Acme context." *(2) The auto-`emailVerified` for invite-sourced sign-ups.* Branch D (signed-out, no account): Bob signs up, the user row is born with `emailVerified: false` by default; the next request hits `requireEmailVerification` middleware and redirects Bob to the verify-email flow — but Bob just *clicked an emailed invite link to reach this page*, which is itself an email-ownership proof. The verify-loop is the failure mode (Bob clicks the verify-email link, lands back in the app, tries to access the org, gets redirected to verify-email again because the cookie didn't refresh, infinite loop in the worst variant). The senior reflex is "the invite click *is* the verification — set `emailVerified: true` on the user row inside the accept action's transaction when the sign-up path runs through invite." Both reflexes share a structural property: they're *transaction-internal* side effects (`setActiveOrganization` inside the same transaction as the member-row insert and the audit-log write; `user.emailVerified = true` inside the same transaction). Treating them as post-action UI concerns is the misframe; they belong in the atomic write.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Pattern archetype delivered as a **two-track post-accept state-change diagram with paired failure-mode replay**. A hand-SVG inside `Figure` draws the accept transaction as a single boxed unit with five writes annotated inside — *(i)* `member` row insert with `role = invitation.role`; *(ii)* `invitation` row update `status='accepted'`, `acceptedAt = now()`; *(iii)* `session.activeOrganizationId = invitation.organizationId`; *(iv)* `user.emailVerified = true` (conditional, only when the sign-up path lit the accept); *(v)* `audit_logs` row `'invitation.accepted'` with `actorUserId = invitee`, `payload = { newMemberId, role }`. Two failure-mode callouts hang off the diagram. *Callout 1 — missing the active-org switch:* a small replay sketch shows Bob's post-accept redirect landing in Personal's context, Bob's dashboard showing no Acme data, Bob's confused face icon, the support ticket overlay. Annotated: *the redirect goes to `/dashboard`, which resolves in `session.activeOrganizationId`; without the switch, Bob's still in Personal; the click's intent is unhonored; "I joined but I see nothing" is the support ticket text.* *Callout 2 — missing the auto-`emailVerified`:* a small replay sketch shows Bob's post-sign-up request hitting the `requireEmailVerification` middleware, redirecting to `/verify-email`, Bob clicking the verify-email link in his mailbox, returning to the app, the cookie-cache hasn't refreshed, the middleware sees `emailVerified: false` still, redirects again, the loop visualized as an actual arrow loop. Annotated: *the invite click is an email-ownership proof; the verify-loop happens because the default sign-up path doesn't know "this user arrived via invite"; the structural fix is to flip `emailVerified` inside the accept transaction when the sign-up branch lit the accept.* A small closing one-liner under the diagram: *the accept transaction owns five writes; missing the third or the fourth ships a real support ticket each. Both are atomic with the member-row insert because they belong to the same intent moment.*

**Engagement.** A `MultipleChoice` round of three scenarios. *"After shipping invites, support gets recurring tickets: 'I accepted but my dashboard still shows my personal account.' The team's first thought is to add a banner reading 'You may need to switch to your new org.' What's the senior reading?"* — the right answer names the missing `setActiveOrganization` call (the click signaled intent; the action should honor it transactionally), names the banner as papering over a structural gap (the banner doesn't fix the bug; the transaction's third write does). *"A user signs up via the invite-flow Branch D. Their first request after accept hits a verify-email redirect. They click the verify-email link, land back in the app, hit the same redirect. What's the structural fix?"* — the right answer names the auto-`emailVerified` on invite-sourced sign-up (the click on the invite *is* the email-ownership proof; setting `emailVerified: true` inside the accept transaction is the fix), names the alternative (force the standard verify flow anyway) and the senior reasoning to reject it (the user just confirmed the address *via* the invite click; making them re-confirm is a friction tax with no security benefit). *"A teammate moves `setActiveOrganization` to run *after* the action returns, in a client-side `useEffect` 'because it's a session concern, not a DB write.'"* — the right answer names this as breaking atomicity (a navigation away from the page before the effect runs leaves the session in the old org while the member row exists; the redirect-to-dashboard fires before the active-org write commits — refresh-the-page UX bug), names the senior shape (it's a transaction-internal side effect because it's part of the accept intent; client-side post-hoc is the wrong layer). Confirmed by a `Tokens` exercise on the canonical accept-action body — `await db.transaction(async (tx) => { await tx.insert(member).values({ userId, organizationId, role }); await tx.update(invitation).set({ status: 'accepted', acceptedAt: new Date() }).where(eq(invitation.id, invitationId)); await auth.api.setActiveOrganization({ headers, organizationId: invitation.organizationId }); if (signedUpViaInvite) await tx.update(user).set({ emailVerified: true }).where(eq(user.id, userId)); await logAudit(tx, { action: 'invitation.accepted', ... }); })` — with five clickable load-bearing positions (the `db.transaction` wrapper, the `member` insert, the `setActiveOrganization` call, the conditional `emailVerified` update, the `logAudit` call inside `tx`) each revealing the bug class missing that position would ship.

## Concept 9 — Resend rotates the token; revoke writes `canceled`; the management surface as a tombstone

**Why it's hard.** Three management actions cluster on pending invites — *resend*, *revoke*, *re-invite collision* — and each has a senior decision that's not obvious from the action name. *(1) Resend has two viable shapes*: (a) re-fire the same email with the same URL (cheap, same expiry, same token), or (b) rotate the token, push `expiresAt` to `now() + 7d`, send fresh. The senior default is (b) — rotation is the safer posture because the original token may have been leaked through email forwarding, link previewers, or corporate scan-and-archive, and rotation invalidates the prior URL; the new expiry gives the invitee a fresh window. (a) is sometimes correct (very fresh send, known-clean inbox, deliberate "exactly the same email" intent) and is named once. *(2) Revoke is `status = 'canceled'`, not DELETE*. The row stays because the audit log references it; an admin asking "did we ever invite this address?" needs the row visible. Revoking by DELETE loses the trail and shifts the audit answer from "yes on date X, revoked on date Y" to "we have no record." *(3) Re-invite collision surfaces as a structured error, not a generic toast*. The partial unique index (Concept 2) raises the constraint violation; the action catches it, looks up the existing pending row, returns `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`; the UI surfaces "Bob already has a pending invite — resend or revoke?" with both actions one click away. The student's tendency is to treat the three actions as parallel CRUD operations on the same row; the senior shape treats the row as a *tombstone for "what we promised"* — never deleted in normal flow, audited at every transition, and managed through a small surface that respects each action's distinct semantics.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Mechanics archetype delivered as a **three-action ledger paired with a token-rotation timeline scrub**. *Beat one — the management ledger.* A hand-coded HTML table inside `Figure` with three rows (Resend / Revoke / Re-invite collision) and four columns. *Action body*: Resend → rotate token + push expiry + send email; Revoke → `status = 'canceled'`; Re-invite collision → caught by the partial unique index, translated to `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`. *Audit event*: `'invitation.resent'` with payload `{ email, role, oldExpiresAt, newExpiresAt }`; `'invitation.revoked'` with payload `{ email, role }`; no new audit row for the collision (the row already exists; the surface is informational). *User-visible side effect*: new email lands in the invitee's mailbox; no email to the invitee (privacy + abuse vector); toast surfaces "Bob already has a pending invite — resend or revoke?" with both buttons. *Senior decision*: rotation-by-default (alternative: same-token resend, named once); cancel-not-delete (audit trail); catch-and-translate (alternative: pre-SELECT, named as the broken shape from Concept 2). *Beat two — the rotation timeline scrub.* A `DiagramSequence` with four frames showing what changes inside the `invitation` row on a resend, against the same admin-vs-invitee timeline used in Concept 1. *Frame 1 — original send (Monday)*: row has `tokenHash = sha256(T1)`, `expiresAt = Monday + 7d`. *Frame 2 — admin clicks resend (Wednesday)*: action mints `T2`, computes `sha256(T2)`, UPDATEs `tokenHash = sha256(T2)`, `expiresAt = Wednesday + 7d`. *Frame 3 — the invitee's *original* email (with T1's URL) is clicked (Thursday)*: the verify pipeline's hash compare fails (`sha256(T1) !== sha256(T2)`), the generic refusal lands. *Frame 4 — the invitee's *new* email (with T2's URL) is clicked (Thursday)*: the hash compare passes, the accept flow proceeds. A small annotation under the scrub: *rotation invalidates the prior URL by construction; the security posture is "the most recent send is the only one that works"; the alternative same-token resend would leave both emails clickable, which is rarely desired.* Closing one-liner: *the row is a tombstone; resend rotates, revoke seals, collision speaks. Three actions, three audit events, one source of truth.*

**Engagement.** A `Buckets` sort on eight management decisions into two columns — *senior default (year-1 SaaS)* / *alternative (sometimes correct, name once)*. Items: *resend rotates the token + pushes expiry* (default); *resend re-fires the same email* (alternative — known-clean inbox, very fresh send); *revoke sets `status = 'canceled'`* (default); *revoke DELETEs the row* (alternative — never correct for invites; flagged as the structural wrong); *revoke emails the invitee "your invite was canceled"* (alternative — privacy / abuse vector; the click reveals it organically; default is no email); *collision returns `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`* (default); *collision returns a generic "something went wrong"* (alternative — wastes the constraint signal; flagged as the structural wrong); *role change on a pending invite via `updateRole` action* (alternative — the senior default is revoke + re-send because the email already promised the original role; named once). Wrong-answer feedback names which senior reading the misplacement misses. Confirmed by a live-coding `ReactCoding` exercise: the student builds the pending-invites list's row component with the action menu (`Resend`, `Revoke`) and the collision-toast surface; the grader's tests assert (a) clicking Resend calls `resendInvitationAction({ invitationId })` and renders an updated expiry countdown after success, (b) clicking Revoke calls `revokeInvitationAction({ invitationId })` and removes the row from the active list, (c) on `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })` from a fresh invite submission, the surface shows the actionable toast with both Resend and Revoke buttons referencing the existing row's id.

## Concept 10 — Edge-case senior calls: orphan inviter, email mismatch, the double-click race

**Why it's hard.** This is the chapter's pure decision lesson, and the student arrives wanting code patterns; the actual teaching is *where the check lives, what the senior call is, and why* across four real production scenarios that show up in support tickets in month two. *(1) Orphan invite (inviter removed before accept).* Alice invites Bob day 1; Alice is removed day 5; Bob clicks day 6. The senior call: *honor the invite*. The invite represents *the org's decision*, not Alice's personal sponsorship; Alice's removal doesn't retract Acme's offer. The alternative (auto-revoke pending invites on inviter removal) is defensible but is *not* the year-1 default; pick one, document, name the other once. *(2) Email mismatch (session email differs from invited email).* Alice invited `bob@acme.com`; Bob's session is `bob.personal@gmail.com`. The senior call: *strict refusal — no "accept anyway" affordance, ever*. The reasoning is privilege-confusion through forwarded emails (the wrong human accepts because they happened to receive the forward); the user-visible message names the invited address and tells the user to sign in as that account or ask for a re-invite to the current address. *(3) The double-click race.* Bob clicks Accept in two tabs simultaneously. Both action invocations enter the transaction; the `UPDATE invitation SET status='accepted' WHERE id=? AND status='pending'` filter is the optimistic-concurrency primitive — one update changes the status; the other reports zero rows affected; the second action surfaces the idempotent "you're already a member" branch. The misframe to break is "add a lock" or "check before update"; the `WHERE status='pending'` precondition is the lock. *(4) Already-a-member case.* Alice invites `bob@acme.com` who's already a member. The senior call: cheap-check in the action body before the row insert — if `member` exists for `(orgId, lower(email))`, return `Result.error('already-member', { memberId })`; the UI surfaces "Bob is already a member as `member`" with a link to the row. The check lives in the action because the database doesn't have a structural constraint (the partial unique index on pending invites doesn't cross-reference `member`); the application is the right layer.

**Ideal teaching artifact.** Decision archetype delivered as a **four-row edge-case table paired with a focused double-click race sequence diagram**. *Beat one — the decision table.* A hand-coded HTML table inside `Figure` with four rows (Orphan inviter / Email mismatch / Double-click race / Already a member) and four columns. *Scenario one-liner*: "inviter removed before invitee accepts"; "session email differs from invited email"; "two tabs accept the same invite simultaneously"; "invitee is already a member of the org." *Senior call*: honor the invite (the org's decision survives the inviter's departure); strict refusal (privilege-confusion vector); the WHERE-on-status filter is the lock — one transaction wins, the other gets the idempotent "already a member" branch; reject in the action body with `Result.error('already-member', { memberId })`. *Where the check lives*: in the accept-action's verification (the inviter's existence is read for UI display but the accept itself doesn't refuse); in the accept-action's session-email comparison (lower-cased on both sides — Concept 1 from 10.3.1 owns the casing); in the UPDATE's `WHERE` clause as an optimistic-concurrency primitive; in the send-action's pre-INSERT check against the `member` table. *Alternative named once*: auto-revoke pending invites on inviter removal (Year-2 product option); offer "accept with current email" via a confirmation dialog (rejected; never offer); use SELECT-FOR-UPDATE on the invitation row (rejected; the WHERE-on-status filter is sufficient and more concurrent); allow the send to proceed and let the partial unique on `member` catch it (no such constraint exists; the application layer is the right layer for this rule). *Beat two — the double-click race diagram.* A focused hand-SVG inside `Figure` shows the two-tab race over four frames. *Frame 1*: Tab A and Tab B both render the Accept screen with the same invitationId and tokenRaw. *Frame 2*: Both clicks fire `acceptInvitationAction` at near-identical timestamps. *Frame 3*: Both actions enter their transactions; both issue the same UPDATE — `UPDATE invitation SET status='accepted', acceptedAt=now() WHERE id=? AND status='pending'`. *Frame 4*: Postgres serializes the two writes; the first wins (1 row affected), the second sees zero rows affected; the second action checks rows-affected, finds zero, re-reads the row (now `'accepted'`), and surfaces the idempotent "you're already a member" branch — without ever attempting the `member` insert. Annotation under the diagram: *the WHERE clause is the lock; the rows-affected check is the read; the idempotent branch is the recovery. Never SELECT-then-UPDATE; never lock-and-wait; the precondition is the primitive.*

**Engagement.** A `Buckets` sort with three columns instead of two — *honor (senior call)* / *refuse (senior call)* / *idempotent short-circuit (senior call)* — across nine edge-case prompts. Examples: *"invitation sent by an admin who is no longer in the org, expired or not"* (honor); *"signed-in user clicks accept; their session email is the invited email, case-difference only"* (honor — lower-case both sides); *"signed-in user clicks accept; session email completely differs from invited email"* (refuse); *"the org was deleted before the invitee clicked"* (refuse — the row was cascaded; the generic refusal lands); *"two tabs accept the same invite"* (idempotent — one wins, the other gets the friendly branch); *"the invitee is already a member of the org"* (refuse — `already-member` from the send side; on the accept side, the row's status would never be pending for an already-member because the send action would have refused); *"the invitation is for `bob@acme.com` and Bob's account is on `Bob@Acme.com` — case mismatch only"* (honor — comparison is lowercased both sides); *"the inviter was demoted from admin to member between send and accept"* (honor — the invite carries `role`, not `inviterRole`; the org's decision was snapshotted at send time); *"Bob accepts an Acme invite while owning Bob Consulting in another tenant"* (honor — cross-org state is independent; the active-org switch happens on accept, his Bob Consulting ownership untouched). Wrong-answer feedback names the senior reasoning each misplacement misses. Confirmed by a `MultipleChoice` round of two scenarios. *"A teammate proposes a 'use my current email instead' confirmation dialog on the email-mismatch screen — 'because the user clearly forwarded the email to their preferred account.' What's the senior pushback?"* — the right answer names the privilege-confusion vector (the wrong human accepts because they received the forward; the invitation's identity is the email, and tying it to a different account silently is a real security risk), names the alternative (the explicit "ask Alice to re-invite my preferred address" recovery path, which keeps the org's offer-to-an-address contract intact). *"The team's instinct on the double-click race is to add `SELECT ... FOR UPDATE` on the invitation row before the member insert. What's the senior reading?"* — the right answer names the WHERE-on-status filter as the sufficient lock (no separate `FOR UPDATE` needed; the UPDATE's predicate *is* the optimistic-concurrency check), names the cost (`FOR UPDATE` holds a row lock for the duration of the transaction, blocking other concurrent reads on a hot row for no added safety), names the idempotent short-circuit on rows-affected = 0 as the recovery surface.

**Components.**
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-coded HTML four-row edge-case decision table (scenario / senior call / where the check lives / alternative named once).
- `Figure` wrapping a hand-SVG double-click-race four-frame sequence diagram with the WHERE-on-status annotation.
- `Buckets` for the nine-prompt three-column sort (honor / refuse / idempotent short-circuit).
- `MultipleChoice` for the mismatch-confirmation-dialog and `FOR UPDATE` diagnoses.
- `Aside` (`note`) below the decision table: "edge cases are decision shape, not code shape. Honor where the org's decision survives the inviter's state; refuse strictly where privilege-confusion lurks; let the WHERE clause be the lock; check membership in the application layer because the database doesn't constrain it. Document the alternative once; don't ship both."

**Project link.** The 10.4 project doesn't ship the orphan-inviter or email-mismatch refusal explicitly (the project's "Done when" list cuts to the core invite handshake — 10.4.7), but the decision table is the *frame* the project's "scope cuts" callout references when explaining what year-1 invitations *won't* attempt; the double-click race's WHERE-on-status pattern is the *why* the project's `acceptInvitationAction` uses the rows-affected idiom rather than a separate concurrency primitive.

## Component proposals

None. Every load-bearing artifact in this chapter composes from existing components — `Figure` wrapping hand-SVGs (Concepts 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10), `Figure` wrapping hand-coded HTML tables (Concepts 4, 6, 9, 10), `Code` and `AnnotatedCode` (Concept 4), `CodeVariants` (Concepts 2, 7), `DiagramSequence` (Concept 9), `TabbedContent` (Concept 7), plus the standard exercise primitives (`Tokens`, `Buckets`, `Sequence`, `Matching`, `MultipleChoice`, `DrizzleSchemaCoding`, `ScriptCoding`, `ReactCoding`).

Three candidate bespoke components were considered and rejected.

A *`SignedUrlAnatomyWidget`* for Concept 4 — interactive URL builder where the student edits any byte of `id`, `token`, or `sig` and watches a live "verify result" indicator transition between pass / fail-fast / fail-after-db-load. Rejected because the teaching weight is on the *layered defense* (the `Figure`-wrapped ledger carries this concretely) and on the *cost-and-order ordering* of the verify (Concept 6's pipeline diagram carries this), not on byte-level interactivity. The `AnnotatedCode` on the URL anatomy plus the `ScriptCoding` exercise on `signedInviteUrl` + `verifyInviteSig` together cover what the widget would teach, at zero new build cost. Forward-link is weak — the signed-URL primitive recurs only in webhook signatures (Chapter 14 territory) where the teaching shape would be different (signing direction reversed); a bespoke widget here would be single-use.

A *`PendingInviteListPlayground`* for Concept 9 — sandbox React component preloaded with five mock pending invites, where the student exercises Resend / Revoke / Re-invite collision and watches a side-by-side timeline of (a) the row's `tokenHash` and `expiresAt` changing, (b) the audit-log events firing, (c) the invitee's "mailbox" receiving fresh emails. Rejected because the `DiagramSequence` already captures the rotation lifecycle as a scrubbed visual (frame 3's "old URL fails" beat is the load-bearing teaching moment, and a scrub carries it without requiring the student to drive the state machine themselves), and the `ReactCoding` exercise on the actual list component is the strongest assessment form (the student writes the surface, the grader checks the behavior). A playground would be a third tool covering the same ground.

A *`FourArrivalShapesSimulator`* for Concept 7 — interactive simulator that lets the student set "session signed-in / signed-out" and "session email matches / differs" toggles and watches the page render switch between the four shapes in real time. Considered seriously. Rejected in favor of `TabbedContent` because (a) the four shapes are discrete and small enough that tab navigation is faster than toggle-setting, (b) the teaching weight is on the *exhaustiveness* of the four shapes (the tabs make this visible as "look, four tabs, that's the set"), and (c) the `Matching` exercise on user-state-to-render is the assessment form that locks the matrix in. The simulator's value-add over the tabs would be on a fifth or sixth shape — and the senior reading is *there is no fifth shape*; surfacing this exhaustively in tabs is itself the teaching move.

## Open pedagogical questions

- Concept 4's HMAC-on-top-of-random-token shape is the chapter's most novel cryptographic idea (every prior token flow in 9.3 used hash-only, no signature). The open question is whether the *defense-in-depth* framing reads as "real value" or as "ceremony" to a student who's already absorbed the hash-at-rest discipline. The current default keeps both layers and teaches the two-dimension framing explicitly; if field-testing finds the second layer reading as ceremony, the fallback is to lean on the *cost* argument first (the HMAC rejects before DB load — abuse-protection at the request layer) and treat the secret-rotation-independence as a secondary win, which reframes the layer as a request-rate-limit primitive rather than a cryptographic deepening.
- Concept 8's auto-`emailVerified` for invite-sourced sign-ups is a real product decision with a legitimate alternative (force the standard verify flow anyway, even after invite-click). The current default goes with auto-verify as the senior reflex; the alternative is named once. If the curriculum's later chapters (17.2 — privacy/GDPR; 22 — security) revisit this and the senior call ends up differing on regulated-vertical SaaS, this chapter's framing should add a forward-link to the per-vertical reconsideration; for now the simple call holds.
- Concept 10's table-with-nine-buckets is the chapter's broadest exercise scope. The open question is whether the three-column `Buckets` (honor / refuse / idempotent) reads cleanly against the existing two-column `Buckets` shape installed across the curriculum. If the three-column form is awkward in the existing component, the fallback is two adjacent two-column `Buckets` exercises (honor-vs-refuse, then refuse-vs-idempotent) at slight cost to the single-screen overview; the teaching weight is on the senior reasoning per row, not on the column count.
