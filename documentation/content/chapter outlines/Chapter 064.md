# Chapter 064 — Stripe billing and plan entitlements

## Chapter framing

Chapter 064 lands the Stripe billing surface — the money seam of the SaaS — on top of the webhook ingestion machinery shipped in chapter 063. The student arrives with a verified, idempotent, ordering-aware webhook handler (lesson 1 of chapter 063–lesson 4 of chapter 063), a `processed_events` ledger, and the discipline that the webhook is the only writer for entitlement state. This chapter teaches the Stripe model and the application contract that sits on top of it: what Products, Prices, Customers, and Subscriptions actually are; how Checkout starts a subscription; how the Customer Portal owns plan changes and cancellation; how the application reads a single derived `plan_entitlements` row instead of asking Stripe at request time; how Stripe's subscription statuses (`trialing`, `active`, `past_due`, `canceled`, `incomplete`) project into first-class application states rather than collapsing into a boolean; and how a thin `billing.*` interface is the second of two named carve-outs to the "use the framework's conventions, don't wrap SDKs" principle.

Threads that run through every lesson: Stripe is the source of truth for billing facts; the database is the source of truth for *derived* entitlements the app reads at every request — never call `stripe.*` on the hot path. The webhook (chapter 063) is the only writer for `plan_entitlements`; Checkout and the Portal are the only writers for Stripe-side state. Status is a string with semantics, not a boolean — `past_due` is "let them in, warn them," `canceled` is "downgrade at period end," `incomplete` is "treat as not subscribed." The `billing.*` interface lives at the app boundary so call sites read `billing.requirePlan('pro')` instead of touching Stripe shapes, the swap cost across pricing experiments justifies the wrapper, and the principle that earned the carve-out also names everything that doesn't earn one (Resend, Trigger.dev, R2). The chapter pairs with chapter 063's idempotency discipline and feeds directly into the chapter project chapter 065 — every concept ships in code there.

## Lesson 1 — The Stripe object graph

Teaches the four-object Stripe model (Products, Prices, Customers, Subscriptions) the rest of the chapter assumes, including `lookup_key` over hardcoded IDs, metadata as the carry-channel for `organization_id`, and the test-vs-live mode discipline.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Stripe's API surface is large; only a small subgraph matters for SaaS subscriptions. Which objects does the application read, which does it write to (always indirectly), and what's the relationship that lets a "Pro plan, monthly, yearly toggle, with a 14-day trial" map cleanly onto Stripe primitives? The lesson lands the four-object mental model the rest of the chapter assumes.
- **Products — the plan as a marketing concept.** A Product in Stripe is "the thing you sell" (Pro plan, Team plan). Products carry name, description, marketing-features metadata, and a stable `id`. Products themselves have no price; pricing lives on Prices. The senior anchor: one Product per plan tier, not per billing cycle — Pro is one Product whose monthly and yearly are two Prices.
- **Prices — the billing cycle and the amount.** A Price binds a Product to a recurring interval (`month`, `year`), a currency, an amount in the currency's smallest unit (cents), and a `lookup_key` that the application uses to find the right Price without hardcoding Stripe IDs. The senior reach: code references `lookup_key`, not `price_id` — IDs change between test and live mode, lookup keys are stable.
- **Customers — the per-billing-account record.** A Stripe Customer is the entity that owns a subscription, a payment method, and an invoice history. The course pins the rule: one Stripe Customer per Organization (from Unit 9), not per user — billing is org-scoped because seats, invoices, and tax IDs are org-scoped. Names the threshold and the `organizations.stripe_customer_id` column shape.
- **Subscriptions — the recurring relationship.** A Subscription joins a Customer to one or more Price items, carries a `status` (next lesson), tracks `current_period_start`/`end`, and emits webhooks on every state change. The senior anchor: one active subscription per Customer in the SaaS default; multiple subscriptions are an edge case (mixed B2C/B2B, multiple workspaces under one billing org) that earns a discussion when it comes up.
- **The 80/20 of object metadata.** Three pieces of metadata earn their keep: `Customer.metadata.organization_id` (so a webhook can find the org without a separate lookup table), `Subscription.metadata.plan` (the canonical plan slug — `pro`, `team`), and `Price.lookup_key` (so the app finds Prices by name). Names the discipline: metadata is for things you'll need at webhook-receipt time and don't want to round-trip the DB to discover.
- **What the application stores vs. what Stripe stores.** Stripe owns: Customer, Subscription, Price catalog, Invoices, payment methods, the full state machine. The app stores: `organizations.stripe_customer_id`, the derived `plan_entitlements` row (lesson 4 of chapter 064), and the immutable invoice records it pulls down for reporting. The senior anchor: don't mirror Stripe's full schema — mirror only what the app reads on the hot path.
- **The seed/sync question — pricing as code.** Some teams version the Stripe catalog in code and apply it via a `pnpm seed:stripe` script that creates or updates Products and Prices through the API; others click around in the dashboard. The course defaults to code-as-source-of-truth for the catalog because diffs are reviewable and test/live mode parity is automatic. Names the convention; the project ships the seed script.
- **Test mode vs. live mode — the discipline.** Stripe gives every account two parallel universes. Test-mode keys, Customers, Subscriptions, and webhooks are wholly separate. The lesson names: dev and CI run against test mode; staging runs against test mode; production runs against live mode; the secret is loaded from env. The Stripe CLI defaults to test mode unless `--live` is passed.
- **The webhook events the application listens to.** A short table maps Stripe event types to the application action: `checkout.session.completed` (provision entitlement on first subscribe), `customer.subscription.created/updated/deleted` (refresh entitlement and status), `invoice.paid` (record the invoice, advance period), `invoice.payment_failed` (the past-due transition). The full handler code lands in the project (chapter 065); the lesson states the surface and what each event means.
- **The Stripe Node SDK — what the course uses.** `stripe` npm package, server-side only, instantiated once with the secret key. The SDK is the one place `stripe.*` calls are allowed; everything else goes through `billing.*` (lesson 6 of chapter 064). The lesson names the singleton pattern (`lib/stripe.ts` exports a configured client) and the env entry (`STRIPE_SECRET_KEY`).
- **Watch-outs:** putting `price_id` strings in code couples the app to a single Stripe mode and breaks on first test-to-live promotion — `lookup_key` instead; one Customer per user (instead of per org) makes seat-based plans unmodellable later; storing the full Stripe Subscription shape in the app DB doubles the schema work and goes stale every time Stripe adds a field — derive what you need; secret keys in client bundles is the classic catastrophic leak — `STRIPE_SECRET_KEY` is server-only, `STRIPE_PUBLISHABLE_KEY` is the client-side counterpart; mixing test and live mode keys in the same env file by name confusion is a real incident category — name the env entries explicitly and check the prefix on boot.

What this lesson does not cover:

- Checkout sessions and the subscription start flow — lesson 2 of chapter 064.
- Customer Portal — lesson 3 of chapter 064.
- The `plan_entitlements` derived view — lesson 4 of chapter 064.
- Subscription status semantics — lesson 5 of chapter 064.
- The `billing.*` interface — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- Tax, invoicing details, dunning, revenue recognition — out of scope for the course.

Estimated student time: 35 to 45 minutes. The vocabulary lesson — every later lesson refers to these four objects.

## Lesson 2 — Starting subscriptions with Checkout

Teaches the Server Action that creates a hosted Checkout session, lazy Stripe Customer creation per organization, trials via `subscription_data`, and the success-page polling that waits for the webhook to land the entitlement.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** A user clicks "Upgrade to Pro" on the pricing page. The application needs to (a) move them through a PCI-compliant payment flow, (b) start a Subscription bound to the correct Customer at the correct Price, (c) come back to a success page that doesn't render before the entitlement exists, and (d) never store a card number or handle PCI in-house. Checkout sessions are the Stripe primitive that delivers (a)–(d). The lesson lands the Server Action that creates the session, the redirect, and the integration with the webhook landing the actual provisioning.
- **Checkout session as a single-use, server-created object.** A Checkout Session is a one-time URL Stripe creates server-side, parameterized with the Customer, Price, success/cancel URLs, mode (`subscription` for recurring), and optional trial. The user is redirected to the Stripe-hosted URL, completes payment, gets redirected back. The senior anchor: the session is server-created (Stripe secret required) and short-lived (24 hours).
- **Hosted vs. embedded — the 2026 default.** Stripe ships both modes: hosted (redirect to Stripe-owned URL) and embedded (iframe-style component on your page). The course defaults to hosted because it ships faster, requires no client-side Stripe.js wiring, and works identically across web and mobile webviews. Embedded earns its weight when retention data shows the redirect breaks conversion or when the brand demands a single-domain experience. Names the threshold; defaults to hosted.
- **The Server Action shape — `billing.upgrade(plan)`.** Foreshadows the interface from lesson 6 of chapter 064. The action: read the current org and its `stripe_customer_id` (create one lazily if missing), look up the target Price by `lookup_key` from the plan slug, call `stripe.checkout.sessions.create({...})` with `mode: 'subscription'`, `customer`, `line_items: [{ price, quantity: 1 }]`, `success_url`, `cancel_url`, `subscription_data.metadata.organization_id`, return `{ url }`, redirect on the client. The lesson shows the signature; full code lands in chapter 065.
- **Lazy Stripe Customer creation.** The first time an org goes through Checkout, the app calls `stripe.customers.create({ email, metadata: { organization_id } })`, stores the returned `id` on `organizations.stripe_customer_id`, then proceeds. Subsequent Checkouts reuse the existing Customer. The senior reach: never create a Customer per session — duplicates produce two billing identities the user has to reconcile in support.
- **The success URL and the redirect race.** The `success_url` is the page the user lands on after paying. Stripe appends `?session_id={CHECKOUT_SESSION_ID}` if requested. The lesson restates the redirect-versus-webhook race from lesson 3 of chapter 063: the user lands on `/billing/success?session_id=...` before `checkout.session.completed` lands. The success page reads-and-polls. Don't write entitlements from the success page; the webhook owns that write.
- **The cancel URL and what counts as cancel.** `cancel_url` fires when the user dismisses the Checkout page without paying. Treat it as "no state change" — the user might come back. The senior anchor: don't log "user canceled subscription" on cancel-url visits; the user never had a subscription to cancel.
- **Trials in the session — `subscription_data.trial_period_days`.** A 14-day trial is a property on the Subscription, set at creation. Pass `subscription_data: { trial_period_days: 14 }` on the Checkout session, and the resulting Subscription starts in status `trialing`. The senior anchor: trials are a Subscription concept; the entitlement layer (lesson 4 of chapter 064) reads `trialing` as a full entitlement, the billing screen surfaces the days remaining.
- **Payment-method collection in trial mode.** Stripe lets you decide whether to collect a payment method at trial start. The course defaults to "collect at trial start" because it converts higher and avoids the "trial ended, no card on file, downgrade" awkwardness. Names the option (`payment_method_collection: 'always'`) and the trade-off.
- **Promotion codes and coupons — when to enable them.** A `allow_promotion_codes: true` flag on the session lets users redeem codes created in the Stripe dashboard. The course names the option for the discount-code feature; the project ships without it.
- **Customer-update permissions on the session.** A session can allow address/email updates that propagate back to the Customer. Pass `customer_update: { address: 'auto', name: 'auto' }` when collecting tax-relevant address data. Names the option; the project keeps it minimal.
- **Tax — what `automatic_tax: { enabled: true }` does and doesn't.** Stripe Tax computes sales tax/VAT on the session. The course names the toggle and the requirement (tax registrations configured in the dashboard); doesn't drill the tax compliance story.
- **What lands when Checkout completes.** The full sequence: user pays → Stripe creates Subscription → fires `checkout.session.completed` → fires `customer.subscription.created` → success page polls → webhook lands, upserts `plan_entitlements` → next poll reads new entitlement → page swaps from "finalizing" to "you're on Pro." The lesson maps the flow against the webhook handlers from chapter 063.
- **The "Upgrade" Server Action signature.** Reference signature for the project: `billing.upgrade(planSlug: 'pro' | 'team'): Promise<{ url: string }>`. The action: resolves org → ensures Customer → creates session → returns URL. Returns the URL rather than redirecting server-side so the client can choose `window.location.assign` (default) or open a new tab.
- **The `billing.*` namespace anchor.** Names `billing.upgrade` as one of three methods in the carve-out interface (with `openPortal` and `requirePlan`). Doesn't drill the architecture — that's lesson 6 of chapter 064 — but plants the flag so the action signature matches.
- **Watch-outs:** creating a new Stripe Customer per Checkout multiplies billing identities — lookup-and-reuse on `organizations.stripe_customer_id`; passing raw `price_id` strings makes the action mode-dependent — resolve via `lookup_key`; writing entitlements on the success-page render duplicates writers and races the webhook — the success page reads-and-polls only; trusting `?session_id=...` as a "they paid" signal without verifying the session server-side is forgeable — the webhook is the proof; setting `success_url` to a marketing page outside the app loses the polling story — keep the user inside the app shell; the Checkout session URL is single-use and expires; storing or emailing it is pointless; embedding Checkout without auditing the parent page's CSP can break the iframe — hosted avoids this entirely.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The webhook side that provisions entitlements — Chapter 063 and project chapter 065.
- The Customer Portal for plan changes — lesson 3 of chapter 064.
- The `plan_entitlements` row shape — lesson 4 of chapter 064.
- Status semantics (`trialing`, etc.) — lesson 5 of chapter 064.
- The full architectural rationale for the `billing.*` interface — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- Embedded Checkout wiring — named, not drilled.

Estimated student time: 45 to 55 minutes. The "money in" lesson — the one path that turns visitors into customers.

## Lesson 3 — Managing subscriptions with the Portal

Teaches the hosted Customer Portal as the default for plan changes, period-end cancellation, payment-method updates, and invoice history, plus deep-link flows and the rule that the return URL is a navigation hint, not state-change proof.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Once a user has a subscription, they need to change plan, switch monthly/yearly, update card, download invoices, and cancel. Each of these is a screen the application would otherwise build, test, and maintain — and Stripe ships them all, behind a single redirect, configured from the Stripe dashboard. When does building this in-house earn its weight, and what does the application have to do to integrate the Portal cleanly? The lesson lands "always use the Portal until product reasons force the carve-out."
- **The Portal as a hosted screen — the default.** `stripe.billingPortal.sessions.create({ customer, return_url })` returns a one-shot URL. The user is redirected, manages billing, gets returned to `return_url`. The application writes zero billing UI. The senior anchor: the Portal is the closest thing to a free, maintained, compliant settings screen in the entire SaaS toolchain — defaulting to it is correct.
- **What the Portal lets the user do — the configuration surface.** Configured per-account in the Stripe dashboard: allow plan changes (which Products are switchable), allow cancellation (immediate vs. at period end), allow updating payment method, allow tax ID/address updates, surface invoice history. The course default: allow plan change among the same product family, allow cancellation at period end, allow payment-method updates, allow invoice download.
- **Cancellation behavior — `cancel_at_period_end` is the default discipline.** When a user cancels in the Portal, the configured behavior is "cancel at end of current period." This keeps the entitlement live until the paid-for period elapses; Stripe fires `customer.subscription.updated` with `cancel_at_period_end: true`, then `customer.subscription.deleted` at the end. The application surfaces "your subscription ends on Aug 14" until the deletion event. Names the rule: never cancel immediately by default; users paid for the month.
- **Plan changes — proration, immediate effect, and the events fired.** A user upgrading from monthly to yearly, or Pro to Team, generates a `customer.subscription.updated` event with new Price items and Stripe's proration math (a credit on the unused portion, an immediate charge for the new amount). The webhook handler refreshes `plan_entitlements` and the new plan takes effect at the next request. The senior anchor: trust Stripe's proration; don't try to compute it in-app.
- **Portal deep-link flows — the 2026 reach.** Stripe ships `flow_data` on the Portal session to deep-link directly to a specific action: subscription update (already on this Price), subscription cancellation confirmation, payment method update. Names the API and one example (`flow_data: { type: 'subscription_cancel', subscription_cancel: { subscription } }`). Used when the application wants a "Cancel" button that lands on the cancel confirmation instead of the Portal home.
- **Authentication and the access question.** The Portal session is for one Customer; the URL is short-lived. The application authenticates the user (server-side org-scoping from Unit 9), resolves the org's `stripe_customer_id`, creates the session, redirects. The Portal itself doesn't re-authenticate — the URL is the auth. The senior anchor: the URL is bearer-style; treat it like any other one-time link.
- **The Server Action shape — `billing.openPortal()`.** Signature for the project: `billing.openPortal(returnPath?: string): Promise<{ url: string }>`. Resolves org → ensures Customer (errors if missing — no Customer means no subscription means no Portal) → creates Portal session with `return_url` defaulting to the billing page → returns URL → client redirects.
- **When the in-house screen earns its weight — the threshold.** Conditions that flip the choice: regulated industries with explicit consent screens the Portal can't host, complex plan-change UX (sliders for seat counts with custom validation), B2B contract upgrades that require sales approval, internationalization of the billing copy beyond Stripe's language list. The course names the threshold and the cost (every screen you build is one Stripe-maintained screen you now maintain in perpetuity). The chapter defaults to Portal.
- **The Portal-configuration-as-code question.** Stripe lets you version Portal configurations via the API (`stripe.billingPortal.configurations.*`). Same calculus as the Product/Price seed (lesson 1 of chapter 064): code-as-source-of-truth is reviewable and reproducible across modes. The course mentions the option; the project ships dashboard-configured for simplicity.
- **Return URL discipline — don't trust it as state-change proof.** The return URL fires regardless of what the user did in the Portal (changed plan, looked around, did nothing). The application must not "assume changes" from the redirect — the webhooks are the source of truth, the redirect is a navigation hint only. The senior anchor: the Portal's redirect is *not* a transaction completion signal.
- **The "no Customer yet" case.** If `openPortal()` runs for an org without a `stripe_customer_id`, the action returns a typed error rather than creating an empty Customer. The UI surfaces "subscribe to manage billing" instead. Names the rule: Portal is for existing customers; new subscriptions go through Checkout (lesson 2 of chapter 064).
- **The free-plan "see invoices" question.** A user who once paid and downgraded to free still has invoices and may need them. The Customer record persists; the Portal still opens with invoice history. Names the rule: the Customer outlives any individual subscription, and the Portal stays accessible as long as the Customer exists.
- **Watch-outs:** building an in-house "change plan" screen because it "feels more on-brand" is a perennial loss-of-engineering-time — start with the Portal and earn the carve-out; treating the return URL as "they changed plan" double-fires entitlement updates already handled by the webhook — webhook is the only writer; cancellation defaulting to "immediate" surprises users who paid for the month — use period-end cancellation; not configuring the Portal in the dashboard ships a broken empty screen — the configuration is required; the Portal session URL is single-use; bookmarking or emailing it is pointless; deep-link flows can confuse users who expected the full Portal — name the destination in the link text ("Cancel subscription," not "Manage billing"); the Portal won't show usage-based metrics or product-internal data — those are the application's job.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The Checkout side of new subscriptions — lesson 2 of chapter 064.
- Webhook handlers for the subscription-update events — Chapter 063 and project chapter 065.
- The `plan_entitlements` write side — lesson 4 of chapter 064.
- `billing.*` interface composition rationale — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- Building custom billing UI — explicitly not the default path.

Estimated student time: 35 to 45 minutes. The "money managed" lesson — the screens the application doesn't have to build.

## Lesson 4 — Plan entitlements as a derived view

Teaches the `plan_entitlements` schema, the one-row-per-org projection of Stripe state, the single-writer rule (only the webhook writes it), and the `getEntitlement` read helper that every request-path gate calls instead of touching Stripe.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** The application needs to answer "can this user do this?" on every request — render the Pro features, gate the export endpoint, count seats, throw on quota. Calling `stripe.subscriptions.retrieve(...)` per request is wrong (latency, rate limits, single point of failure). Mirroring the full Stripe subscription schema is wrong (drift, schema churn). What's the minimum durable, query-fast representation the application reads on the hot path, and where does it get updated from? The lesson lands `plan_entitlements` as a derived view, written exclusively by the webhook, read on every request.
- **The derived-state pattern — Stripe is the truth, the app stores the projection.** Stripe owns the Subscription's full state; the app owns a small projection — current plan, status, period end, key boolean entitlements — denormalized into one row per organization. The senior anchor: derived state is read-shaped, not write-shaped — every column exists because some request path reads it without joining.
- **The schema — `plan_entitlements`.** Columns the course ships: `organization_id` (PK, FK to orgs), `plan` (`'free' | 'pro' | 'team'`), `status` (the Stripe-derived status, lesson lesson 5 of chapter 064), `subscription_id` (the Stripe Subscription ID, nullable for free plans), `current_period_end` (timestamptz, nullable), `cancel_at_period_end` (boolean), `seats` (int for seat-based plans), `last_event_at` (timestamptz, for the ordering predicate from lesson 3 of chapter 063), `updated_at` (timestamptz). One row per org, created at org creation with `plan: 'free'`.
- **Why one row per org, not per subscription.** The application reads "what plan is this org on right now." That's one answer. Modeling history (the Subscription log) is a separate concern — derived from Stripe events when needed, not on the hot path. Names the rule: hot-path tables are about *current state*; history is a different table or comes from Stripe directly.
- **The write side — the webhook is the only writer.** Restates from lesson 3 of chapter 063 as the load-bearing rule. Every entitlement column is computed in the webhook handler from the Stripe event, then UPSERT-ed with the `last_event_at` predicate. The application never writes this table from a Server Action or a Portal callback. Names the principle structurally: single-writer tables are hard to write to from the wrong place if the only Server Actions that touch the table are the ones the project explicitly forbids.
- **The read side — `getEntitlement(orgId)`.** A simple Server Component query (or cached helper) returns the row. Used everywhere a feature gate is needed: dashboards check `plan`, exports check `status === 'active' || 'trialing'`, paywalls check `plan === 'free'`. The senior reach: one well-named helper, called from every gate, never replaced with ad-hoc `db.select`.
- **The provisioning step — free row at org creation.** Every new org inserts a `plan_entitlements` row with `plan: 'free'`, `status: 'active'`, the rest nullable. This means every org has an entitlement row from day one; the read side never has to handle "row missing." Closes a class of null-handling bugs structurally.
- **What "computed from Stripe" means in the handler.** A `customer.subscription.updated` event payload carries `status`, `items.data[0].price.lookup_key` (which maps to `plan`), `current_period_end`, `cancel_at_period_end`. The handler extracts these, builds the UPDATE statement, applies the `last_event_at` predicate. The lesson shows the projection function shape (`subscriptionToEntitlement(event.data.object): EntitlementPatch`); the project (chapter 065) ships the full code.
- **Why `lookup_key` and not `price_id`.** Restates from lesson 1 of chapter 064 because the projection function is where it shows up. The mapping from "Stripe Price" to "app plan" goes through `lookup_key`, so the projection is mode-independent and the catalog can be re-seeded without code changes.
- **Caching the entitlement read — when it earns its weight.** A Server Component read per request is cheap (`organizations` is small, the join is one). Caching `getEntitlement` with React's request-scoped `cache()` deduplicates within a single request. Cross-request caching (Redis, Next data cache) is named with the threshold: when the read is in a tight loop or a request budget; for the default Server Component path, the request-scoped cache is enough.
- **The `seats` count and per-user vs. per-org gating.** For team plans, `seats` is a column. The membership table (Unit 9) is the source of truth for "how many members"; the application enforces `members.count <= entitlement.seats` at the action boundary (invite, role change). The senior anchor: the entitlement says how many you bought; the membership says how many you have; the gate is the action that crosses the line.
- **The "free plan" model — no row from Stripe, full row in the app.** The free plan has no Stripe Subscription; the row in `plan_entitlements` is "free, active, no period end." The application's gates treat it like any other plan, the entitlement helper returns a uniform shape. Names the principle: the same code path serves free and paid; the only difference is the column values.
- **Audit and observability — what to log when the entitlement changes.** Every webhook-driven UPDATE logs the diff (plan-before/plan-after, status-before/status-after, event ID, organization ID). The structured logger from Chapter 092 picks it up. The senior reach: entitlement changes are high-signal events for product, support, and incident debugging — log them with enough context to reconstruct the timeline.
- **Migrations and re-derivation — what to do when the projection logic changes.** If you add a column or change a mapping, the existing rows are stale until the next webhook fires. The senior pattern: a one-shot job that walks `organizations` with non-null `stripe_customer_id`, fetches each Subscription from Stripe, re-derives, and writes. Names the migration discipline; doesn't drill the code.
- **Watch-outs:** calling `stripe.subscriptions.retrieve` on the request path makes every page load Stripe-dependent — read from `plan_entitlements`; allowing Server Actions to write `plan_entitlements` recreates the dual-writer race from lesson 3 of chapter 063 — webhook is the only writer; mirroring more Stripe columns "in case we need them" creates schema churn that pays off rarely — store the projection only; not seeding a row at org creation gives the read side a "null entitlement" branch that's easy to forget — insert at creation; defaulting `status` to a boolean (`is_paid`) collapses information that matters (`past_due` vs. `canceled`) — store the string; reading entitlement in client-only components without server-side validation lets a privileged-feature gate become a UI-only check — server is the source of truth.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The full Stripe object graph — lesson 1 of chapter 064.
- Status semantics (`trialing`, `past_due`, `canceled`, `incomplete`) — lesson 5 of chapter 064.
- The webhook handlers that write the row — Chapter 063 and project chapter 065.
- The `billing.*` interface that reads it — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- The full caching decision tree — Chapter 032 and Chapter 15.
- Membership and seat enforcement mechanics — Chapter 056.

Estimated student time: 40 to 50 minutes. The schema that the rest of the application reads. Load-bearing for the project and for every gate downstream.

## Lesson 5 — Subscription status as first-class state

Teaches the Stripe status enum (`trialing`, `active`, `past_due`, `canceled`, `incomplete`, `unpaid`), the `hasActiveAccess` decision table, grace-period banners over instant lockout, and the `cancel_at_period_end` winding-down state.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Stripe's Subscription has a `status` field that can be `trialing`, `active`, `past_due`, `canceled`, `incomplete`, `incomplete_expired`, `unpaid`. The naive shape is "if paid, let them in" — a single boolean. That model loses critical information: a `past_due` user should keep access while the dunning emails fire; an `incomplete` user (their card failed during Checkout) should be treated as if they never subscribed; a `canceled` user whose period hasn't ended yet still has access. Each status has a meaning the application's gates and UI must respect. The lesson lands status as a string with semantics, not a boolean.
- **The status enum — what each value means.** Walk through each status: `trialing` (trial active, full access, expires at `current_period_end`); `active` (paid, healthy, full access); `past_due` (an invoice payment failed but Stripe is retrying; keep access while dunning emails fire, surface a banner); `canceled` (subscription ended — either at period end (graceful) or immediately (rare); the row stays for history but access ends); `incomplete` (initial payment failed in Checkout — treat as not subscribed, no access); `incomplete_expired` (the user never completed the initial payment and the window expired — same as not subscribed); `unpaid` (Stripe has stopped trying — terminal failure, no access). Each gets a one-line "what the app does" answer.
- **The decision table — status → access decision.** A small table the lesson shows: `trialing | active | past_due` grant access; `canceled` grants access only while `current_period_end` is in the future and `cancel_at_period_end` is true (the "paid for the month, will end" case); `incomplete | incomplete_expired | unpaid` deny access. The senior anchor: the gate is `hasActiveAccess(entitlement)`, encoded once.
- **The grace period for `past_due` — letting users stay in.** A user whose card auto-renewal failed shouldn't be locked out instantly. Stripe retries the invoice over a configurable window (default ~3 weeks of smart retries). The app keeps `hasActiveAccess` true throughout, surfaces a "update your payment method" banner, and only flips when status moves to `canceled` or `unpaid`. The senior anchor: dunning is Stripe's job; the app's job is the banner and the deep-link to the Portal.
- **The `cancel_at_period_end` flag — the "winding down" state.** When a user cancels via the Portal, the Subscription moves to `active` with `cancel_at_period_end: true` and `current_period_end` set to the period end. The app keeps access until `current_period_end`, surfaces a "your subscription ends on Aug 14" banner with an "undo cancellation" link (which calls `billing.reactivate()` or sends them to the Portal). At the period end, Stripe fires `customer.subscription.deleted`, the webhook flips `status: 'canceled'`, the entitlement projects to "no access." Names the full sequence.
- **The `trialing` UI — the days-remaining surface.** During trial, the app surfaces "X days left in your Pro trial" with a "subscribe now" CTA. The CTA isn't a Checkout (they already have a Subscription) — it's a Portal deep-link to subscription_update or a `billing.confirmTrial()` Server Action that calls `stripe.subscriptions.update(...)` to remove the trial. Names the asymmetry: starting from free uses Checkout; modifying an existing subscription uses Portal or the API directly.
- **`incomplete` — the Checkout failure that doesn't look like one.** A subscription in `incomplete` means the user filled out Checkout, the Subscription got created, the first invoice's payment failed (3DS failure, declined card, etc.). The app must treat this as "no subscription" — entitlement stays on free. Stripe gives the user 23 hours to complete the payment; afterwards it moves to `incomplete_expired`. The senior anchor: don't grant access from `incomplete`; the row exists but the relationship doesn't.
- **The `unpaid` terminal state — when dunning gives up.** After Stripe's retry schedule exhausts, the Subscription moves to `unpaid` (or `canceled`, depending on dashboard configuration). The app revokes access, surfaces "your subscription was canceled due to payment failure," and offers a re-subscribe path via Checkout. Names the cliff.
- **The status banner system — the UI surface.** Every authenticated page renders a small status banner above the main content when `status !== 'active'` and `status !== 'trialing'`. Banner copy is keyed by status: "your payment failed — update your card," "your subscription ends Aug 14 — keep your Pro features," "your subscription was canceled — re-subscribe." Each carries a CTA that deep-links to the Portal. The senior reach: status awareness is a UI concern, not just a gate.
- **Computing access — `hasActiveAccess(entitlement)` as the single function.** One function, used at every gate, replaces ad-hoc `entitlement.status === 'active' || entitlement.status === 'trialing'` checks throughout the codebase. Signature: `(e: PlanEntitlement) => boolean`. Implements the decision table. The senior anchor: one function so the rule is one place to change when Stripe adds a status or the product redefines grace.
- **Mapping status to plan-feature gates — the orthogonality.** Two gates compose: "do you have access at all" (status) and "what tier are you on" (plan). A `past_due` user on Pro still has Pro features (during grace); a `canceled` user is downgraded to free. The lesson names the two-dimensional model: `(plan, hasAccess)` together.
- **Don't synthesize a synthetic status.** A tempting move is "let's add a `'trial_ended'` synthetic status." Stripe doesn't emit that; computing it requires "status was trialing and now isn't" — a state machine over events. The senior anchor: don't invent statuses Stripe doesn't ship; let the application read the Stripe-derived status and compose semantic predicates (`isTrialEnded(entitlement)`) at the call site.
- **The seat-overage problem — what to do when the team plan loses seats.** A Team plan with 10 seats whose subscription downgrades to 5 seats (via Portal) leaves 5 over-seated members. The app's response: don't kick anyone out automatically; surface a banner ("you have 10 members but 5 seats — remove 5 or upgrade"), block new invites until reconciled, let the owner pick. Names the discipline: the app doesn't make permission decisions on the user's behalf; it surfaces the constraint and lets a human resolve.
- **Watch-outs:** treating status as a boolean loses `past_due` and `cancel_at_period_end` and ships either a locked-out angry customer or a freeloading lapsed one; granting access from `incomplete` lets card-failure attempts get a free ride; revoking access immediately on user cancel is a fair-billing violation — they paid for the month; not surfacing a banner for `past_due` means the user discovers the lapse by losing access — banner first, then revocation; adding an `is_paid` column alongside `status` recreates the boolean problem and lets the two go out of sync — derive at read time; synthesizing statuses Stripe doesn't ship creates a state machine no one wants to own; assuming Stripe will retry forever — `unpaid` is the terminal cliff; the seat-overage flow is product-specific; the project ships the banner-and-block approach, real products may invert.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The `plan_entitlements` schema and write side — lesson 4 of chapter 064.
- The webhook handlers that produce the status updates — Chapter 063 and project chapter 065.
- The `billing.*` interface that consumes `hasActiveAccess` — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- Dunning configuration in the Stripe dashboard — named, not drilled.
- Custom retry logic or in-app payment recovery — out of scope; Stripe handles it.

Estimated student time: 40 to 50 minutes. The lesson that makes the application status-aware instead of binary.

## Lesson 6 — The thin billing interface

Teaches the `billing.*` carve-out to Architectural Principle #5 — `upgrade`, `openPortal`, and `requirePlan` as the three methods, `requirePlan` as the load-bearing server-side gate, and `/lib/billing/` as the only place the Stripe SDK is imported.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** lesson 4 of chapter 043 introduced Architectural Principle #5: use the framework's conventions, don't invent a tRPC-style call wrapper. Server Actions are the default at the action seam; route handlers at the untrusted-input seam. Yet two surfaces earn an interface: the `authedAction` authz wrapper (lesson 2 of chapter 057) and now a `billing.*` interface around Stripe. Why does billing earn the carve-out, what does the interface look like, and where exactly is the boundary between "use the framework directly" and "wrap it"? The lesson lands the billing interface and pins the rule that explains both carve-outs.
- **The threshold for an interface.** A wrapper earns its weight when (a) the SDK shape is read-hostile at the call site, (b) the swap cost across changes (pricing experiments, vendor change) is high, (c) it centralizes a discipline (auth, gating) that would otherwise spread. Billing passes three for three: Stripe's `checkout.sessions.create` shape is six lines of structure per call, pricing experiments rewrite call sites often, and "must have an org," "must have a Customer," "must scope by plan" centralizes cleanly.
- **The interface — three methods, one file.** `billing.upgrade(planSlug)`, `billing.openPortal(returnPath?)`, `billing.requirePlan(planSlug)`. Each is a Server Action or a Server Component helper depending on use. Signatures:
  - `upgrade(planSlug: 'pro' | 'team'): Promise<{ url: string }>` — creates a Checkout session, returns the URL for the client to redirect to.
  - `openPortal(returnPath?: string): Promise<{ url: string }>` — creates a Portal session, returns the URL.
  - `requirePlan(planSlug: 'pro' | 'team'): Promise<void>` — server-side gate; reads the current org's entitlement, throws if the plan tier is insufficient or if `hasActiveAccess` is false. Called at the top of Server Components and Server Actions that gate behind a plan.
- **The directory shape — `/lib/billing/`.** Following the directory shape from lesson 4 of chapter 043: `lib/billing/stripe.ts` (the configured SDK client), `lib/billing/upgrade.ts` (the action), `lib/billing/portal.ts` (the action), `lib/billing/require-plan.ts` (the gate), `lib/billing/index.ts` (re-export). The `stripe` import only happens inside `/lib/billing/`; everywhere else imports from `billing`. The senior anchor: the SDK is a transitive dependency of `billing`, never a direct dependency of the rest of the app.
- **Why `requirePlan` is the load-bearing gate.** The wrapper's main payoff isn't the Checkout/Portal sessions — those are one call each. It's `requirePlan`. Every privileged Server Component reads `await billing.requirePlan('pro')` at the top; the implementation reads the entitlement, evaluates `hasActiveAccess` and the plan tier, throws a structured error on fail. One call site, one rule, structurally enforced — the missing-call class becomes the same kind of "find every Server Action that doesn't call `authedAction`" lint that Unit 16 audits.
- **What `requirePlan` throws and how the framework handles it.** A `BillingError` (custom Error subclass — closes the thread from lesson 2 of chapter 009) with a status code and a message. Server Component code throws and `error.tsx` at the segment catches; Server Action code throws and the action's return shape becomes `{ ok: false, error }`. Names the pattern; restates from chapter 043.
- **What's explicitly NOT in the interface — the "don't wrap" cut.** No `billing.cancelSubscription` (use the Portal); no `billing.changePlan` (use the Portal or a Portal deep-link); no `billing.listInvoices` (use the Portal). The interface is "what the application initiates"; everything the user initiates routes through the Portal. The senior anchor: the interface is small because most billing flows belong to Stripe-hosted UI.
- **The two carve-outs in one frame.** `authedAction` (lesson 2 of chapter 057) and `billing.*` (this lesson) are the only two carve-outs to "use the framework's conventions" in the entire course. Both because (a) the wrapping centralizes a discipline (authz and billing), (b) the underlying shape would otherwise spread across every call site, (c) the swap cost (auth provider change, billing vendor change) is real. The lesson restates the principle as: "wrap when the discipline matters more than the call site's clarity, and only then."
- **Testability — the wrapper is the seam for tests.** Because all billing calls go through `billing.*`, integration tests can mock the three methods rather than the full Stripe SDK. The senior reach: testing surface area shrinks from "every Stripe call across the codebase" to "three function mocks." Names the payoff; restates the testing thread from Unit 18 at a high level.
- **Watch-outs:** wrapping the SDK without the discipline (just a "billing.stripe = stripe" alias) is the worst of both worlds — pure indirection; adding methods like `billing.cancel()` reverses the "user-initiated goes through Portal" call and pulls UI into the app you didn't sign up for; spreading `import stripe` across the app defeats the seam — only `/lib/billing/` may import `stripe`; not enforcing `requirePlan` at the action boundary leaves UI-only gates as the last line of defense — server gate first; failing to map `BillingError` through the same error contract as the rest of the app produces inconsistent error UX — share the contract; using the wrapper for the wrong things (calling `billing.upgrade` from a Server Component just because it exists) confuses the surface — actions for state changes, helpers for reads.

What this lesson does not cover:

- Architectural Principle #5 in general — lesson 4 of chapter 043.
- The `authedAction` wrapper, the first carve-out — lesson 2 of chapter 057.
- The third-party-not-wrapped principle for Resend/Trigger.dev/R2 — lesson 7 of chapter 064.
- Custom error subclasses — lesson 2 of chapter 009.
- The full Server Action and error-contract shape — Chapter 043 and chapter 046.
- The implementation code for `requirePlan` (worked example shipped in chapter 065).

Estimated student time: 40 to 50 minutes. The architecture lesson — names the rule that explains both carve-outs and the silence around everything else.

## Lesson 7 — When an SDK adapter earns its weight

Teaches the three-test threshold for wrapping an SDK (read-hostile shape, real swap cost, discipline to centralize), and applies it to show why auth and billing earn interfaces while Resend, Trigger.dev, and R2 only earn helpers.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Billing earned a wrapper (lesson 6 of chapter 064); auth earned a wrapper (lesson 2 of chapter 057). The course uses Resend, Trigger.dev, and Cloudflare R2 — three more third-party SDKs — and explicitly does not wrap them. What's the test that produces "yes" for billing and "no" for these, and how does the rule stay decidable rather than aesthetic? The lesson lands the test as an explicit threshold and applies it to each of the named integrations.
- **Resend — fails the test, deliberately.** Resend's SDK shape is simple: `resend.emails.send({ from, to, subject, react })`. Call sites are readable as-is. Swap cost is low (any transactional email vendor exposes the same five fields). The discipline (suppression check, template rendering) lives in a `sendEmail` helper from Unit 7 — but that's a helper, not a wrapper around the SDK. The helper calls `resend.emails.send` directly. The senior anchor: when the SDK is already terse and the discipline is a thin pre-flight check, a helper is enough, not an interface.
- **Trigger.dev — fails the test, deliberately.** Trigger.dev's primitives (`task`, `schemaTask`, `runs.trigger` — SDK introduced in Chapter 066; used here for shape comparison, not as a prerequisite) are themselves the abstraction — they already wrap the durable-execution discipline (Chapter 066). Wrapping them again adds nothing. Call sites read `myTask.trigger(payload)`; the application's discipline (Zod schemas, idempotency keys at the trigger) lives in the task definition, not in an outer wrapper. The senior anchor: when the vendor's SDK is itself the discipline-bearing layer, wrapping is redundant.
- **R2 — fails the test, deliberately.** The S3-compatible SDK is verbose (`PutObjectCommand({ Bucket, Key, Body, ContentType })` — SDK introduced in Chapter 068; used here for shape comparison, not as a prerequisite), but the call sites are few (one for presigned upload, one for presigned download — Chapter 068). The swap cost is real (S3 and R2 are interchangeable; Backblaze and Tigris are nearly so), but the call site shape doesn't multiply because the SDK is used in two places only. A helper named for intent (`presignedPut(key)`) wraps the verbosity at the call sites; the SDK call is one line below.
- **The matrix — when a wrapper, when a helper, when neither.**

  | Test | Resend | Trigger.dev | R2 | Auth (chapter 057) | Billing (chapter 064) |
  |------|--------|-------------|----|-------------|----------------|
  | Read-hostile shape | no | no | partly | yes | yes |
  | Swap cost real | low | medium | medium | high | high |
  | Discipline lives in wrapper | helper does it | SDK already does | helper does it | yes (authz) | yes (gating, scoping) |

  Two-of-three is "helper at the call site." Three-of-three is "interface in `/lib`." Names the cut.

- **Helper vs. interface — what differs.** A helper is a function in `/lib` (or co-located) that simplifies one call. An interface is a module with a stable public surface — all imports go through `billing.*` or `authedAction(...)`, never the underlying SDK. The senior anchor: the discipline question is whether other code is *forbidden* from touching the SDK. For Stripe and the auth library, yes (audit, security). For Resend and R2, no (the helpers and the direct calls coexist).
- **The "I want to wrap it because consistency" smell.** A common request: "let's wrap Resend too, for symmetry." The lesson names the failure mode — symmetry isn't a goal, decision quality is. Every wrapper is a maintenance surface; every call-site that has to route through a wrapper rather than the SDK is an indirection the reader has to follow. Only the carve-outs that pay back the indirection earn the layer.
- **Class-based adapters — when the SDK is itself class-based.** Closes a thread from lesson 2 of chapter 009 (classes earn their weight in three sites, one of which is "adapters wrapping a class-based SDK"). Stripe's Node SDK exposes a class; the `billing.*` interface is one such adapter and the class instance is hidden inside `/lib/billing/stripe.ts`. The senior anchor: when the SDK is class-based, the wrapper hides the class instance behind functions. Restates briefly.
- **Versioning the wrapper — when Stripe changes.** Stripe pins API versions per account; the SDK accepts an `apiVersion` option. The `billing.*` interface is the natural place to pin the version, because every Stripe call goes through it. The senior reach: a wrapper centralizes the API-version-bump conversation — one place to update, one place to test. Names the payoff.
- **Observability — the seam for logs and metrics.** The wrapper is also the natural place for structured logs (every `billing.upgrade` call logs the org, plan, and resulting session ID) and metrics (every `billing.requirePlan` failure increments a counter). The third-party calls that don't go through a wrapper get observability at the helper or at the call site. The senior anchor: wrappers attract observability gravity; that's a feature, not a bug.
- **The rule, one more time.** Don't wrap unless: (a) the SDK shape harms the call site, (b) the swap cost is real, (c) a discipline must be centralized. Helpers for everything else. The principle is named once, applied twice, withheld three times — that's the entire architectural posture toward third-party SDKs in this course.
- **Watch-outs:** wrapping for symmetry adds indirection without payoff; not wrapping when the discipline needs central enforcement leaks the discipline across the codebase (the auth carve-out exists precisely to prevent this); confusing "helper" with "wrapper" (a helper that simply renames a single call isn't a wrapper, it's a code-style choice); wrapping with an interface that allows the SDK to escape (re-exporting the raw client from `billing.*`) defeats the seam; pinning the API version outside the wrapper makes the version a global env concern instead of a billing concern — keep it inside; over-mocking everything in tests because the wrapper makes it convenient hides real integration drift — the wrapper is for production gating, not test scaffolding.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The `billing.*` implementation details — lesson 6 of chapter 064.
- The `authedAction` implementation — lesson 2 of chapter 057.
- Resend usage and the `sendEmail` helper — Unit 7.
- Trigger.dev task primitives — Chapter 066.
- R2 and presigned URLs — Chapter 068.
- The full architectural principle list — surfaced at chapter-level in lesson 4 of chapter 043, audited in Unit 21.

Estimated student time: 30 to 40 minutes. The decision lesson — names the rule that explains the course's silence around wrapping everything else.
