# Chapter 062 — Invitations and the seat-handoff lifecycle (SaaS pattern #3)

## Chapter framing

Chapter 060 installed the org skeleton and Chapter 061 landed RBAC, the `authedAction` wrapper, and the audit log. Chapter 062 lands the third SaaS pattern in Unit 10 — how a new human becomes a `member` row. The student arrives with `requireOrgUser` returning `{ user, orgId, role }`, the `authedAction(role, schema, fn)` wrapper, the `tenantDb(orgId)` helper, the `logAudit(tx, event)` discipline, the Resend `lib/email.ts` send wrapper from Unit 8, and the Web Crypto primitives from lesson 1 of chapter 020 (`crypto.randomUUID`, `crypto.getRandomValues`, `crypto.subtle` for HMAC). They leave with an `invitation` table whose row is the *seat reservation* between "admin clicked invite" and "human accepted," a `sendInvitationAction` that mints a cryptographically random token, stores its hash, signs the accept URL with an HMAC, and sends a React Email through Resend, an `acceptInvitation` server flow that handles the four real-world arrival shapes (signed-in same-email, signed-in different-email, signed-out with account, signed-out without account), a management surface for pending invites (list, resend, revoke, re-invite collision), and a senior read on the edge cases that bite in production — expiry, ownership of the email address, the inviter who lost their seat before the invitee accepted.

Threads that run through every lesson. The invitation token is *cryptographically random and hashed at rest* — the raw token lives only in the URL the invitee receives; the database stores `sha256(token)`. The accept URL is *signed with an HMAC* so a tampered token rejects before the database lookup, and *time-bound* by an explicit expiry column the accept path enforces. Better Auth's organization plugin owns the `invitation` table shape and the `inviteMember` / `acceptInvitation` APIs — the senior reflex is to consume those primitives and add the auditing, the signed-URL discipline, and the edge-case handling at the call site, not to reinvent the table. Every invite-flow mutation goes through `authedAction('admin', ...)` and writes an `audit_logs` row for the canonical events (`'invitation.sent'`, `'invitation.accepted'`, `'invitation.revoked'`, `'invitation.resent'`). The email is sent *after the row is committed* (the row commits inside the action transaction; the send call happens outside it, with a deferred-send recovery story named once). Every edge case surfaces a senior trap: re-inviting an already-pending address, an invitee whose login email differs from the invited email, an inviter who was removed from the org before the invitee accepted, an expired token clicked three weeks later. Four teaching lessons plus a chapter quiz.

## Lesson 1 — The seat reservation that outlives the request

Model Better Auth's `invitation` table with `tokenHash` and `acceptedAt` additions, the `pending` -> `accepted` / `canceled` state machine, the seven-day expiry as a security primitive, and the partial unique index on `(orgId, lower(email))` where `status = 'pending'`.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** An admin types `bob@acme.com` and clicks invite. Bob doesn't exist in the system yet. The seat reservation has to outlive the request — Bob might accept in five minutes, in three days, or never. What does that row look like, what states does it pass through, how does the role chosen at invite time bind to the eventual `member` row, and when does the row get cleaned up? Land the data shape and the lifecycle the rest of the chapter writes against.
- **The shape — Better Auth's `invitation` table.** Columns the plugin owns: `id` (uuid pk), `organizationId` (uuid fk), `email` (text, the invited address), `role` (the role to grant on accept — `'owner'` excluded by invariant, only `'admin'` and `'member'`), `inviterId` (uuid fk to `user.id`), `status` (`'pending' | 'accepted' | 'canceled' | 'rejected'`), `expiresAt` (timestamp). The student adds two columns via `additionalFields`: `tokenHash` (text, the SHA-256 of the raw token), and `acceptedAt` (timestamp, nullable). The senior reflex: Better Auth's plugin generated columns are the canonical names; the two additions are the things the framework intentionally leaves to the application.
- **The role at invite time — the inviter chose it, accept is no-decision.** From lesson 1 of chapter 061: the invite carries the role. The accept flow writes `member.role = invitation.role`; it never re-prompts. The senior reflex: role choice is the *inviter's privileged decision*, captured at invite time so the accept screen stays trivial ("you're invited to Acme — accept"). The invite form disables the `'owner'` option; ownership transfer is its own flow (lesson 4 of chapter 061).
- **The status state machine.** `pending` (created, email sent) → `accepted` (invitee clicked, row written) | `canceled` (admin revoked) | `rejected` (invitee declined; rare in B2B, named once) | `expired` (computed from `expiresAt < now()` — *not* a stored status; the read filters on time, no background job mutates rows). The senior reflex: don't write a cron to mark invites expired — the read path's `where` clause is the source of truth, the rows accumulate harmlessly, and the retention job deletes ancient rows in batch.
- **The expiry decision — seven days as the default.** The senior call for year-1 SaaS: seven days. Long enough that "I'll get to it Monday" works, short enough that an old leaked email link doesn't grant access months later. Configured via Better Auth's `organization({ invitationExpiresIn: 60 * 60 * 24 * 7 })` (seconds). The senior reflex: the expiry is a *security primitive*, not a UX convenience; resending an expired invite mints a new row with a new token, never extends the old one.
- **The token shape — random, then hashed.** The token is 32 bytes of `crypto.getRandomValues` encoded as base64url (43 characters), generated inside the send action. The raw token goes into the invite URL; only `sha256(token)` lands in the database. The senior reflex: this protects pending invitations against a database read — an attacker with read access to `invitation` cannot forge an accept URL because the raw token isn't there. The pattern mirrors password hashing without the bcrypt cost: invites have a 7-day window and are throwaway, so SHA-256 (a fast hash) is appropriate; a slow hash like bcrypt would be cargo.
- **The `email` column is the invitation's identity, not the user.** The invite is bound to an email address, not to a `user.id`. The invitee may not have an account yet. The senior reflex: every database lookup on accept reads by `(tokenHash, status='pending', expiresAt > now())` — the email column is the audit/display value, not the join key. The accept flow's email-mismatch check (lesson 3 of chapter 062) reads `invitation.email` to confirm intent.
- **The unique-pending constraint.** At most one `pending` invitation per `(organizationId, email)` pair. Enforced as a partial unique index: `CREATE UNIQUE INDEX invitation_pending_unique ON invitation(organization_id, lower(email)) WHERE status = 'pending'`. The senior reflex: the partial index encodes the business rule — re-inviting the same address fails at the DB layer if a pending row exists, and the action surfaces that as the "already invited, resend?" branch (lesson 4 of chapter 062). The `lower(email)` normalizes case so `Bob@Acme.com` and `bob@acme.com` collide.
- **The relationship to `member`.** The two tables are *sequential*, not joined. On accept, the row in `invitation` transitions to `'accepted'` and a new row in `member` is inserted. The senior reflex: don't add a `memberId` FK on `invitation` — the invitation's job ends on acceptance; the member's job begins. The audit log is what links them ("invitation.accepted" carries the new `memberId` in payload).
- **What happens to the row after acceptance.** The row stays — historical accept records are part of the audit story. The status update is the only write; no DELETE. The senior reflex: an admin asks "did Bob ever get invited and when did he accept?" — the answer is in the table; rows are cheap, the answer is irreplaceable.
- **Retention for old invitations.** Accepted invitations stay forever (the audit log references them). Canceled and expired pending invitations accumulate; the senior call is a retention job (Chapter 17 territory) that deletes `canceled`/`expired` rows older than 90 days. Named here; built later.
- **What this is not — a seat-counting layer.** Some products gate invites on "you have N seats remaining, this invite consumes one." That's billing territory (Chapter 068 — `entitlements.canInviteMember(orgId)`). The schema in this chapter doesn't track seats; the entitlement check happens *before* the invite write in the action body. Named once.
- **Watch-outs.** Storing the raw token in the database — defeats the threat model, any DB read grants the invitation; using `crypto.randomUUID` for the token — UUID v4 is 122 bits of entropy, fine but not the senior reflex; `getRandomValues(new Uint8Array(32))` is the call shape; comparing `tokenHash` against a candidate with `===` — timing-safe compare is the discipline (lesson 1 of chapter 020 owns), even though SHA-256 lookups by index don't have the same exposure as HMAC verification; not indexing `(tokenHash)` — the accept path's lookup is by hash, the index is required; writing a status column with arbitrary strings — define a `text` column with a CHECK constraint or use a Drizzle enum so typos in seed data fail loudly; storing `email` without normalization — `bob@Acme.COM` and `bob@acme.com` are the same address per RFC 5321; lowercase before insert; relying on the plugin's default expiry without naming it explicitly in config — the value is a security primitive and should sit in a named constant; allowing `'owner'` as a value in `role` — the form excludes it but the schema/Zod should refuse it too, defense in depth; not adding the unique-pending partial index and discovering re-invite duplicates in production — the partial index is the structural fix.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The send action and the signed URL (lesson 2 of chapter 062).
- The accept flow and its arrival shapes (lesson 3 of chapter 062).
- Pending-invite listing, resend, revoke (lesson 4 of chapter 062).
- Edge cases around inviter removal, email mismatch (lesson 5 of chapter 062).
- Seat-counting / entitlement checks (Chapter 068).
- SCIM / IdP-driven provisioning (out of scope for year-1).
- Background jobs that mark invites expired (named once; not built — the read filter is the source of truth).

Estimated student time: 35 to 45 minutes. Setup/wiring with one decision thread — the table shape, the token hashing posture, the partial unique index. An ER fragment showing `invitation` next to `organization` and `member` carries the lesson; a small state diagram (`pending` → `accepted` / `canceled`, expired computed) closes it.

## Lesson 2 — Minting the signed accept link

Build `sendInvitationAction` with a 32-byte Web Crypto token, SHA-256 hash at rest, HMAC-signed accept URL, Resend dispatch after the DB transaction commits, and the `'invitation.sent'` audit event.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Admin enters `bob@acme.com`, picks `member`, clicks send. The next request has to mint a cryptographically random token, hash it, write a `pending` row, build an accept URL the invitee can click in their mailbox, sign that URL so a tampered token rejects fast, and dispatch a React Email through Resend — all without leaking the raw token to logs and without leaving the system in a half-state if the email send fails. What's the signed-URL shape, where does the HMAC come from, where does the email send sit relative to the database transaction, and which steps belong in the `authedAction` body?
- **The send action — `sendInvitationAction`.** `authedAction('admin', sendInvitationSchema, async ({ email, role }, ctx) => ...)`. The schema validates `email: z.string().email().toLowerCase()` and `role: z.enum(['admin', 'member'])` — `'owner'` excluded at the type level. The body: (1) check the entitlement (named, deferred to chapter 068), (2) check for an existing pending invitation (the re-invite branch lands in lesson 4 of chapter 062), (3) generate the token, (4) write the row, (5) sign the URL, (6) send the email, (7) log the audit row, (8) return `Result.ok({ invitationId })`.
- **The token generation — 32 bytes through Web Crypto.** `const raw = crypto.getRandomValues(new Uint8Array(32))`, base64url-encode for the URL (`Buffer.from(raw).toString('base64url')`). The raw value is computed once in memory; SHA-256 lands in `tokenHash`; the raw value goes into the URL and is discarded after the email send. Senior reflex: don't `console.log` the URL during development without redaction — the dev mailbox is also a real mailbox, but server logs are not the same trust boundary as an email inbox.
- **The signed accept URL — what HMAC adds on top of a random token.** The token alone is sufficient for authentication (32 bytes of entropy, lookup by hash). The HMAC signature adds a *fast pre-check* that rejects tampered URLs before the database round-trip. The URL shape: `${BASE_URL}/accept-invite?id=${invitationId}&token=${tokenRaw}&sig=${hmac(secret, invitationId + '.' + tokenRaw)}`. The accept handler verifies the signature first (constant-time compare via `crypto.subtle`), then looks up by `tokenHash`. The senior reflex: the signature isn't the primary auth — the random token is — but it's a cheap defense-in-depth that closes "the database is a pure key/value store; if someone reads `tokenHash` they can't forge a URL without the HMAC secret either."
- **The HMAC secret — where it lives.** A separate env var `INVITATION_SIGNING_SECRET` (32 bytes, base64-encoded). Distinct from the auth session secret (different rotation cadence, different blast radius). The senior reflex: never reuse one secret across two cryptographic uses — secret rotation gets tangled. The Vercel env entry is named, the local `.env.local` example carries a generated value.
- **The URL builder helper — `signedInviteUrl(invitationId, rawToken)`.** A pure function in `src/lib/invitations/url.ts` that takes the two inputs and returns the absolute URL. The HMAC uses `crypto.subtle.sign('HMAC', key, encoder.encode(invitationId + '.' + rawToken))`, base64url-encodes the signature, builds the URL. The senior reflex: one function, one place to read what's in the URL, one place to keep the signature shape in sync with the verify path.
- **The email send — React Email through `lib/email.ts`.** Unit 8 installed the `sendEmail({ to, subject, react })` wrapper. The invitation template is `<InviteEmail orgName inviterName acceptUrl expiresAt />` — a React Email component the student writes alongside the `WelcomeEmail` from lesson 4 of chapter 054. Senior reflex: the email body's accept URL is the *only place* the raw token appears outside server memory; the template doesn't log the URL in the dev preview server, and analytics never see it (no UTM tags on the accept URL — a tracker fetching the URL would consume the invitation).
- **The transaction boundary — DB write commits before the email send.** The `invitation` row commit and the audit-log write happen inside `withTenant(orgId, async (tx) => {...})`. The Resend send call happens *after* the transaction commits. The senior reflex: if Resend is down, the DB row already exists; the admin sees a `Result.error('email-send-failed', { invitationId })` with a "resend email" option from the pending-invites list (lesson 4 of chapter 062). The alternative — sending inside the transaction — leaves orphan emails referencing rows that rolled back, which is the worse failure mode.
- **The deferred-send recovery story — named once.** Year-2 reach: queue the send through Trigger.dev or similar (Chapter 14 territory), with retries and dead-letter on failure. Year-1 reach: synchronous send with the visible "resend" affordance. The senior reflex: the row is the source of truth; the email is a delivery; resend is cheap. Don't reach for a background queue in this chapter.
- **The audit-log write — `'invitation.sent'`.** Inside the transaction: `logAudit(tx, { action: 'invitation.sent', subjectType: 'invitation', subjectId: invitationId, payload: { email, role } })`. The senior reflex: the audit row records the *intent* (the admin invited Bob as member at this time); the email-delivery success/failure is a separate dimension surfaced in the UI, not in the audit log.
- **The unauthenticated dev convenience — the invite link in the UI.** In development, surface the accept URL in the admin's UI alongside the "email sent" toast (gated by `NODE_ENV !== 'production'`). The senior reflex: this short-circuits the local mailbox and accelerates testing. In production, never surface the URL in the inviter's UI — it's a credential the inviter shouldn't possess after send.
- **The inviter name on the email.** The template displays `inviterName` (the admin's name from `ctx.user.name`). Senior reach: not a security feature, a recognition feature — Bob recognizes "Alice invited you to Acme" faster than "You've been invited to Acme." The `org.name` and `org.logo` from the active org carry through too.
- **Rate-limiting consideration — named once.** Sending invites is a real abuse vector (an attacker who compromised an admin account can spam thousands of addresses; the org's domain ends up on a deliverability blocklist). Chapter 078 owns rate-limiting; the senior reflex here is to *name* the limit (something like "20 invites per admin per hour, configurable per plan") and apply it via the same middleware that gates other actions. Don't build it in this chapter.
- **Watch-outs.** Logging the raw token to the console or to Sentry — every error breadcrumb is a credential leak; redact the `token` and `sig` query params at the logger; comparing the HMAC signature with `===` on the accept side — timing leak; use `crypto.subtle.verify` or a constant-time compare (lesson 1 of chapter 020 owns); sending the email inside the DB transaction — orphan emails on rollback; building the URL with the route-handler's `request.url` host — fragile in preview deployments; read from `process.env.BASE_URL` (or `NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_URL`) named at one location; using `Math.random()` for the token — disqualifying weak entropy; using `crypto.randomUUID()` as the *only* token — 122 bits is fine for entropy, but the URL shape mixes the UUID with the org/invite namespace; the 32-byte random approach is the senior reflex; reusing the auth session secret as the HMAC key — secret-reuse coupling; reading `email` from `formData` without `.toLowerCase()` — the unique-pending index keys on `lower(email)`, the action input must match or duplicate-detection silently fails; missing the `revalidatePath('/settings/members')` after send so the admin's pending-invites list doesn't update; surfacing the invite URL in production logs even briefly — log filters should redact the `token` and `sig` params globally.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The accept flow on the other side of the email (lesson 3 of chapter 062).
- Managing pending invites — list, resend, revoke (lesson 4 of chapter 062).
- Edge cases — inviter removed, email mismatch (lesson 5 of chapter 062).
- Rate limiting (Chapter 078).
- Seat / entitlement gating (Chapter 068).
- Background-queued sends with retries (Chapter 14).
- Authoring React Email templates in depth (Chapter 053 owns).

Estimated student time: 50 to 60 minutes. Pattern lesson; the send action's eight steps and the signed-URL shape carry the lesson. A sequence diagram for "admin submits → action → DB commit → Resend send → audit row" earns its weight. Live coding exercise: write `signedInviteUrl` and its verify twin against a small fixture.

## Lesson 3 — Four arrival shapes on one accept URL

Route signed-in same-email, signed-in different-email, signed-out with account, and signed-out without account through one accept route with a verify order, an explicit Accept-button consent gate, auto-`emailVerified` for invite-sourced signups, and an active-org switch on accept.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Bob clicks the link in his mailbox. He might be signed in already (and his account email might match the invited email, or differ). He might be signed out with an existing account on the invited email. He might be signed out with no account at all. The single accept URL has to route all four shapes to the right next step, write the `member` row only after the right user is authenticated, and refuse silently when the token is tampered, expired, or already used. What's the route shape, what's the verification order, and what's the post-accept redirect for each branch?
- **The accept route — `/accept-invite/page.tsx`, not a Server Action by default.** The link in the email is a `GET` — clicking it must land somewhere renderable. The page is a Server Component that reads `searchParams` (`id`, `token`, `sig`), verifies them, looks up the invitation, and decides the next step. Senior reflex: the *verification* is server-side and idempotent (clicking the link twice doesn't double-accept); the *acceptance write* is a Server Action invoked from a button on the page so the human confirms ("Accept invite from Alice to join Acme").
- **The verify order — signature, then expiry, then status, then token hash.** (1) Verify the HMAC — bad signature → render "invalid invite link" (404-equivalent). (2) Look up by `id` — no row → same generic refusal. (3) Verify `tokenHash` matches `sha256(rawToken)` with a timing-safe compare — mismatch → same refusal. (4) Check `expiresAt > now()` — expired → render "this invite expired, ask for a new one." (5) Check `status === 'pending'` — `accepted` shows a friendly "you're already a member" page with the org link, `canceled` shows "this invite was revoked," `rejected` shows a recovery path. The senior reflex: the order matters — never reveal whether the `id` exists for a bad signature; the refusal screen is the same shape across signature failure, missing row, and hash mismatch.
- **The four arrival shapes — what the page renders.** (A) Signed in, session email matches `invitation.email` → render "Accept invitation to Acme as member" with an Accept button. (B) Signed in, session email differs from `invitation.email` → render the email-mismatch refusal (lesson 5 of chapter 062 owns the full posture). (C) Signed out, account exists on `invitation.email` → render a sign-in form prefilled with the invited email, returning to the same accept URL on success. (D) Signed out, no account on `invitation.email` → render a sign-up form prefilled with the email; on sign-up success, auto-accept and redirect to the org dashboard. The senior reflex: the page is one route, four UI states; the routing decision is server-side based on session + invitation row.
- **The accept Server Action — `acceptInvitationAction`.** Not wrapped in `authedAction` because the *authorization* is the invitation itself, not a role check. The action takes `{ invitationId, token }` from the form, re-verifies the token (the page's verification doesn't carry across the action call), checks `session.user.email` matches `invitation.email`, opens a transaction, writes the `member` row with `role = invitation.role`, updates `invitation.status = 'accepted'`, sets `acceptedAt = now()`, sets `session.activeOrganizationId = invitation.organizationId` (so the post-redirect lands in the right org), writes the audit row, commits, redirects to `/dashboard`. Senior reflex: re-verify on the action because the form submission is a separate request; never trust the page's pre-check as authorization for the write.
- **The session-after-accept reflex — switching active org.** Bob already had Personal as his active org. Accepting the Acme invite should *probably* switch his active org to Acme (he just clicked an Acme link; the intent is clear). The action calls `auth.api.setActiveOrganization({ headers, organizationId })` inside the transaction. Senior reflex: the alternative ("accepted, but you're still in Personal — switch manually") is a worse UX; the explicit switch makes the link's intent match the resulting state.
- **The signed-out signup path — auto-accept on first session.** Branch (D): the sign-up form returns to `/accept-invite?...`; after sign-up creates the user and the session, the page re-renders, now in branch (A), and offers the Accept button. The senior reach: skipping the button click in the auto-accept case is tempting but worse — the human should see "you're about to join Acme" once explicitly; the click is the consent signal. Year-1 default: render the Accept button even after sign-up, one extra click, clearer intent.
- **The email-verification interlock — signup-via-invite as verification.** A user who signs up via an invite link arrives with a server-confirmed email (the admin sent the invite to that address; receipt of the click confirms the address is reachable). Better Auth's `requireEmailVerification` would otherwise block sign-in. The senior reflex: set `emailVerified: true` on the `user` row inside the accept action's transaction when the sign-up path runs through invite — the invite *is* the verification step. Name the alternative (force the standard verification flow anyway) — the senior call is to honor the invite.
- **The token-already-used branch.** Status `'accepted'` on lookup: render "you're already a member of Acme" with a link to the org's dashboard. The senior reflex: a friendly idempotent response, not an error — the most common cause is the invitee clicked the link twice; the second click should still land them in the right place.
- **The audit-log write — `'invitation.accepted'`.** Inside the action transaction: `logAudit(tx, { action: 'invitation.accepted', actorUserId: ctx.user.id, subjectType: 'invitation', subjectId, payload: { newMemberId, role } })`. The senior reflex: the actor on this audit row is the *invitee*, not the inviter — the action represents Bob accepting, not Alice inviting (the invite audit row from lesson 2 of chapter 062 already records Alice's intent).
- **The post-accept redirect.** Always to a deterministic org-scoped URL — `/dashboard` (which the protected layout will resolve in Bob's now-active Acme context). The senior reflex: don't redirect to a `returnTo` query param on this flow — invite acceptance is its own intent, not a generic auth-return.
- **The generic-refusal screen — what it says.** "This invitation link isn't valid or has expired. Ask the person who invited you to send a new one." The senior reflex: one error string for signature failure, missing row, hash mismatch — they all map to the same recovery action from the user's perspective ("I need a fresh invite"). Differentiating "this was tampered with" from "this expired" leaks information without adding user value.
- **Watch-outs.** Auto-accepting on the GET (the page render itself writing the `member` row) — clicking the link in an email-scanning crawler or a corporate URL-rewriter would silently consume the invite; the explicit Accept button is the consent gate; trusting the email-mismatch branch to the UI (showing the wrong-email warning but still rendering the Accept button) — the action must refuse server-side; relying on the page's verification carrying across to the action without re-verifying — the action is a separate request and must do its own check; not switching the active org on accept — the redirect to `/dashboard` lands in the *previous* active org and the invitee sees no change; missing the auto-`emailVerified` for invite-sourced signups — the user gets a verification-required redirect after just confirming their email *through* the invite click, infinite loop; logging the `token` query param in the route handler's request log — same redaction discipline as lesson 2 of chapter 062; rendering the org name from `searchParams` (rather than the row) — XSS; row read is the only trustable source; using `auth.api.acceptInvitation` from Better Auth without writing the audit row alongside — the library's call doesn't know about your audit shape; either call the library inside `withTenant` and add the audit insert, or write the member-row + status-update + audit in one transaction yourself; not handling the race where two clicks open two tabs both accept — one transaction wins (the `status = 'pending'` precondition on the `UPDATE` filters), the other gets the "already a member" branch; redirecting to `/dashboard` before the session active-org write commits — refresh-the-page UX bug; commit then redirect.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The send side (lesson 2 of chapter 062).
- Listing, resending, revoking pending invites (lesson 4 of chapter 062).
- The "inviter removed before accept" edge (lesson 5 of chapter 062).
- Sign-in / sign-up flows in depth (Chapter 057 owns).
- Magic-link sign-in patterns (lesson 5 of chapter 057 owns — though the accept URL is shaped similarly, the lesson doesn't re-teach signed-link discipline).
- Multi-invite stacking (multiple pending invites visible after sign-up — named once; rare in year-1 product surface).

Estimated student time: 50 to 60 minutes. The most senior-mindset-heavy lesson in the chapter; the four arrival shapes are the load-bearing teaching. A decision tree diagram (`signed in? → email matches? → render Accept button` versus the three other branches) earns its weight. The wrong-then-right archetype lands well: show the naive "auto-accept on GET" version, then the explicit consent button with re-verify in the action.

## Lesson 4 — Pending invites: list, resend, revoke, collide

Build the admin's pending-invites surface with an expiry-filtered list, `resendInvitationAction` that rotates the token, `revokeInvitationAction` that sets `status = 'canceled'`, and a catch-and-translate of the unique-pending constraint into `'already-invited'`.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** Three invitations Alice sent last week are still pending. Bob never opened his; Carol opened hers but didn't accept; Dave's email was a typo. Alice opens `/settings/members` and needs to see the pending list with status, resend Bob's email, fix the typo by revoking Dave's and inviting the correct address, and confirm Carol's hasn't expired yet. What's the read surface, what are the three admin actions on a pending row, and how does re-inviting handle the unique-pending constraint?
- **The pending-invites list — read shape.** A Server Component on `/settings/members` (next to the member list from lesson 4 of chapter 061) queries `tenantDb(orgId).query.invitation.findMany({ where: and(eq(status, 'pending'), gt(expiresAt, now())), with: { inviter: true }, orderBy: desc(createdAt) })`. Columns rendered: email, role, inviter name, sent date, expiry countdown ("expires in 3 days"), action menu (Resend, Revoke). Senior reflex: filter expired *in the query* — they're operationally "no longer pending" even though the column is still `'pending'`. The retention job (named in lesson 1 of chapter 062) handles the cleanup.
- **The expired branch in the UI.** A separate "Recently expired" collapsible section, last 30 days, status `'pending'` but `expiresAt < now()`. Each row has a "Send new invite" button (which mints a fresh row with a fresh token via `sendInvitationAction`). The senior reflex: don't surface expired invites as still-actionable — the "send new" action is structurally a new send, not a magic re-extension of the old token.
- **Resending an invitation — `resendInvitationAction`.** `authedAction('admin', resendSchema, async ({ invitationId }, ctx) => ...)`. Two senior choices for the implementation: (1) re-send the existing token with its existing expiry (cheap, but the original might be lost in spam and the user has the same window remaining), or (2) rotate the token, push `expiresAt` to `now() + 7d`, send. The senior default is option 2 — rotation is the safer posture (the original token may have been leaked through a forwarded email and rotation invalidates it; the new expiry gives the invitee a fresh window). Inside the action: generate new token, update `tokenHash` and `expiresAt`, send email, log audit (`'invitation.resent'`). Rate-limited the same as send (Chapter 078).
- **Revoking an invitation — `revokeInvitationAction`.** `authedAction('admin', revokeSchema, async ({ invitationId }, ctx) => ...)`. Sets `status = 'canceled'`. Doesn't delete the row (audit history). The accept flow's status check rejects future clicks with "this invite was revoked." Audit event: `'invitation.revoked'` with the email in payload so a later audit search by address surfaces the canceled attempt. Senior reflex: revoke is *cheap*; one row update, one audit row, one revalidate; no email to the invitee (no "your invite was revoked" notification — the click reveals it organically).
- **Re-inviting an already-invited address — the collision UX.** Alice tries to invite `bob@acme.com` who already has a pending invite. The unique-pending index makes the INSERT fail; the action catches the constraint error and returns `Result.error('already-invited', { existingInvitationId })`. The UI surfaces "Bob already has a pending invite — resend or revoke?" with both actions in the toast. The senior reflex: the database is the source of truth for the collision; the action body doesn't pre-query because the race window between SELECT and INSERT allows duplicate sends; let the index enforce, catch and translate the error.
- **Changing the role on a pending invitation.** Alice realized she invited Bob as `member` but should have been `admin`. The senior call: don't add an `updateRole` action — revoke and re-send. The reasoning is the email already promised the original role; quietly mutating the row underneath would have the email say one thing and the accept screen another. Two clicks, one clearer mental model. Name the option (some products do allow it); the senior default is the simpler shape.
- **The "leave-org for a non-accepted invite" reflex.** The invitee hasn't accepted, so there's no `member` row to remove; "leave" doesn't apply. If the invitee wants to refuse, they click a "reject" affordance on the accept page that sets `status = 'rejected'`. The senior reflex: reject is rare enough in B2B that the year-1 product can skip the UI button and rely on revoke from the admin side; named here for completeness, not built.
- **The audit-row schema for these events.** `'invitation.resent'` payload `{ email, role, oldExpiresAt, newExpiresAt }`. `'invitation.revoked'` payload `{ email, role }`. `'invitation.rejected'` payload `{ email }` with `actorUserId = the invitee`. Senior reflex: the email lives in the payload so an audit search by address surfaces every event in the invite's lifecycle.
- **The `member.role` change versus the `invitation.role` choice.** From lesson 4 of chapter 061: after acceptance, admins change roles through `changeMemberRoleAction`. The invitation row's role is *historical* after acceptance — the audit log answers "what role were they invited as?" Senior reflex: don't try to keep `invitation.role` in sync with `member.role` after accept; they describe different moments.
- **The senior pattern — the pending-invite row is a tombstone for "what we promised."** Even after revoke or expiry, the row's payload says "we offered this person admin access on this date, with this expiry, sent by this user." That's the audit value. Senior reflex: never delete pending-invite rows in normal flows; the retention job is the only cleanup, and it runs on a long horizon.
- **Watch-outs.** Implementing resend as "re-fire the same email with the same URL" without rotating the token — the original token's window is unchanged, the user-visible expiry on the email is the same, the new email gives no actual new affordance; ignoring the partial-unique-index error and instead pre-checking with a SELECT — the race condition between SELECT and INSERT allows duplicate sends from rapid double-clicks; surfacing a generic "something went wrong" on collision instead of the actionable "resend or revoke" branch — wastes the constraint's signal; revoking by DELETE instead of `status = 'canceled'` — loses the audit trail; not running `revalidatePath('/settings/members')` after resend/revoke so the list looks stuck; allowing a `member` to view the pending-invites list — the read should require `roleAtLeast('admin')` per lesson 1 of chapter 061; sending a "your invite was canceled" email on revoke — privacy/abuse vector, and noisy for the invitee who never engaged; mixing the resend with a token-rotation in the *same* update without sending the email atomically — partial state (new token saved, old email still works) breaks the rotation's security claim; not handling the case where an admin tries to resend an *accepted* invitation — the action's status precondition must refuse.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The send and accept flows (Lessons lesson 2 of chapter 062 and lesson 3 of chapter 062).
- The edge cases — inviter removed, email mismatch (lesson 5 of chapter 062).
- Member-list management — role change, removal, leave-org (lesson 4 of chapter 061).
- Bulk invitations and CSV upload (out of scope for year-1).
- Notification-style updates to the invitee on revoke (named once; not built).
- Rate-limiting on the resend action (Chapter 078).

Estimated student time: 40 to 50 minutes. Mechanics with one decision thread (resend rotates the token by default). A small grid showing the three actions and their audit events earns its weight. Exercise: implement the catch-and-translate for the unique-pending constraint error so it surfaces as `'already-invited'`.

## Lesson 5 — Orphans, mismatches, and the double-click race

Make the senior calls on inviter-removed-before-accept (honor the invite), strict email-mismatch refusal, the double-click race against the `WHERE status='pending'` filter, and the already-a-member short-circuit.

Topics to cover:

- **The senior question.** The happy path is rare in production. Bob's invitation was sent by Alice three weeks ago; Alice was removed from Acme last week; Bob just clicked the link. Or: the link landed in Bob's personal mailbox but Bob's company-email account is what he uses to sign in; should he be able to accept against the wrong email? Or: two browser tabs, two simultaneous accepts; what wins? These aren't theoretical — they show up in support tickets in month two. What's the senior posture for each, and where does the check live?
- **The orphan-invite case — inviter removed before acceptance.** Alice invited Bob on day 1; Alice was removed from Acme on day 5; Bob clicks on day 6. The senior call: the invitation is *still valid*. The invite represents Acme's decision (the org granted Bob a seat), not Alice's personal sponsorship; Alice's removal doesn't retract Acme's offer. The accept flow proceeds; the audit log's `'invitation.sent'` row still names Alice as the actor, the `'invitation.accepted'` row names Bob. The UI on the accept page can omit "invited by Alice" if Alice is no longer a member (read `member.exists(inviterId, orgId)`), but the accept itself doesn't refuse. The product alternative — auto-revoke pending invites when an inviter is removed — is a defensible choice; the year-1 senior default is to honor the invite because the cost of "Bob can't join when he should be able to" exceeds the cost of "Bob joins after his inviter left."
- **The email-mismatch case — invitee's session email differs.** Alice invited `bob@acme.com`; Bob's session is signed in as `bob.personal@gmail.com`. The accept page detects the mismatch and refuses with: "This invitation was sent to bob@acme.com. Sign out and sign in as bob@acme.com to accept, or ask Alice to re-invite your current email." The senior reflex: never auto-link the invited email to the signed-in account — the invitation's identity is the email, and tying it to a different account silently is a real privilege-confusion risk (the wrong human accepts because they happened to forward the email). The strict refusal is the right shape.
- **The case-insensitivity nuance for email-mismatch.** Compare lower-cased on both sides — `Bob@Acme.com` matches `bob@acme.com`. The senior reflex: store the invitation's email lowercased (lesson 1 of chapter 062), normalize the session email when comparing, never display the lower-cased form to the user (display the original casing on email entities, compare on lower).
- **The expired-link click.** Bob clicks the link six weeks later. `expiresAt < now()` → render "this invite expired, ask for a new one." Senior reflex: don't offer a "request a new invite from Alice" button on this page — Bob's recovery is out-of-band (he emails Alice, Alice clicks resend). The product alternative — a self-serve "request to join" form — is a different feature (the "request to join" SaaS pattern, named once, not built here).
- **The token-tamper case — bad signature or bad token.** The accept page's verification rejects with the generic refusal. The senior reflex: same surface as expired — "this invitation link isn't valid or has expired" — never differentiate between expired, tampered, and revoked publicly. The product alternative (different copy for "revoked" so the invitee understands the rejection) is sometimes correct in regulated B2B; year-1 default is the unified refusal.
- **The double-click race.** Bob clicks Accept in two tabs simultaneously. Both Server Action invocations enter the transaction; the `UPDATE invitation SET status='accepted' WHERE id=? AND status='pending'` filter ensures only one update changes the status, the other reports 0 rows affected and the action surfaces "already accepted, you're a member" idempotently. The senior reflex: the WHERE-on-status is the optimistic-concurrency primitive for this flow; never assume the row is still pending at write time.
- **The same-user-already-a-member case.** Bob is already a member of Acme; Alice (forgetting) invites `bob@acme.com` again. The send action's body checks membership *before* writing the invite row: if `member` exists for `(orgId, lower(email))`, return `Result.error('already-member', { memberId })`. The UI surfaces "Bob is already a member as member" with a link to their row. Senior reflex: the cheap check belongs in the action body, not in the schema; the post-condition (no invite created) and the user-visible error (with the existing member's role) are the value.
- **The invitee accepting after the org was deleted.** Acme was deleted on day 5; Bob clicks on day 6. The invitation row was deleted by the org's CASCADE (or its status went to canceled via a delete hook). The accept page's row lookup returns nothing → generic refusal. Senior reflex: the cascade-delete on the org makes this case structural — the org's gone, the invite's gone with it; no orphan branch to handle.
- **The invitee accepting an invite for an org they own elsewhere.** Bob owns "Bob Consulting" (one of his orgs) and was invited to Acme as member. Acceptance writes a new `member` row in Acme; his ownership of Bob Consulting is untouched; the session's `activeOrganizationId` switches to Acme. Senior reflex: cross-org state is independent; the accept is a write in one tenant, not a multi-tenant operation.
- **The invitation for an admin who later got demoted to member.** Alice invited Bob while she was admin; Alice was demoted to member before Bob accepted. The invite carries `role`, not `inviterRole`; Bob still gets the role Alice chose (member or admin). Senior reflex: the invite is the *organization's* decision, snapshotted at send time; later changes to Alice's role don't propagate to the open invite.
- **The "two pending invites from different orgs to the same email" case.** Bob has pending invites to Acme and Beta. He signs up via Acme's link; only Acme's pending invite is consumed. Beta's pending invite stays `pending` and is visible on his post-signup dashboard as a pending action (the senior reach is a small "Pending invitations" widget on the dashboard). Year-1 default: don't build the widget; Beta's invite waits until Bob clicks Beta's email link or expires. Named once.
- **Watch-outs.** Auto-revoking pending invites on inviter removal — defensible but year-1 default is to honor; pick one and document; allowing a signed-in user with a mismatched email to "accept anyway" via a confirmation dialog — creates the privilege-confusion vector; never offer that affordance; comparing emails with `===` without lowercasing — Acme's casing breaks the mismatch check both ways; deleting `member` and `invitation` rows on org delete without ON DELETE CASCADE wired explicitly — orphan invites that nobody can revoke; pinging the inviter with "Bob accepted" emails without considering Alice may have been removed (NPE on the email send); fetching the inviter's email at audit-write time and joining at notification time, not at write time, so a removed inviter's notification quietly skips; not testing the accept flow with a deleted org — the row-lookup-returns-nothing branch is real; allowing the same email to hold a `member` row and a `pending` invite simultaneously — the post-accept transaction must check both directions; trusting the accept page's session.user.email cached in a React Server Component without re-reading inside the action — session can change between render and action; failing to handle the "Bob clicked the link but signed up with the wrong email at the prompt" UX — the post-signup re-render lands in the mismatch branch and Bob is confused; the senior reflex is to prefill the signup email from the invite and disable editing.

What this lesson does not cover:

- The send / accept / manage paths (Lessons lesson 2 of chapter 062 / lesson 3 of chapter 062 / lesson 4 of chapter 062).
- Multi-invite pending-on-signup widgets (named once; not built).
- Self-serve "request to join" flows (named once; not built).
- Auto-revoke-on-inviter-removal as a product option (named once; not built).
- The org-delete cascade in depth (Chapter 060 owns the cascade shape).
- Privacy / GDPR posture on invitee email retention (Chapter 085 owns).

Estimated student time: 35 to 45 minutes. Decision-heavy lesson; the load is the senior reasoning for each edge case, not the code. A table of "edge case → senior call → where the check lives" closes the lesson cleanly; one small sequence diagram for the double-click race against the `WHERE status='pending'` filter earns its weight.

## Lesson 6 — Quizz

Top 10 topics to quiz:

- The invitation table's identity column — bound to `email` (not `userId`) because the invitee may not have an account yet; the `(organizationId, lower(email))` partial unique index on `status = 'pending'` as the structural defense against duplicate pending invites.
- The token discipline — 32 bytes of `crypto.getRandomValues`, base64url-encoded in the URL, SHA-256 hashed at rest; raw token never lands in the database or in logs; the URL also carries an HMAC signature for fast pre-DB rejection of tampered links.
- The seven-day default expiry as a security primitive (not a UX convenience); resend rotates the token and pushes a new expiry; "extend the existing token" is not the senior reflex.
- The eight-step `sendInvitationAction` shape — entitlement check, collision check, token generation, DB row write (in a transaction with the audit insert), email send *after* commit, `'invitation.sent'` audit event.
- The four arrival shapes on the accept URL — signed-in same-email, signed-in different-email, signed-out with account, signed-out without account — and what each renders; the verify order (signature → row lookup → hash match → expiry → status); the single generic refusal for tamper / missing / expired.
- The explicit Accept-button consent gate — the GET doesn't write; the Server Action invoked from the button does, and re-verifies the token; the rationale (corporate URL scanners shouldn't consume invites).
- The auto-`emailVerified` for invite-sourced signups — the invite click *is* the email-ownership proof; setting `user.emailVerified = true` in the accept transaction prevents the verification-required loop.
- The active-org switch on accept — `setActiveOrganization` inside the accept transaction so the post-redirect lands in the joined org's context; the alternative ("accept but still in old org") is a worse UX.
- The orphan-invite senior call — when the inviter was removed before the invitee accepted, honor the invite (the org's decision survives the inviter's departure); the alternative (auto-revoke on inviter removal) is named once but not the year-1 default.
- The email-mismatch refusal — invited email vs. session email comparison is case-insensitive on lowercase, the refusal is strict (no "accept anyway" affordance); the senior reasoning is privilege-confusion through forwarded emails.
