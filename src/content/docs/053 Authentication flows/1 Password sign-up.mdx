Picture the simplest sign-up form there is: a field for a name, a field for an email, a field for a password, a button. Someone fills it in and presses the button. That single press is the whole subject of this lesson, and it hides four questions, each with a senior answer:

- **What's the call?** When the form submits, what code runs, and what does it return?
- **Where does the password go?** Plaintext is unthinkable — so what hashes it, and into which table does the result land?
- **What comes back if the email is already taken?** And — the question most people never ask — what happens to your security the moment you tell them "that email is taken"?
- **What does success even look like?** There's a twist here: a correct 2026 sign-up does *not* drop the user into the dashboard.

Last chapter you stood up Better Auth's `auth` instance — four tables migrated, cookies hardened, session helpers wired. But the instance is plumbed and inert: there's no flow a person can actually run. The pipes are in; this is the water turning on. By the end of this lesson you'll have a working email-and-password sign-up that hashes the password for you, writes exactly three rows, leaks nothing about which emails exist, and lands the user on a calm "check your inbox" page.

One thing this lesson deliberately does *not* do: send the actual email, mint the token, or handle the verify click. That's [Verifying email](/053-authentication-flows/3-verifying-email/). We stop the moment the verification email is *queued* — and you'll see that "queued, no session" state is exactly where a hardened sign-up is supposed to stop.

## Turning on the provider

The senior question for this section: what's the smallest config that makes sign-up exist at all — and which knobs does an experienced engineer move off the library's defaults before shipping?

Better Auth ships sign-up turned **off**. You opt in with an `emailAndPassword` block. This is an *addition* to the `betterAuth({ ... })` call you wrote last chapter, not a new file — you're dropping four lines into `lib/auth.ts` next to the adapter and `nextCookies()` that are already there.

The four knobs, each as *default → why we move it*:

<AnnotatedCode lang="ts" maxLines={6} code={`
emailAndPassword: {
  enabled: true,
  requireEmailVerification: true,
  autoSignIn: false,
  minPasswordLength: 12,
},
`}>
  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{2} "enabled"`} color="blue">
    **The on switch.** Nothing in this lesson exists without it — sign-up simply isn't a route Better Auth serves until this is `true`.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{3} "requireEmailVerification"`} color="violet">
    **The hinge.** This flips the meaning of a successful sign-up from "credential stored, **session issued**, you're in" to "credential stored, **verification email queued, no session**." Everything else in the lesson — the success page, the enumeration defense — falls out of this one boolean. Default is `false`.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{4} "autoSignIn"`} color="blue">
    Paired with the line above so the user never holds a session before their email is confirmed. Default is `true`, which would sign them straight in. The rule, plainly: `autoSignIn: true` together with `requireEmailVerification: true` is a **contradictory, broken state** — you'd be saying "don't trust them until they verify" and "log them in immediately" in the same breath. Verification on means auto-sign-in off.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{5} "minPasswordLength"`} color="blue">
    The library ships a floor of **8**; 12 is the 2026 senior floor. Length is the cheap structural minimum, not the whole strength story — entropy comes later in this lesson. (`maxPasswordLength` defaults to 128; you rarely touch it.)
  </AnnotatedStep>
</AnnotatedCode>

That's the entire on-ramp. Notice how little of it is "code" in the sense of logic — it's four decisions, and three of them are about *trust*, not syntax. That ratio is the lesson.

## The library owns the hash

The senior question here: where does the password actually go, what algorithm protects it — and is that a decision *you* have to make?

The answer is the most reassuring sentence in this lesson: **you don't hash anything.** You never see a hashing call, you never pick a cost parameter, you never write a salt. When `signUpEmail` runs, Better Auth hashes the password with <Term definition="A deliberately slow, memory-hard password-hashing function — native to Node, which is why Better Auth defaults to it. 'Memory-hard' means it's expensive to crack even on specialized hardware.">scrypt</Term> by default and writes the result to one column: `account.password`.

That column is on `account`, not `user` — and that's not a detail, it's the spine of the whole auth model you met last chapter: *your identity is one row; every way you can prove that identity is a separate row.* The password is one way to prove who you are, so it lives on an `account` row with `providerId: 'credential'`, sitting next to whatever other proofs (a Google login, a passkey) you might add later. Your `user` row never holds a secret. The per-password <Term definition="A per-password random value mixed in before hashing, so two people who pick the same password don't get the same stored hash. Handled for you by the library.">salt</Term> is handled for you too.

Here's the posture worth absorbing: the *value* of reaching for the library is that it encodes the hashing decision correctly so you can't get it wrong. Rolling your own auth is exactly where developers reliably ship `md5`, or `sha256` with no salt, or a string comparison that leaks timing — the classic credential-storage disasters. By using `emailAndPassword`, the right answer is the only answer available to you.

The override door does exist. You *can* swap the algorithm:

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts title="lib/auth.ts" "hash" "verify"
// only for a measured constraint — the scrypt default is the right call
emailAndPassword: { password: { hash, verify } },
```

</div>

But reaching for that door is a *measured* decision driven by a specific constraint — a compliance requirement that names Argon2id, a benchmark that says you need different cost parameters. It is never a day-one choice, and you don't need to know a single Argon2 cost parameter to ship a correct sign-up. The default is the right call until something concrete tells you otherwise.

## Writing the sign-up action

The senior question: the browser can call Better Auth's sign-up directly — so why wrap it in a Server Action at all, and what's the shape?

Start with the fact that there are *two faces* of the same operation, because Better Auth gives you one instance reachable two ways — a client face for the browser and a server face for your backend. They have the same job and **opposite failure shapes**, which is the whole reason the action exists.

<CodeVariants>
  <CodeVariant label="Client — returns { data, error }">
    ```ts
    const { data, error } = await authClient.signUp.email({
      name,
      email,
      password,
      callbackURL: '/dashboard',
    });

    if (error) {
      // error.code: 'USER_ALREADY_EXISTS', 'PASSWORD_TOO_SHORT', ...
    }
    ```
    **Failure is a value you branch on.** The client call *returns* a `{ data, error }` object; `error.code` carries the string. Familiar — but it runs in the browser, so there's no place to parse input on the server and no place to keep the library's error wording from reaching the UI.
  </CodeVariant>

  <CodeVariant label="Server — throws APIError">
    <div data-mark-color="blue">

    ```ts {3} "headers: await headers()"
    // inside our Server Action
    await auth.api.signUpEmail({
      body: { name, email, password },
      headers: await headers(),
    });
    ```

    </div>
    **Failure is a throw.** The server call does the *opposite* — on a genuine failure (a password under the floor, a malformed email) it throws an `APIError`, it does not return `{ error }`. The same codes live on `error.body.code`, with `error.status` like `'UNPROCESSABLE_ENTITY'` (422). Part of the action's job is to catch that throw and translate it into the course's `Result`. (A taken email is the interesting exception — it *doesn't* throw here, and the next section is all about why.)
  </CodeVariant>
</CodeVariants>

So: why the action and not a raw client call? Three reasons, all of them the action-boundary discipline you learned with Server Actions:

1. The form's input gets **parsed and validated on the server** — the client can't be trusted to have done it.
2. The library's **throw becomes a typed `Result`** the form can branch on without a `try/catch` of its own.
3. A library error message **never leaks to the UI** — the user sees copy you wrote, not Better Auth's internal wording.

A naive client-only call skips all three. Now the action itself. It follows the five-seam shape every Server Action in this course uses — `parse → authorize → mutate → revalidate → return` — and sign-up specializes a couple of the seams in instructive ways.

<AnnotatedCode lang="ts" maxLines={18} code={`
'use server';

const signUpSchema = z.object({
  name: z.string().min(1),
  email: z.string().trim().toLowerCase().pipe(z.email()),
  password: z.string().min(12),
});

export async function signUp(
  prevState: Result<{ email: string }> | null,
  formData: FormData,
): Promise<Result<{ email: string }>> {
  const parsed = signUpSchema.safeParse(Object.fromEntries(formData));
  if (!parsed.success) {
    return err('validation', 'Check the highlighted fields.', z.flattenError(parsed.error).fieldErrors);
  }

  const { name, email, password } = parsed.data;

  try {
    await auth.api.signUpEmail({ body: { name, email, password }, headers: await headers() });
  } catch (error) {
    return mapSignUpError(error);
  }

  return ok({ email });
}
`}>
  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{13} "safeParse"`} color="blue">
    **parse.** `Object.fromEntries(formData)` turns the form fields into an object, then `safeParse` runs `signUpSchema` (lines 3–7). The schema is the contract — `z.email()` is the top-level email builder, `min(12)` mirrors the server floor we configured. The load-bearing bit is the email line — its own step next.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{5} "trim" "toLowerCase"`} color="blue">
    **The email normalization** is a real watch-out, not decoration. Without it, `Ada@acme.com` and `ada@acme.com` are *different strings* and both slip past the unique index — one human, two accounts. Note the order: `.trim().toLowerCase()` runs **before** `.pipe(z.email())`, so the email is canonical *and* a stray trailing space won't fail the format check. Normalize first, validate second.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{14-16} "err"`} color="green">
    **parse failure.** On a bad form, return `err('validation', ...)` with `z.flattenError(parsed.error).fieldErrors` — the flat `Record<string, string[]>` projection the course's `Result` is shaped around. The form reads it per field.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{18}`}>
    **authorize — the empty seam.** Sign-up is the one action with *no caller to authorize* — it's a public endpoint, anyone may sign up. The seam is named so you know it was considered and deliberately left empty, not forgotten.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{20-24} "signUpEmail"`} color="orange">
    **mutate.** The single library call, wrapped in `try/catch` because the server API throws. `headers: await headers()` hands Better Auth the request headers (`headers()` is async in Next.js 16). There's no `db.transaction` here — the library owns the writes. **revalidate** is empty too: nothing cached changes on sign-up, so there's no list to refresh.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`{26} "ok"`} color="green">
    **return.** On success, `ok({ email })` — we pass the email back so the "check your inbox" page can echo the exact address. On a caught throw, `mapSignUpError(error)` turns it into a `Result` code. Which failures actually reach this `catch` — and, crucially, which one *doesn't* — is the subject of the very next section, where the enumeration trade lives.
  </AnnotatedStep>
</AnnotatedCode>

The form that *consumes* this `Result` — wiring it through `useActionState`, rendering field errors, showing pending state — is exactly the form shape you've already built. Don't re-plumb it here; the action's `Result` plugs into that wiring unchanged.

One more vocabulary note before the hard part. <Term definition="Better Auth's typed error, thrown by auth.api.* calls on a genuine failure. Carries body.code (a string like PASSWORD_TOO_SHORT) and status (like UNPROCESSABLE_ENTITY).">`APIError`</Term> is the thing your `catch` receives. The next section is about *which* failures actually arrive there — and the one case that, surprisingly, never does.

## Answering the same way whether the email exists

This is the decision at the heart of the lesson, so slow down with me here.

The senior question: a user types an email that's already registered. What does the response look like — and what happens to your security posture the *instant* you tell them "that email is taken"?

The threat has a name: <Term definition="An attack that abuses differing responses to learn which emails or usernames have accounts. The harvested list is fuel for the next two attacks.">user enumeration</Term>. Suppose your sign-up is friendly: a fresh email gets "check your inbox," a taken one gets "that email's already registered, sign in instead." You've built a free oracle. An attacker scripts your sign-up endpoint against a list of ten million emails and, purely from which message comes back, sorts them into "has an account here" and "doesn't." That sorted list is the raw material for <Term definition="Replaying leaked username/password pairs from other breaches against your login, betting people reuse passwords. A confirmed account list makes it far more efficient.">credential stuffing</Term> and for targeted phishing ("we noticed unusual activity on your AcmeCorp account…"). The leak isn't the breach — it's the thing that makes the breach cheap.

Now the satisfying part. Look back at section one: you already closed this leak, and you did it before you'd even heard the word "enumeration." With `requireEmailVerification: true` (equivalently, `autoSignIn: false`), Better Auth's public sign-up endpoint returns the **same 200 success whether the email is new or already taken**. Both paths queue a verification email. Neither issues a session. There's nothing for the attacker to distinguish — the responses are byte-for-byte the same shape.

That's two birds with one flag. The configuration that gives you the calm "check your inbox" UX is the *same* configuration that closes the enumeration hole. The structural defense and the nice user experience are not two features you wire separately — they're one decision, and you already made it.

Here's the part worth getting precise, because it's where the library does more for you than you might expect. The defense is closed *at the source*: with this config, `auth.api.signUpEmail` on a taken email **does not throw** — it returns the same generic success as a fresh sign-up. The library even hashes a password it will never store, so the *timing* of the two paths matches and can't be used as a side-channel. There is no `USER_ALREADY_EXISTS` for your `catch` to mishandle, because your `catch` never sees it. Your `mapSignUpError` is therefore only ever handed *genuinely-failing* sign-ups — `PASSWORD_TOO_SHORT`, `INVALID_EMAIL` — none of which say anything about whether an account exists. Map those to their own `Result` codes freely; there is no enumerating throw left to suppress.

That raises an honest question: if the taken-email path is silent, how does the *real* account owner ever find out someone tried to register with their address? Better Auth's answer is a dedicated seam, `onExistingUserSignUp` — a callback on `emailAndPassword` that fires **only** when this enumeration protection is active (i.e. `requireEmailVerification: true` or `autoSignIn: false`). It runs server-side, off the response path, so notifying the owner costs the attacker nothing observable:

<div data-mark-color="violet">

```ts title="lib/auth.ts" "onExistingUserSignUp"
emailAndPassword: {
  enabled: true,
  requireEmailVerification: true,
  autoSignIn: false,
  minPasswordLength: 12,
  onExistingUserSignUp: async ({ user }, request) => {
    // fires server-side on a taken-email sign-up — the response stays generic
    void sendEmail({
      to: user.email,
      subject: 'Did you try to sign up?',
      text: 'Someone used your email to sign up. If it was you, sign in instead.',
    });
  },
},
```

</div>

That's the senior shape: the *response* tells an attacker nothing, while the *real owner* gets a heads-up out of band. The leak is closed without losing the one person who genuinely needs to know.

<CodeVariants>
  <CodeVariant label="Leaks — don't">
    <div data-mark-color="red">

    ```ts {1-4}
    // a "helpful" pre-check bolted on before the signUpEmail call
    if (await emailAlreadyRegistered(email)) {
      return err('conflict', 'That email is already registered. Sign in instead.');
    }

    await auth.api.signUpEmail({ body: { name, email, password }, headers: await headers() });
    ```

    </div>
    **Reopens the leak you just closed.** The library already answers a taken email with a generic success — but the moment *you* add a check that lets the form render a distinct "already registered" message, you've rebuilt the oracle by hand. Tempting because it's friendlier; wrong because it's the exact tell the attacker scripts. If you want to warn the owner, that's `onExistingUserSignUp`, not a branch in your response.
  </CodeVariant>

  <CodeVariant label="Enumeration-safe — do">
    <div data-mark-color="green">

    ```ts {3-4}
    function mapSignUpError(error: unknown) {
      if (error instanceof APIError) {
        // a taken email never reaches here — the library returned ok instead
        if (error.body?.code === 'PASSWORD_TOO_SHORT') {
          return err('validation', 'Password is too short.', { password: ['Too short.'] });
        }
      }
      return err('internal', 'Something went wrong. Try again.');
    }
    ```

    </div>
    **No `USER_ALREADY_EXISTS` branch — because there's no such throw to catch.** Under `requireEmailVerification: true`, a taken email already returned a generic success, so `mapSignUpError` only ever sees genuine, non-enumerating failures (a short password). The comment is the kind production code keeps: it explains the *non-obvious security reason* a branch is absent.
  </CodeVariant>
</CodeVariants>

:::danger
The defense lives at *both* layers or neither. Even with the config correct, the form must not branch on a distinct "email-taken" state and render different copy — that re-opens the exact leak the config closed. Enumeration safety is a property of the *whole path*, from endpoint to pixels.
:::

There's a legitimate version of the friendly message — and naming it is the actual senior skill. Some products *do* choose to say "this email is already registered, sign in instead," because for low-value accounts the support tickets saved ("why won't it let me sign up?") outweigh the harvesting risk. That can be the right call. The rule isn't "never be friendly" — it's **decide it on purpose, with the enumeration cost on the table, and never sleepwalk into the leak because the friendlier copy felt nicer.** Default to opaque; trade it deliberately when the product genuinely warrants it.

## Keeping sign-up minimal: name, and the fields you defer

The senior question: the product owner wants `companyName`, `role`, and `timezone` collected at sign-up. Do they go on the form *now*?

Two fields are mandatory: `email` and `password`. One more is worth collecting at sign-up — `name` — because you'll address the person by it in the very first email. Everything beyond those three is an `additionalFields` decision, and Better Auth gives you a clean hook for it. Custom columns you declared on the `user` config last chapter become typed on `signUpEmail`'s `body` and on the session's `user`:

<div data-mark-color="green">

```ts title="lib/auth.ts" "input: true" "input: false"
// top-level user config — a sibling of emailAndPassword, not nested inside it
user: {
  additionalFields: {
    companyName: { type: 'string', required: false, input: true },
    role: { type: 'string', required: false, input: false },
  },
},
```

</div>

The load-bearing detail is the `input` flag, and it's a security boundary disguised as a config option. `input: true` exposes the field on sign-up's `body` — the user may set it themselves. `input: false` keeps the field server- and admin-only — it exists on the row, but no sign-up payload can write it. The canonical case is `role`: a user must **never** be able to set their own role at sign-up, or the first thing an attacker does is register as `admin`. `input: false` is the wall between "a field the form collects" and "a field the app assigns." Get that flag wrong on `role` and you've handed out admin.

So back to the product owner's three fields. The senior call is **keep sign-up minimal; defer everything else to onboarding.** Every extra required field at sign-up is friction at the single highest-drop-off moment in the whole funnel — the form a stranger meets before they've gotten any value from you. `name` stays. `companyName` and `timezone` are `input: true` but *collected later*, in an onboarding step after the account exists. `role` is `input: false` and assigned by the app, never the user. Read `additionalFields` as "here's the hook for *when* you need it" — paired immediately with "and the default is not to reach for it at sign-up." (One more thing it buys you: a declared field also lands typed on `getSession().user` — which, recall from last chapter, is why custom fields have to be *declared* to show up in the session cookie cache.)

## A password-strength meter, and the line you must not cross

The senior question: `minPasswordLength: 12` is the floor — how do you nudge users toward a genuinely *strong* password without ever trusting the browser to enforce it?

Length is the floor, and it's enforced on the server. <Term definition="A measure of unpredictability. A short, random password can be stronger than a long, guessable one — 'correct horse battery staple' beats 'P@ssw0rd123'. Entropy is what a strength meter estimates.">Entropy</Term> is the ceiling, and it's a *UX* concern — never a trust boundary. You can drop a client-side strength meter on the form (`zxcvbn-ts` is the usual pick) that scores the password as the user types and shows a live bar from "weak" to "strong." Great encouragement. But here's the hard line, and it's the same one running through every form you've built: **the meter's score is never sent to the server, and the server never trusts it.** The server's only password rule is `minPasswordLength`. Client meter = *encouragement*. Server `minPasswordLength` = *enforcement*. Client validation is UX; server validation is correctness — the meter is a textbook instance of that split.

For elevated-risk products there's a reach beyond length: a <Term definition="A range-query technique that checks a password against breach lists without ever sending the password itself — only a short hash prefix goes over the wire, and the service can't reconstruct the original.">k-anonymity</Term> check against **HaveIBeenPwned** that rejects passwords known to appear in public breaches. That's the senior reach when you handle money or admin surfaces — named here, not built.

That's the whole teaching of this section: the trust boundary, not the widget. The meter is a half-hour of UI; knowing it can never be load-bearing is the part that matters.

## The success state: check your inbox

The senior question: the action returned `ok` — but there's no session and no dashboard to send anyone to. So what does the user actually *see*?

Because `autoSignIn: false`, success is **not** a redirect to `/dashboard`. It's a "check your inbox" view: a line of confirmation copy echoing the email address they typed (that's the `email` you threaded through `ok({ email })`), and a **resend** button. Wire the resend to `authClient.sendVerificationEmail({ email })`. That endpoint is **rate-limited** by design, so the button can't be turned into a tool to flood someone's inbox — you'll wire the full rate-limit story in a later chapter, but it matters that the protection exists at this call site from day one.

The hard rule: **no session, no `/dashboard`, no protected content.** The session gate you built last chapter would bounce an unverified user out of the app anyway — but the success page isn't *relying* on that bounce, it's deliberately a dead-end-until-verified surface. There's nowhere to go from here except to your email. The click on the verification link that finally lifts this state — issues the session, opens the app — is [Verifying email](/053-authentication-flows/3-verifying-email/).

## The whole flow, end to end

You've met this feature in six pieces. Hold it now as one motion. Scrub through the sequence below — it's the entire sign-up from the button press to the inbox page, with what's true at each step written underneath. Pay particular attention to step five: the outcome is identical whether or not the email already existed, which is the enumeration defense made visible.
