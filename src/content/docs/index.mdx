This is a course about understanding the modern SaaS stack at senior depth: the decisions, the trade-offs, and the reasoning behind every tool you'd reach for in 2026. The emphasis is on judgment over keystrokes — by the end you'll be able to look at a SaaS feature, decide what to build and why, and defend those choices the way a senior engineer would.

## What you'll come away with

A working mental model of the entire production SaaS surface, and the senior judgment to navigate it. The course covers the full breadth — auth with email verification, organizations with roles and invitations, Stripe billing, transactional email, list views with filters and soft-delete, background jobs and file uploads, notifications, caching and rate limiting, internationalization, testing, observability, CI/CD with zero-downtime migrations, and an AI chat over your own data. That breadth is the point: by the end none of these are black boxes — you understand how each one works, when to reach for it, and what it costs.

## Is this course for you?

This isn't a first-week-of-coding course, and it isn't a backend veteran's frontend refresher — and that's good news, because it means we get to skip the parts you already know and spend our time where it actually counts. It's for you if you've shipped something before, you're returning to the web on a modern stack, and you want to work at senior depth.

One expectation worth setting now: this is a course about how to think, not a build-along bootcamp. You'll write code, but only the minimum practice needed to make each idea concrete. If you're after a follow-along where you type out a finished product, this probably isn't it — and it's better to know that up front.

## Why this one is different

You've probably worked through a tutorial that had you copy code without ever explaining why it was written that way. We flip that around: each lesson starts with the decision — what to build and why — and the code follows to show how it plays out.

Two ideas shape every lesson. The first is **systems design over syntax.** In 2026 the typing is the easy part; what really compounds is the thinking that comes before it — modeling your data, designing your API contracts, shaping code so it stays easy to change. So we lead with the decision, then show you the code.

The second is **a lean, real-world 2026 stack.** We use the smallest set of tools a real SaaS would actually ship with, and nothing more. Each one is either a sensible default or earns its place for a clear reason we'll name when we get there.

## Inside a lesson

Lessons are built to be worked through, not just read. Dense code arrives as **stepped walkthroughs** that highlight one section at a time and explain it, with hover definitions on the tricky terms and side-by-side tabs contrasting the wrong way with the right one. Concepts that are hard to picture — the event loop, a request moving through the router, a state machine — show up as **interactive diagrams you advance step by step**, so you watch cause and effect instead of squinting at a static image.

And you're kept active throughout: **predict-the-output drills, drag-to-sort and matching exercises, and in-browser editors that actually run and check your code** — even PR-style reviews where you leave comments on a diff — with a short quiz closing most chapters. Some of the coding exercises go a step further and give you **AI-written feedback** on your work. When someone has already explained a topic beautifully on **YouTube**, we embed it, with a note on how long it is and why it's worth your time. The aim of all of it is the same: keep you thinking, and make the reasoning stick.

:::note[Setting up AI feedback]
The AI feedback runs locally on your own machine through [Ollama](https://ollama.com) with the `qwen3.5:9b` model. Without it, those exercises still run and check your code — you just won't get personalized feedback.
:::

## How it all works

This is a big course — 108 chapters — and that scale buys depth: it covers the full surface of a production SaaS stack, end to end, with the reasoning behind every piece. Most of it is **teaching chapters** — they work through a set of ideas and close with a short, low-pressure quiz so you can check what stuck. Interspersed are shorter **project chapters**: focused, hands-on practice where you apply what you just learned on a real codebase. They're deliberately lean — just enough building to make a concept concrete, never busywork. Across them you do assemble a real app, but think of it as the vehicle for the ideas, not the destination.

The course reads front to back: the sidebar is your table of contents, and the next-page link at the bottom of each lesson keeps you on the path.

## How this course was made

A little honesty up front, because you deserve it: this course was written in May and June 2026 by Claude Opus 4.7 and 4.8, with human curation along the way. Because it's AI-generated, you may run into the occasional small error or inconsistency — if something looks off, trust your instincts, check it against the official docs, and let us know so we can fix it for the next person. We've also deliberately pinned everything to **May 2026**: the "best" tool always drifts over time, so whenever we tell you to reach for something, we'll explain the reasoning — so you'll know it's still the right call, and you'll spot the day it isn't.
