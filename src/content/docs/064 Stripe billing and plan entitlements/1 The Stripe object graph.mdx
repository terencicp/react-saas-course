In the last chapter you built the machinery that receives Stripe events safely: a verified, idempotent, single-writer webhook handler, backed by a `processed_events` ledger, with one firm rule — the webhook is the only thing allowed to write your app's entitlement state. That handler works. But right now it's a mail room with no idea what's in the envelopes. The events it accepts describe objects you haven't met yet.

So before you wire up a single payment, you need the map. Stripe's API is enormous — hundreds of endpoints, dozens of object types, an entire surface for marketplaces and connected accounts and physical card issuing that a subscription SaaS will never touch. The experienced move is not to learn all of it. It's to ask: which small handful of objects does a subscription product actually read, which does it write to (always indirectly, it turns out), and how do they connect to each other?

Here's the question made concrete. Picture the plan you're going to sell: a **Pro plan**, offered **monthly and yearly**, with a **14-day free trial**. That's one English sentence a product manager would say without thinking. By the end of this lesson you'll know exactly how that sentence decomposes into Stripe primitives — which part is a Product, which parts are Prices, where the trial lives — and you'll be able to open any Stripe event payload, recognize the four objects inside it, and know what your application should do with each one.

This is the dictionary every later lesson in this chapter quotes. Checkout, the Customer Portal, the entitlements row, subscription status, the billing interface — all of it refers back to four objects. Let's install them.

## The four objects at a glance

Almost everything that matters for a subscription SaaS lives in four Stripe objects and the edges between them: **Products, Prices, Customers, and Subscriptions**. Before we drill into any one of them, look at the whole shape. The relationships between these objects carry as much of the lesson as the objects themselves.

Read that graph as a single sentence and it tells the whole story: a **Customer** has a **Subscription**, which is **billed at** a **Price**, which belongs to a **Product**. Walk it the other way and you've answered "is this org on Pro?" — follow the org's Customer to its Subscription to its Price to its Product, and the Product is the plan. The two shapes worth burning in are these: a Product has many Prices (one-to-many), and a Customer has one Subscription that simply *references* a Price. The Subscription doesn't own the Price; it points at it.

One orientation before the detail. Your application mostly **reads** these objects — and even then, rarely by calling Stripe directly. It reads a small local copy instead (we'll build that copy in the lesson on plan entitlements). Your application **writes** to these objects only through Stripe-hosted flows: Checkout for starting a subscription, the Customer Portal for changing or cancelling one. And the way a change makes its way back into your app is the webhook you already built. Hold onto one rule from this paragraph: you never call `stripe.*` on the request hot path. We'll pay that off properly later, but plant the flag now.

## Products: the plan as a sellable thing

A <Term definition='In Stripe, the object that represents a thing you sell — a plan tier like "Pro" — carrying its name, description, and marketing copy, but no price of its own.'>Product</Term> is the thing you sell. "Pro plan." "Team plan." It carries the human-facing identity of the plan — a name, a description, marketing copy listing the features — and a stable `id`. What it conspicuously does *not* carry is a price. A Product is the answer to "what is this?", never "what does it cost?".

Here's the decision that trips people up, so let's settle it before anything else: **one Product per plan tier, not per billing cycle.** Pro is a single Product. Its monthly option and its yearly option are not two Products — they're two Prices that hang off the one Pro Product (the next section). Likewise, Team is its own single Product. So our running example is exactly two Products: Pro and Team.

Why insist on this? Imagine you modeled it the other way — a "Pro Monthly" Product and a separate "Pro Yearly" Product. Now the plan's identity is split across two objects. The question your whole application keeps asking — "is this org on Pro?" — becomes a two-value question, *"are they on Pro Monthly **or** Pro Yearly?"*, and every entitlement check, every feature gate, every analytics query has to remember both. Keep the tier as the Product and the cycle as the Price, and "are they on Pro?" stays a single, clean lookup. Tier is identity; cycle is pricing. Don't let pricing fragment identity.

You won't create Products by hand. They're defined in a seed script you run against your Stripe account, which we'll come to at the end of the lesson. For now, just hold the shape: Product equals plan tier.

## Prices: the billing cycle and the amount

If the Product is *what* you sell, the Price is *how it's billed*. A Price binds a Product to four things: a recurring **interval** (`month` or `year`), a **currency**, an **amount**, and a stable handle called a **`lookup_key`**.

The amount deserves a half-second of care. Stripe stores it as an integer in the currency's <Term definition="The indivisible unit a currency is denominated in — cents for USD, so $20.00 is stored as the integer 2000. Stripe stores money as integers in this unit to avoid floating-point rounding.">smallest currency unit</Term> — cents, for USD. So "$20.00 per month" is stored as `2000`, not `20.00`. This is the same "store cents, not dollars" rule you met all the way back when we first modeled money: integers don't have floating-point rounding errors, and a fraction of a cent is not a thing you can charge. Our Pro example becomes two Prices under the Pro Product: `pro_monthly` at `2000` ($20.00/month) and `pro_yearly` at, say, `20000` ($200.00/year).

Now the rule that earns this whole section. **Your code references a Price by its `lookup_key`, never by its raw `price_id`.**

A <Term definition={`A stable, author-chosen string handle for a Price — for example "pro_monthly". Unique within a Stripe account's active Prices and identical across test and live mode, so application code can resolve a Price by meaning instead of by a mode-specific ID.`}>lookup_key</Term> is a string *you* choose — `pro_monthly`, `pro_yearly` — and assign when you create the Price. The `price_id`, by contrast, is the `price_xxxxxxxxxxxxxx` identifier Stripe generates. Both identify a Price. The difference is what happens when you go to production, and it's the single most expensive Stripe mistake a beginner makes:

:::caution
Every Stripe account has two completely separate worlds — a test world and a live world (we'll get to the full discipline soon). The `price_id` generated in your test world is **different** from the one in your live world. So the moment you hardcode a `price_xxx` string and promote to production, that ID points at nothing and your checkout breaks. A `lookup_key` is one you author, and you assign the *same* key to the matching Price in both worlds — so it's identical everywhere. Reference Prices by `lookup_key`, and a test-to-live promotion is a non-event.
:::

So instead of carrying an opaque ID, your application asks Stripe "give me the Price whose lookup key is `pro_monthly`" and gets the Price back, mode and all.

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts "lookup_keys: ['pro_monthly']"
const { data } = await stripe.prices.list({
  lookup_keys: ['pro_monthly'],
  limit: 1,
});
```

</div>

That single line is the whole idea: the key is the query handle. The resolver that turns "plan `pro`, cycle `monthly`" into the right Price is the project's job to build in full; here, just see that the application never has to know a Stripe-generated ID to find the right Price.

## Customers: one billing account per organization

A <Term definition="In Stripe, the entity that owns subscriptions, payment methods, and invoice history — the account that gets billed. In this course there is exactly one Stripe Customer per organization.">Customer</Term> is the entity that gets billed. It owns the subscriptions, the saved payment methods, the invoice history, the billing address and tax IDs — everything that accumulates around an account that pays you money.

The course's pinned rule, and it's a decision you make once and never revisit: **one Stripe Customer per organization, never per user.**

You already have organizations as your tenancy unit — the boundary that owns members, roles, and invitations. Billing belongs to that same boundary, because the things billing accumulates are org-level facts, not personal ones. Seats are counted per org. Invoices are issued to the org. The tax ID and billing address are the org's. A single user is just one member who happens to be holding the credit card today; the *subscription* belongs to the organization.

Watch what breaks if you ignore this:

:::caution
Pin the Customer to the *user* instead of the org, and the moment you sell a team plan you're stuck: whose Customer holds the team's subscription? The founder's? What happens when they leave and hand off the org? You've also split the org's invoices across whichever individuals happened to pay, so finance can never get one clean billing history for the account. One Customer per org from day one sidesteps the entire mess — there's no migration that retrofits it cheaply later.
:::

Your application stores almost nothing of Stripe's Customer — just a pointer to it. That's a single column on the organizations table:

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts title="db/schema.ts" {4} "stripeCustomerId" "'stripe_customer_id'"
export const organizations = pgTable('organizations', {
  id: uuid().primaryKey().default(sql`uuidv7()`),
  // …existing org columns…
  stripeCustomerId: text('stripe_customer_id'), // nullable: no Customer until first checkout
});
```

</div>

The column is nullable on purpose: a brand-new org has no Stripe Customer yet. The Customer gets created the first time the org goes through Checkout — that lazy creation is the next lesson's job. All you're establishing here is the mapping (one org, one Customer) and where the pointer lives (this column).

There's a pointer in the other direction too, and you'll meet it shortly: Stripe lets you stamp `organization_id` onto the Customer itself, so that when a webhook arrives describing some Customer, your handler can read the org straight off the event. Hold that thought — it's the whole reason the metadata section exists.

## Subscriptions: the recurring relationship

The <Term definition="In Stripe, the object that joins a Customer to the Price they pay on a recurring basis — carrying a status, tracking the current billing period, and emitting a webhook on every state change.">Subscription</Term> is where it all comes alive. It's the object that joins a **Customer** to the **Price** they're paying, on a recurring basis. A Subscription carries three things you'll keep coming back to:

- A **status** — a string like `active` or `trialing` that says where this subscription is in its lifecycle. Note that it's a *string with meaning*, not a true/false. The full set of statuses and exactly how your app should treat each one is its own lesson; for now, just register that the field exists and that it carries more than a yes/no.
- The **current billing period** — when the paid interval started and when it ends. This drives every "your plan renews on…" and "your access ends on…" line in your billing UI.
- A connection to one or more <Term definition="The line inside a Subscription that pairs a Price with a quantity. The item — not the Subscription itself — carries the current billing period, so a Subscription can mix multiple billing intervals.">subscription items</Term> — each item pairs a Price with a quantity. In our Pro example, the org's Subscription has exactly **one** item, referencing `pro_monthly`, quantity 1, status `active`, renewing each month.

And — this is the through-line — the Subscription **emits a webhook on every state change**. That's what all those events your handler accepts in the last chapter were *about*. Stripe mutates the Subscription, fires an event, and your single-writer handler projects the new state into your app's row. That loop is the spine of this whole chapter: **Stripe changes the Subscription → fires an event → your webhook reads the new status, the plan, and the period off it → writes them to your database.**

Now a detail that will bite you if you learn it from an older tutorial, so learn it correctly here.

<Aside type="caution" title="The billing period moved onto the item">
Where does the billing period live? Until recently it sat right on the Subscription, as `current_period_start` / `current_period_end`. As of Stripe's `2025-03-31` API version those fields **moved off the Subscription and onto the subscription item** — you now read the period as `subscription.items.data[0].current_period_end`, not `subscription.current_period_end`. A lot of tutorials (and even some libraries) still reach for the old top-level field and quietly get `undefined`. The reason for the move is sound: because each item carries its own period, one Subscription can mix billing intervals. For the single-item SaaS default, you just read item zero. Throughout this course, the period lives on the item.

<PeriodOnItem />
</Aside>

So when you see <Term definition={`The timestamp at which the current paid interval ends and Stripe attempts the next charge — what drives "your plan renews/ends on…". As of Stripe's 2025-03-31 API version it lives on the subscription item, read as subscription.items.data[0].current_period_end.`}>current_period_end</Term> in this course, picture it on `items.data[0]`, never on the Subscription root. Same field, new home.

What about the default of one item, one subscription? **One active Subscription per Customer, with a single item, is the SaaS default** — and keeping it crisp is what lets every other lesson say "the org's subscription," singular, without hedging. Multiple subscriptions, or multiple items on one subscription, are real but advanced: a billing org with separate B2C and B2B lines, a workspace mixing a monthly and a yearly add-on, several workspaces invoiced together. Those earn a conversation the day you actually need them. Until then: one subscription, one item, read `items.data[0]`. We'll keep that assumption everywhere downstream.

## Metadata: the carry-channel back to your app

Here's a problem the four objects don't solve on their own. A webhook event lands — say, a Subscription just changed. The event hands you a Stripe Customer ID like `cus_xxxxxxxx`. But your handler needs to update *one specific org's row*. How does it get from a Stripe Customer ID to your `organization_id`? You could keep a lookup table mapping one to the other and query it on every event. Or you could have Stripe just *tell* you, right there in the event.

That's what metadata is for. <Term definition="Arbitrary key/value pairs you attach to a Stripe object. Stripe stores them and echoes them back on every webhook event for that object, so your handler can read app-specific values without a database round-trip.">Metadata</Term> is a bag of arbitrary key/value strings you can attach to most Stripe objects. Stripe stores them and — this is the magic part — **echoes them back on every webhook event** for that object. So anything you'll need *at the moment an event arrives*, you stash in metadata, and it rides along on the event. No database round-trip to discover it.

In this stack, exactly three handles earn their keep:

- **`Customer.metadata.organization_id`** — so a webhook describing a Customer maps straight back to your org, with no lookup table.
- **`Subscription.metadata.plan`** — the canonical plan slug (`pro`, `team`) stamped right on the Subscription, so the handler reads the plan directly instead of reverse-mapping from a Price back to a Product.
- **`Price.lookup_key`** — not metadata in the strict sense (it's a first-class field on the Price, as you saw), but the same *idea*: a stable, app-chosen handle so you find the Price by meaning rather than by a mode-specific ID. Metadata is the generic bag; `lookup_key` is the purpose-built version for Prices. Same goal, find-by-app-meaning.

The discipline to keep straight: **metadata is for what you need at webhook-receipt time and don't want to round-trip the database to learn.** It is not a general-purpose data store and not a stand-in for your own tables. If a value belongs in your database, put it in your database; metadata is the thin carry-channel for the few facts an incoming event needs to be actionable on arrival.

Stamping it is one line at Customer-creation time — this is illustrative; the real call, with lazy creation and storing the returned ID, is the Checkout lesson's job:

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts {3}
const customer = await stripe.customers.create({
  email: org.billingEmail,
  metadata: { organization_id: org.id },
});
```

</div>

And here's the payoff, tying it back to the handler you already built: when an event lands, `event.data.object.metadata.organization_id` is exactly how your single-writer handler knows *which org's row* to update. That one expression is why this section exists. (The Customer create call above is illustrative — the real one, with lazy creation, is the next lesson's.)

## What the app stores vs. what Stripe stores

Step back from the objects for a moment, because the most important decision in this whole chapter isn't about any single object — it's about a line. Stripe is the source of truth for billing facts. Your database is the source of truth for the small, *derived* slice of those facts your app reads on every request. Drawing that line wrong is how billing integrations rot.

<OwnershipLine />

The rule that governs that table: **don't mirror Stripe's schema — mirror only what your app reads on the hot path.** Every column you keep should exist because some request path needs it *without* calling Stripe. Your middleware checking "is this org on Pro and not past due?" runs on a huge fraction of requests — it cannot afford a network round-trip to Stripe each time, so the answer lives in your database. But Stripe's Customer has dozens of fields — tax IDs, shipping addresses, balance, currency, delinquency flags — and you read almost none of them on a request. So you don't copy them.

:::caution
The tempting mistake is to mirror the whole Stripe Subscription object into a table of your own, field for field. Don't. You'll double your schema work, and the copy goes stale the moment Stripe adds or renames a field — which they do. Derive the handful of values your app actually reads; let Stripe keep the rest.
:::

What does that derived slice look like? Just enough to answer "what can this org do, and until when":

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts "plan" "status" "currentPeriodEnd" "cancelAtPeriodEnd"
// The shape the entitlements lesson builds — a projection, not a copy:
{
  plan,                 // 'pro' | 'team' — from Subscription.metadata.plan
  status,               // 'active' | 'trialing' | ... — from Subscription.status
  currentPeriodEnd,     // from subscription.items.data[0].current_period_end
  cancelAtPeriodEnd,    // whether it lapses at period end
}
```

</div>

That's the destination, sketched — not the contract. The full table, column by column, plus how it gets written and read, is the entitlements lesson's job. Notice only that `currentPeriodEnd` is *derived from* `subscription.items.data[0].current_period_end` — the same item-level location from before, now showing up as the source of one projected column. Four or five fields, each present because a request reads it. That's the whole projection.

## The Stripe Node SDK: one client, server-only

Time for the one piece of real code in this lesson. Talking to Stripe from your application means the official `stripe` npm package — a class-based SDK you instantiate once with your secret key. This is the natural place for one of the carve-outs you met when we drew the module boundaries: an SDK adapter is exactly the kind of class that earns its weight, because you instantiate it a single time and then hide the instance behind a module so nothing else has to think about it.

That module is `lib/stripe.ts` — the same singleton you stood up in the last chapter to verify webhook signatures, now refined for the wider billing surface with two additions: a `server-only` guard and a pinned `apiVersion`. Every line in it is load-bearing.

<AnnotatedCode lang="ts" code={`
import 'server-only';
import Stripe from 'stripe';
import { env } from '@/env';

export const stripe = new Stripe(env.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY, {
  apiVersion: '2025-03-31.basil',
});
`}>
  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"'server-only'"`} color="blue">
    The `server-only` guard. Importing this package makes the module impossible to bundle into client code — if anything in the browser bundle ever imports `lib/stripe.ts`, the build fails loudly. That's the firewall that keeps your secret key off the client.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"env.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY"`} color="blue">
    The secret comes from `env`, your validated environment module — not raw `process.env`. A missing or malformed key is caught at build time, not at 2am in production.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"apiVersion: '2025-03-31.basil'"`} color="blue">
    The pinned API version. Pinning it explicitly makes any upgrade an intentional, reviewable bump rather than a drift under you — and it's exactly *why* field locations like the item-level `current_period_end` are version-dependent. The string itself rolls over time; the discipline of pinning is what matters.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"export const stripe"`} color="blue">
    One configured instance, exported once. This is the single place a `stripe.*` call originates in the entire codebase.
  </AnnotatedStep>
</AnnotatedCode>

Read that as a set of rules, not just code. The `server-only` import is a firewall — it makes it a *build error* for any client bundle to pull this module in, which is the one guarantee that keeps your secret key out of the browser. The secret itself comes from your validated env module, so a missing key fails the build instead of surfacing as a mysterious 500. The pinned `apiVersion` turns Stripe upgrades into deliberate, reviewable bumps — and it's the very reason field locations like the item-level `current_period_end` depend on which version you're on. And the export is a single configured instance: **this module is the one and only place a `stripe.*` call is allowed to originate.** Everything else in your app will go through a thin `billing.*` interface that we'll build later in this chapter — but the rule starts here.

## Test mode and live mode: two parallel universes

Several of the worst Stripe mistakes — hardcoded IDs, key mix-ups — are really the same mistake wearing different costumes: forgetting that every Stripe account is *two completely separate universes*.

There's a **test universe** and a **live universe**, and they share nothing. Test-mode keys, Customers, Subscriptions, Prices, webhooks — all of it is wholly disjoint from live. A Customer you create in test does not exist in live. The `price_id` your test Pro Price got is not the one your live Pro Price has. Objects never cross the boundary, and IDs differ across it — which, to close the loop from earlier, is *exactly why* you reference Prices by `lookup_key`: the key is the one handle you author identically in both worlds.

<TestVsLiveUniverses />

Which universe runs where is a convention, not a guess: **dev and CI run against test, staging runs against test, and only production runs against live.** The key is loaded from your validated env module and chosen per environment. The Stripe CLI, which you'll use to forward webhooks to your local machine, defaults to test mode unless you explicitly pass `--live` — a safe default that's hard to fumble.

Two keys are in play, and naming them apart matters:

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```bash title=".env" frame="code" /sk_test_/ /pk_test_/ /whsec_/
# server-only — authenticates SDK calls, never client-bundled
STRIPE_SECRET_KEY=sk_test_...        # sk_live_... in production

# safe to ship to the browser — used by Stripe.js to mount payment UI
STRIPE_PUBLISHABLE_KEY=pk_test_...   # pk_live_... in production

# verifies incoming webhook signatures
STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET=whsec_...
```

</div>

The prefix *is* the universe: `sk_test_`/`pk_test_` in dev, CI, and staging; `sk_live_`/`pk_live_` only in production. Because that prefix is machine-readable, a boot-time assertion can refuse to start the app when it doesn't match the environment — so a production deploy carrying an `sk_test_` key fails loudly instead of quietly charging no one (or, worse the other way, charging real cards from a test run).

The <Term definition="The server-only Stripe key that authenticates SDK calls. Prefixed sk_test_ in test mode and sk_live_ in live mode. Must never reach the client bundle.">STRIPE_SECRET_KEY</Term> authenticates your SDK calls and must never, ever reach the browser — it's the key that can charge cards and read every customer's data. The <Term definition="The client-side Stripe key, safe to ship to the browser, used by Stripe.js to mount payment UI. Prefixed pk_test_ in test mode and pk_live_ in live mode.">STRIPE_PUBLISHABLE_KEY</Term> is its public counterpart — safe to ship, used by Stripe's client-side library to render payment UI. Two watch-outs, both incident-grade:

:::danger
A secret key in your client bundle is the classic catastrophic Stripe leak — anyone who views source can charge cards and read your customers. The defense is structural: `STRIPE_SECRET_KEY` lives behind your env module's server/client split, so a client module physically cannot import it, while `STRIPE_PUBLISHABLE_KEY` is the only Stripe key the browser ever sees.
:::

:::caution
Mixing a test key and a live key in the same `.env` by name confusion is a real, recurring incident class — you think you're in test, you're charging real cards, or vice versa. Name the entries explicitly (test vs live is right there in the prefix) and add a boot-time check that the prefix matches the environment, so a mismatched key refuses to start the app instead of silently doing the wrong thing.
:::

## Pricing as code: the catalog seed script

One last convention before we put it together. You have two ways to get your Products and Prices into Stripe. You can click around the dashboard creating them by hand. Or you can define the catalog in code and apply it with a script — `pnpm seed:stripe` — that creates or updates the Products and Prices through the API.

The course default is **code as the source of truth for the catalog**, and the reasoning is the same single-source-of-truth principle that's run through this whole course: a catalog defined in code produces diffs you can review in a pull request, and it gives you test/live parity for free — you run the *same* seed against each universe, so your test Prices and live Prices are guaranteed to match, lookup keys and all. Dashboard clicks leave no diff, no review trail, and no guarantee that test mirrors live.

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```ts title="stripe/catalog.ts" "lookup_key: 'pro_monthly'" "lookup_key: 'pro_yearly'"
// The catalog, as reviewable code — one Product, its two Prices:
const catalog = [
  {
    product: { name: 'Pro' },
    prices: [
      { lookup_key: 'pro_monthly', unit_amount: 2000, interval: 'month' },  // $20.00/mo
      { lookup_key: 'pro_yearly', unit_amount: 20000, interval: 'year' },   // $200.00/yr
    ],
  },
];
```

</div>

That fragment is the entire point: your pricing lives in a file you can read, review, and diff — one Pro Product with its two Prices, keyed by `pro_monthly` and `pro_yearly`. The seed script upserts each entry (creating it the first time, updating it on later runs), and the same file applied against test and against live keeps the two universes in sync. The full script is the project's to ship; here you only need to adopt the convention.

## Putting it together

Let's walk the original sentence back through everything you now have. **"A Pro plan, offered monthly and yearly, with a 14-day trial"** decomposes like this:

The **Pro plan** is one **Product**. Its **monthly and yearly** options are two **Prices** under it — `pro_monthly` and `pro_yearly` — which your code finds by `lookup_key`, never by a Stripe-generated ID. The org that subscribes has one **Customer** (pointed at by `organizations.stripe_customer_id`, and stamped with `metadata.organization_id` so webhooks can find their way home). That Customer has one **Subscription** with a single item referencing the chosen Price — its `status` driven entirely by Stripe, its billing period tracked on the item. And every time Stripe touches that Subscription, it fires a webhook that your single-writer handler projects — the status, the plan, the item's `current_period_end` — into the entitlements row that the rest of your app actually reads. The 14-day trial? That's just a property on the Subscription, which the next lesson wires up.

That's the four-object model, the conventions that ride on top of it, and the seam between Stripe and your database — the whole vocabulary the rest of this chapter speaks. Two checks to lock it in.

Second, the load-bearing rule of the Prices section:

<MultipleChoice>
  Your app resolves Prices by `lookup_key`. What goes wrong the day you promote the *exact same code* from your test environment to production if you'd hardcoded a `price_id` instead?

  <McqChoice correct>Checkout breaks: the hardcoded ID was minted in the test universe, and the matching live Price has a different `price_id`, so the lookup resolves to nothing.</McqChoice>
  <McqChoice>Nothing breaks, but every Price lookup runs slower because resolving an opaque ID is more expensive than resolving a short string key.</McqChoice>
  <McqChoice>The SDK rejects the call at runtime — `stripe.prices.*` only accepts a `lookup_key`, never a raw `price_id`.</McqChoice>
  <McqChoice>The build fails because `price_id` is a deprecated field that Stripe removes in its newer API versions.</McqChoice>

  <McqWhy>Test and live are two disjoint universes, and a Price gets a *different* `price_id` in each. A hardcoded test ID points at nothing in live, so checkout silently breaks on promotion. A `lookup_key` is one you author and assign identically in both worlds, so the same code resolves the right Price everywhere — test-to-live becomes a non-event.</McqWhy>
</MultipleChoice>
