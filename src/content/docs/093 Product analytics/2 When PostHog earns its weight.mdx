The marketing site already has Vercel Web Analytics on it. Traffic is flowing, you can see which pages people land on, and Core Web Vitals are green. That floor shipped last lesson, and for a while it's the whole stack.

Then three asks land in the same week, from three different people:

- Product: "Did the new onboarding flow improve trial-to-paid conversion?"
- Engineering: "Let's roll the new billing UI out to 10% of orgs first, before everyone."
- Support: "Three users say the dashboard just broke this week. No console error, every log is a clean 200. What happened on their screens?"

None of them have noticed the catch yet: **not one of these questions is answerable from Vercel Web Analytics.** That floor counts visits and measures how fast pages load. It doesn't know *who* a given user is, *what* they did across several sessions, *which* version of a feature they saw, or *what their screen looked like* the moment it broke. Those are different questions, and they need a different tool.

This lesson doesn't wire that tool up — that's the next one. This lesson installs the *decision*: what the exact threshold is past the cookieless floor, which platform you reach for when you cross it, and why you reach for one platform instead of four. By the end you'll be able to name that threshold precisely and defend "PostHog, or not yet?" on a real product instead of cargo-culting it because every SaaS seems to have it.

## The four needs that cross the threshold

Last lesson framed the floor's edge as five signals: when you need to know *who* a user is, *what they did*, *across sessions*, *gated by a flag*, or *in replay*. That's the right instinct, but it's easier to act on if you collapse it into the four concrete product needs a team actually budgets for. The first two signals — "what they did" and "across sessions" — are really one need wearing two hats, so five becomes four. Each of these is a real line item on its own, and each maps to one of those three asks above (plus one more).

**1. Event-level analytics.** This is the one that answers *what users do across sessions.* Not "how many people visited" but "of the people who started onboarding, how many finished step two, and how many of those upgraded a week later." You record an <Term definition="A single thing a user did — signed_up, clicked_upgrade, created_invoice — recorded with a timestamp and properties.">event</Term> for each meaningful action, and attach properties to it — `plan: 'pro'`, `org_seats: 12` — so you can slice later. From those events you build a <Term definition="A sequence of steps you measure drop-off between, e.g. signup → activate → pay.">funnel</Term> to see where people leak out, group users into a <Term definition={"A group of users defined by shared properties or behavior, e.g. 'orgs on the pro plan'."}>cohort</Term>, and chart <Term definition="The share of users who come back over time.">retention</Term> to see who comes back. This is the need behind the product team's onboarding question.

**2. Feature flags.** A flag is a switch you flip from a dashboard, without a deploy, to decide *who sees what feature, when.* Roll a new feature out to 10% of orgs, kill a broken one instantly without shipping a revert, or turn something on only for users on the pro plan. Flags are also the substrate that A/B tests ride on top of. This is the need behind engineering's "10% of orgs first" ask.

**3. Session replay.** A privacy-masked, video-like playback of what a user actually did in the browser — every click, scroll, and form interaction, with sensitive content blanked out. It answers *what they did right before they bailed.* This is the tool for the UX bug that throws no error: the button that looks disabled, the modal that traps focus, the form that silently rejects valid input. Your logs are all 200s because nothing *crashed* — the user just got stuck. This is the need behind support's "the dashboard broke with no error" ask.

**4. Experiments.** A statistical A/B test with a primary metric and an automated call on whether the difference is real or noise. Not "I think variant B feels better" but "variant B lifted trial-to-paid by 4%, and here's the confidence it isn't random." It answers *did this change actually move the metric.* This is the disciplined version of the product team's onboarding question — the one that survives a skeptical review.

:::note
The decision rule, in one sentence: **name the four needs against your product; if zero of them are real, don't add PostHog yet.** The threshold isn't a vibe or a headcount — it's these four needs, and the question is always how many are genuinely true *today*, not someday.
:::

Let's make sure that boundary is a reflex and not just something you nodded along to. The hard part isn't the obvious cases — it's the borderline ones, where a question *sounds* like traffic but is really about identified behavior.

That last one — "which referrer sends the most signups?" — is the trap. Raw referrer counts are traffic data, so Vercel answers it. But the moment the question becomes "which referrer sends signups that turn into *paying* customers," you've crossed the line: now you're tying an anonymous referrer to an identified user's later conversion, across sessions, and only product analytics can stitch that together. The boundary isn't the word "signup" or "referrer" — it's whether the question can be answered without knowing who the user is and what they did over time.

## One platform, or four vendors

So you've got a real need — say events and flags, the two most products hit first. The naive next move is to go shopping, one tool per need. It's worth walking that path deliberately, because it looks reasonable right up until it isn't.

You'd pick an events tool (Mixpanel or Amplitude). Then a flags tool (LaunchDarkly or Statsig). When the replay need shows up, a replay tool (FullStory or Hotjar). When you start running experiments, an experiments tool (Statsig again, or Optimizely). Each one is best-in-class. Each one has a free tier. What could go wrong?

Four things, and they compound:

- **Four contracts** to evaluate, negotiate, and renew — four vendor relationships, four invoices, four security reviews.
- **Four SDKs** shipped into your client bundle. Every one is JavaScript the user's browser downloads and runs before your app feels fast. The performance floor you just measured with Speed Insights starts sinking under the weight of your own analytics.
- **Four dashboards** to context-switch between. The funnel lives in one tab, the flag lives in another, the replay in a third.
- And the one that quietly breaks everything: **four identity models to keep aligned.** The same user is a different ID in Mixpanel than in LaunchDarkly than in FullStory. So the question you actually want to ask — "the users who saw variant B, did they finish the funnel, and can I watch one of them struggle?" — spans three tools that don't share an identifier. Answering it means building and maintaining a join across three vendors' export formats. Nobody does. The question just doesn't get asked, and you paid four times for the privilege of not asking it.

That's the cost. Now the resolution.

PostHog folds those four needs into **one platform, one SDK, one identity, one dashboard.** The four primitives — events, flags, replay, experiments — all hang off the same identifier for a user. So "the users who saw variant B (a flag) completed the funnel (events) — let me watch one struggle (replay)" is a single query against a single dataset, not a four-vendor join nobody maintains. The contrast *is* the argument.

### When you'd actually pick something else

A decision is only credible if you can name what you turned down and the exact condition that would have changed your mind. PostHog being the course default doesn't mean the alternatives are bad — it means folding usually wins. Here's each credible competitor and the *one* trigger under which you'd reach for it instead:

- **Mixpanel / Amplitude** — the deepest event-analytics tooling there is. No flags, no replay. Reach for one of these when event analytics is your *only* need and that depth genuinely outweighs having everything in one place — a data-heavy team whose entire job is funnels and cohorts.
- **Statsig** — strong experiments and flags, weaker event UX, replay still maturing. Reach for it when experiments are the *load-bearing daily* primitive — the team's whole rhythm is "ship a test, read the result, ship the next test."
- **LaunchDarkly** — the flag specialist. No events, no replay. Reach for it when flag *scale* outgrows PostHog's flag UX: thousands of flags, complex targeting trees, enterprise governance and approval workflows.
- **Plausible / Fathom / June.so** — these are privacy-first *traffic* analytics, the same tier as Vercel Web Analytics. They are not product analytics, and they don't touch the four needs. Don't let the word "analytics" fool you into reaching for one of these to answer a funnel question.

The default is PostHog because the *typical* SaaS has two to four of these needs at once, and once you have more than one, folding them onto a shared identity wins decisively. The alternatives stay in your back pocket for the day one of their specific triggers fires.

## The cost shape, and why you don't ship all four at once

"Earns its weight" should have an actual number behind it, so let's make the economics concrete.

PostHog bills per ingested event, per replay session, and per <Term definition={"One evaluation of a flag for one user — 'is feature X on for this person right now?'."}>flag request</Term>, in volume tiers, and every primitive comes with a generous monthly free allowance that resets each month, no credit card. At the time of writing that's roughly **1M product-analytics events, 5k session replays, and 1M feature-flag requests per month** — comfortably more than a pre-PMF SaaS will touch. In practice it's effectively free until you find product-market fit, then it scales per-primitive as you grow.

:::caution
Those numbers move — PostHog adjusts the per-primitive tiers periodically. Treat the figures above as the shape ("generous free tier, billed per primitive"), not a contract. The pricing page is linked at the end of this lesson; check it for the current allowances before you commit a budget.
:::

The free tier being generous is exactly why the over-adoption trap is so easy to fall into. If it's free, why not turn on all four primitives the day you sign up? Because "free to ingest" isn't "free to maintain." Every primitive you enable is a thing someone has to instrument, watch, and reason about. An event taxonomy nobody reads still rots; replay you never watch is just disk you're not using. The discipline is to adopt incrementally:

- Pick the **two primitives that earn their weight right now** — for most products that's events plus flags.
- Add **replay when a specific bug-class demands it** — the no-error UX bug, the rage-click report. Not before.
- Reserve **experiments for the metric-led moment** — the first time someone asks "is this change real, statistically?" rather than "does this feel better?"

While you're wiring those primitives in the next few lessons, three quota-busting shapes are worth recognizing now so you don't write them in:

- A `capture()` call inside a React render — every re-render fires another event, and React re-renders a lot.
- A missing identity stitch — the same real action gets recorded twice, once anonymous and once identified, because PostHog couldn't tell they were the same person.
- Recording every anonymous session on a high-traffic B2C site — your replay quota evaporates on visitors who never even sign up.

Don't worry about the fixes yet; the wiring lessons show the guardrails for each. For now, just know the traps exist so they don't surprise you.

## PostHog Cloud EU, and the consent gate you'll wire next

Two decisions shape how you set PostHog up, and you have to make both *now* even though the wiring is the next lesson. Neither is code. Both are the kind of choice that's far cheaper to get right at the start than to undo later.

### Pick the region first: PostHog Cloud EU

PostHog Cloud comes in two regions. **EU Cloud runs in Frankfurt and disables IP capture by default for new projects**, which covers your <Term definition="The legal or contractual requirement that personal data is stored in a specific region, e.g. the EU.">data residency</Term> posture without a <Term definition="Data Processing Agreement — the contract a GDPR data controller signs with a processor; EU-region hosting reduces the friction.">DPA</Term> dance. **US Cloud** is the equivalent for teams whose users are mostly outside the EU. There's also a self-hosted, open-source PostHog — but operating it pulls in Postgres, Redis, Kafka, and <Term definition="The column-store database PostHog uses for analytics queries — fast for events, heavy to self-operate.">ClickHouse</Term>, and the on-call cost of running that stack outweighs the savings for any team under tens of millions of events a month.

The course default is **PostHog Cloud EU**. And here's the watch-out: picking US Cloud when your users are mostly in the EU is genuinely *harder to undo than to set up right* — moving regions later means a data migration and a fresh project. The region is a load-bearing first decision. If your users are in the EU, choose EU now.

### The consent gate is non-negotiable

This is the single most important constraint to carry into the next lesson, and it's where the contrast with Vercel Analytics matters most.

PostHog handles personal data — distinct IDs (its identifier for one user or browser, the key every event, flag eval, and replay hangs off), IP addresses, event properties that can carry user state. Under GDPR and ePrivacy, that makes it **non-essential**: nothing PostHog does is allowed to fire before the user actively accepts. You already built the machinery for this back when you shipped the cookie consent gate — the app has a single source of truth, `useConsent()`, that exposes booleans for each category. PostHog reads one of them:

```ts
const { analytics } = useConsent();
```

Every PostHog reach in this chapter routes through `useConsent().analytics`. When it's `false`, nothing loads — the SDK isn't even imported. When it flips to `true`, the SDK is dynamically imported and capture begins. As a final safety floor, the SDK is also initialized with capturing opted out by default, so even if the gate is somehow bypassed, nothing leaks. The next lesson wires all of this; here it's enough to hold the contract.

This is also a *budget* input, which loops back to the cost section: consented users are the only population PostHog ever sees. Your event volume is gated by your accept rate, not your total traffic.

Now the contrast you have to keep straight, because it's the most likely confusion between this chapter's two lessons:

:::caution
**Vercel Web Analytics is cookieless and does NOT pass through the consent gate. PostHog does. They do not share the rule.** Don't gate Vercel Analytics behind the banner by reflex just because PostHog is gated — you'd throw away traffic data for zero compliance benefit, since cookieless aggregate analytics doesn't need consent in the first place. Two tools, two rules: Vercel runs always, PostHog runs only on accept.
:::

One more boundary while we're naming complementary tools: PostHog does **not** replace Sentry. Sentry catches thrown errors — the exceptions, the stack traces, the things that crash. PostHog captures behavior and the bug-class that throws *no* error — the user who got stuck on a screen that worked perfectly as far as the code was concerned. You want both. They answer different halves of "something's wrong."

## A quick look at what the install pulls in

So the next lesson isn't a cold open, here's a preview of what you'll install. This is the *only* code in this lesson, and it's a manifest — a list of what's coming, not something to type yet:

```bash
pnpm add posthog-js posthog-node @posthog/next
```

- **`posthog-js`** — the browser SDK. It captures events, evaluates flags on the client, and records session replay.
- **`posthog-node`** — the server SDK. It captures events from server code and evaluates flags on the server, before a page even reaches the browser.
- **`@posthog/next`** — the official App Router wrapper that ties the two together with Next.js conventions: a server-component provider that bootstraps SSR-evaluated flags, distinct-ID cookie seeding in middleware, a built-in same-origin proxy, and automatic event flushing.

:::note
One honest caveat to carry forward: `@posthog/next` is real and official, but as of mid-2026 it's still pre-release, and PostHog itself still points teams at its standard Next.js setup guide for production. The next lesson reaches for `@posthog/next` where it can, and falls back to wiring `posthog-js` plus `posthog-node` by hand where stability matters. Don't treat the wrapper as a settled default yet.
:::

That's the whole manifest. The next lesson wires it end-to-end — through the consent gate, in the EU region — so the SDK only ever loads for users who've said yes.

Everything in this lesson collapses into a single procedure you can run on any future product. Walk it now, in the order an experienced engineer actually asks the questions — past the marketing-page stage first, then the four needs, then the verdict.

Look at where those branches land: every single "add PostHog" leaf converges on the same platform. That's the one-platform thesis restated as a picture. Reaching past the floor for *any one* of the four needs lands you on the platform that already covers all four — so you decide once, not four times, and the other three primitives are waiting for you when their moment comes.
