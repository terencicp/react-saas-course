You ship a modal. It sits at `z-50`, the dim backdrop behind it covers the page, everything looks right. Then the design adds a tooltip *inside* the modal, and the tooltip needs to float above the modal's content. Easy — the modal is `z-50`, so you give the tooltip `z-[100]`. You reload. The tooltip renders *behind* the backdrop. You're confused, so you bump it to `z-[9999]`, the number you reach for when you mean *absolutely on top of everything*. Still behind. You've now spent twenty minutes losing a fight to a number that, by every instinct you have, should obviously win.

Here's the reframe, and it's the whole lesson: `z-index` is not a global "bring to front" dial. It's a *local* ordering, scoped to something called a <Term definition="A self-contained group of elements that are stacked on the z-axis among themselves, then placed as a single unit inside the parent context. z-index only competes between members of the same context.">stacking context</Term>, and the reason `9999` loses is that your tooltip is trapped inside a context that — *as a whole* — sits below the thing it's fighting. No number you type on the child can lift it past that, because the number never leaves the box it's trapped in.

If that shape feels familiar, it should. In the lesson on position and inset utilities you met the containing-block surprise: an ancestor silently changed what your `top`/`left` offsets resolved *against*. This is the exact same move on a different axis — an ancestor silently changed what your `z-index` competes *against*. You already have the muscle for "an ancestor quietly rewrote the coordinate system." By the end of this lesson you'll own the stacking-context model, you'll find the trapping ancestor in seconds instead of an afternoon, and you'll reach for the right fix — portal it out, or scope it deliberately — instead of escalating numbers. And remember the `z-50` on that toast a couple of lessons back, the one we promised to explain later? This is later.

## Two facts before z-index makes sense

Before any of this is reasonable, two facts have to be solid. They're short, they're non-negotiable, and almost every "my z-index is broken" bug is really a failure of one of them. So we install them first.

### Fact 1 — z-index only bites on positioned elements

Set `z-index` on a plain block element and nothing happens. It is silently ignored. `z-index` only takes effect on an element that is *positioned* — `relative`, `absolute`, `fixed`, or `sticky` — plus, as a useful surprise, on flex and grid *items* even when they're otherwise static.

This is why, back in the position lesson, every floating thing you built paired a `position` value with its `z-*`. The `position` is what makes the `z-index` mean anything at all.

<div data-mark-color="green">

```html title="z-index needs a position" "relative"
<div class="z-50">Static — z-index is silently ignored, nothing moves.</div>

<div class="relative z-50">Now z-50 orders this above static siblings.</div>
```

</div>

That's the first sub-bug, and it's worth separating from everything else: "I set `z-index` and *literally nothing moved*" is almost always a missing `position`, and it is a different problem from the trap we're building toward. Rule it out first, every time.

### Fact 2 — every page is a tree of stacking contexts

Now the load-bearing idea. A stacking context is a self-contained group of elements: its members are sorted on the z-axis *among themselves*, and then the entire context is placed as a single unit inside its parent. Think of it like a sealed envelope. Inside the envelope, you can shuffle the pages into any order you like. But the envelope as a whole gets filed in one slot in the drawer — and shuffling the pages inside never changes which slot the envelope sits in.

The whole page starts with one root context, on the `<html>` element. Every other context nests inside it, forming a tree.

Here is the sentence the entire lesson hangs on: **`z-index` values only compete inside the same context.** A `z-100` living in one context and a `z-40` living in another never compare directly. What compares is the two *contexts'* own positions inside whatever parent they share. The number on the child is an internal detail of its envelope; it has no say in where the envelope gets filed.

That single rule is why `9999` lost. Hold onto it — we're about to make it visible.

A quick note on terminology before we go further. When something "stacks on top" of something else, what's literally happening is <Term definition="The order in which the browser paints elements onto the screen. Later-painted elements cover earlier ones. z-index sets paint order within a stacking context.">paint order</Term> — the browser paints the lower thing first, then paints the higher thing over it. "Higher on the z-axis" just means "painted later, so it covers." We'll use those interchangeably.

So the browser does a two-step sort, and naming it once makes every future bug legible. **Step one: inside each context, sort the members by z-index.** **Step two: treat each context as a single sealed unit and place it among its siblings in the parent.** The child's `z-[999]` won step one decisively — it's the frontmost thing *inside its parent*. But step two never looked at that number. Step two only saw the parent's envelope, which had no z-index of its own, sitting next to a sibling at `z-40`. The sibling won, and it took the whole envelope down with it. Every "bigger number loses" bug is this two-step sort, every time.

## Why your z-index "doesn't work": the trapped overlay

This is the section the whole lesson exists for. Now that you can see the scope rule, the canonical bug stops being mysterious and becomes a thing you can reason about line by line.

Here's the setup. A parent `<div>` gets `opacity-95` — a barely-perceptible fade, the kind a designer adds to soften a card, the kind you would *never* suspect of anything. Inside it sits a child with `relative z-50`. Next to the parent — as its sibling — sits a panel with `relative z-40`. You look at the numbers: 50 is bigger than 40, so the child should sit on top of the sibling. It doesn't. The sibling covers it.

Walk it against the two-step sort. That innocent `opacity-95` created a stacking context on the parent (yes — `opacity` less than `1` does that; we'll get to the full list next). So the child's `z-50` is now scoped *inside the parent's envelope*. The parent itself participates in the root context with no z-index of its own — call it `auto`, which sorts roughly at zero. The sibling at `z-40` is also in the root. Step two compares them: the parent's envelope at ~0 versus the sibling at 40. 40 wins, so the sibling paints over the *entire* parent envelope — and the child, trapped inside, goes down with it. The child's `z-50` was never in the race; it was busy winning a contest inside a sealed envelope that lost the contest that mattered.

The best way to believe this is to cause it yourself. In the playground below, drag the parent's opacity. At `1` the parent creates no context, so the child's `z-50` competes directly in the root and sits on top of the sibling, exactly as the numbers promise. Nudge opacity down to `0.99` — a change you can barely see — and watch the child snap *behind* the sibling. Nothing about the numbers changed. The only thing that changed is that the parent became an envelope.

Opacity is the most surprising trigger precisely because it reads as purely cosmetic; barely a visible change flips the entire stack.

One production gotcha worth flagging while the concept is fresh, because it produces a bug that looks like a flickering ghost.

:::caution
A `transition: opacity` fade — the kind you put on a dropdown so it eases in — *briefly* drops the element's opacity below `1` mid-animation. During those few hundred milliseconds, the element is a stacking context, and a z-indexed child can flicker *behind* a sibling for the duration of the fade, then snap back when the animation lands on `opacity: 1`. The fix is the same as the trap above: portal the floating element out, or pair the opacity fade with a `transform` (like `scale`) — `transform` already creates a context, so animating it adds no *new* trap.
:::

## What secretly creates a stacking context

You now understand the trap. The remaining skill is *recognition* — spotting the ancestor that created the envelope. The senior framing is the headline here: most of the triggers look purely cosmetic, and that's exactly why they bite. Nobody suspects a fade or a blur of breaking layering. So don't memorize twelve facts; learn the *shapes*, grouped.

**Always create one, no z-index needed.** `position: fixed` and `position: sticky` create a stacking context just by existing. This pays off a promise from the position lesson: remember the warning that a `transform` or `filter` on an ancestor can *capture* a `fixed` child, pinning it to the ancestor instead of the viewport? Now you know why — that ancestor created a context (and a containing block), and `fixed` can't escape it. Same root cause, two visible symptoms.

**Positioned with a real z-index.** `relative` or `absolute` *plus* a `z-index` that isn't `auto`. Note the trap inside the trap: bare `relative` does **not** create a context, but `relative z-0` **does** — `z-0` is a real z-index value, and it's not the same as no z-index at all. That distinction has cost people hours.

**The cosmetic villains.** These are the surprising ones: `opacity` less than `1`, any `transform` other than `none`, `filter` and `backdrop-filter`, `mix-blend-mode` other than `normal`, `contain: layout`/`paint`/`strict`, and `will-change` naming one of those properties. `opacity` is the headline villain because it hides inside fade transitions and reads as pure styling.

**The deliberate, side-effect-free one.** `isolation: isolate` (Tailwind's <Term definition="Tailwind utility for isolation: isolate. Creates a stacking context with no other visual effect — the clean way to deliberately scope layering.">`isolate`</Term> utility). This is the *good* trigger, the only one whose entire job is to create a context with zero visual change. It's the modern replacement for the old `transform: translateZ(0)` hack, and it's the hero of the next section.

**One-clause nods, don't dwell.** A container-query container (`container-type: size`/`inline-size`) also creates a context — you'll meet those in the next chapter, so it's named here just so it isn't a surprise then. And <Term definition="A browser-managed layer that renders above all page content, escaping every stacking context. Native dialogs, popovers, and fullscreen elements render here.">top-layer</Term> elements — native `<dialog>`, the `popover` attribute, fullscreen — escape *every* stacking context by rendering above the whole page. That last one is the deeper reason portaled and native popovers "just work," and it's the seed of the first fix.

Here's the takeaway to actually carry to work — the diagnostic reflex. When `z-index` "doesn't work," **do not bump the number.** Instead, walk *up* the DOM from the floating element and find the first ancestor with `opacity < 1`, a `transform`, a `filter`, `position: fixed`/`sticky`, or `isolate`. That ancestor is the trap. The whole skill is "stop typing bigger numbers, start walking up the tree." For the gnarly cases, Chrome DevTools has a **Layers** panel (Cmd+Shift+P, then "Show Layers") that visualizes <Term definition="A surface the browser composites separately on the GPU. Roughly — not exactly — corresponds to a stacking context. Useful as a debugging hint, not a precise model.">compositing layers</Term>, which roughly map to stacking contexts. It's a rough correspondence, not a perfect one, but it's enough to spot the trapping ancestor when reading the source isn't fast enough.

## Three ways out of the trap

The model converts into exactly three fixes. They're ordered by how often a 2026 developer reaches for each, and each comes with a one-line "reach for it when."

**1. Portal to `<body>` — the default for floating UI.** Move the floating element's DOM node out of the trapping subtree entirely and render it as a direct child of `<body>` (or a dedicated portal root). There, it lives in the root context and competes fairly with everything else. This is what a <Term definition="Rendering a component's DOM node somewhere else in the tree — typically a direct child of <body> — while keeping it logically inside its React parent. The standard fix for escaping a trapping stacking context.">portal</Term> does, and `createPortal` is React's API for it — you'll build one properly in a later chapter, so don't worry about the mechanics yet. What matters now is the recognition: **shadcn's Dialog, Popover, Tooltip, and Dropdown all portal to `<body>` for exactly this reason.** That's *why* those components float above everything without a z-index war — and it's what you'd reach for when you hand-roll a floating element. *Reach for it when:* the floating thing is a modal, popover, tooltip, dropdown, or toast — anything that should escape its layout entirely.

**2. `isolation: isolate` — deliberately scope a context.** Sometimes you *want* layering contained. A card with a decorative background layer behind it and a badge above it should keep all that stacking *to itself*, never leaking into the page or fighting a neighboring card. Drop `isolate` on the card and you've created a context on purpose, with zero visual side effects.

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```tsx title="Card.tsx" "isolate"
<article className="relative isolate rounded-xl border p-4">
  <div className="absolute inset-0 -z-10 bg-gradient-to-br from-blue-50 to-white" />
  <span className="absolute top-2 right-2 z-10 rounded bg-blue-600 px-2 text-white">New</span>
  <h3>Pro plan</h3>
</article>
```

</div>

This is the inverse of fix 1, and holding the pair together is the senior insight: a portal *escapes* a context; `isolate` *creates* one on purpose. *Reach for it when:* a component's internal layering (badges, decorative layers, overlapping elements) must stay sealed off from the rest of the page.

**3. Restructure the DOM.** The bluntest fix: lift the floating element above the trapping ancestor in source order, so it's no longer inside the envelope. *Reach for it when:* the trapping ancestor is incidental and the element is easy to move. *Don't* reach for it when the parent's `opacity` or `transform` is load-bearing — a fade animation you can't delete, a transformed card. You can't move the child out without moving it out of the thing that needs the child. In that case, portal it (fix 1) or isolate deliberately (fix 2).

And now the anti-pattern, stated bluntly because it's the single most common junior mistake and a reviewer will flag it on sight: **`z-9999` is not a fix.** A trap can't be defeated from inside it. Bumping the number reorders things *within* the trapped context and never lifts the context as a whole — you're rearranging pages inside the sealed envelope while the envelope stays in the wrong drawer. If you find yourself typing a four-digit z-index, stop: you've misdiagnosed the problem. Walk up the tree and find the envelope.

When you hit the bug in the wild, this decision tree is the reflex to run. Work through it as if you're staring at a real misbehaving element.

## A z-index scale your team can read

Here's a smell you'll find in real codebases: `z-12` here, `z-300` there, a `z-9999` someone added in a panic, all sprinkled across files by different people who never agreed on anything. Nobody can predict what's on top of what, and every new overlay starts another round of the number-bumping game. The senior deliverable isn't a clever number — it's a *convention*: a small, named tier scale that the whole team shares, so a z-index tells you what *kind* of thing an element is, not just an arbitrary height.

Tailwind's default scale is the convention. Adopt it as your tier set and use it on purpose:

<ZIndexTierScale />

That `z-50` row is the toast from the position lesson, finally explained: `z-50` was never magic, it's just "the overlay tier." And here's the reframe that ties the lesson together: once your floating UI is *portaled*, you barely need high z-index at all. Portaling moves the element into the root context, where plain DOM order does most of the work — the last-rendered overlay sits on top because it was painted last, not because it won a number race. A tidy tier scale plus portaling is the whole game. The number race is something you opt out of, not something you win.

One line on negative z-index. `-z-10` puts a child *behind its parent's own background* — the right reach for a decorative layer tucked under a card's content, exactly like the gradient in the `isolate` example earlier. Pair it with `isolate` on the parent so "behind the parent" doesn't accidentally mean "behind unrelated content on the page."

      <div className="absolute inset-0 z-40 rounded-xl bg-amber-200/80 p-3 text-sm font-medium text-amber-900">
        Panel
      </div>
      <span className="absolute top-3 left-3 z-50 rounded-md bg-blue-600 px-2 py-1 text-sm font-semibold text-white">
        New
      </span>
    </div>
  );
}`}
/>

If you got the badge on top with a small, deliberate change — and not by typing `z-[9999]` — you own this. That's the entire skill: find the envelope, then escape it, scope it, or move past it.
