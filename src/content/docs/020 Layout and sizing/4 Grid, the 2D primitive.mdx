In the last lesson you learned flexbox — the one-dimensional primitive. You give it a row or a column of items with unpredictable content widths, and its algorithm distributes the leftover space among them. That's the right tool for a nav bar, a toolbar, a form row, a stack of cards. One axis, variable content, the algorithm sorts out the slack.

Now picture a different shape: a product catalog. Three columns on a desktop, two on a tablet, one on a phone. Every card the same width as its neighbors. A consistent gap between them in both directions. Reach for the tool you already know — `flex flex-wrap gap-6` — and you'll get something that's *almost* right, and the "almost" is the kind that ships to production and then nags at you.

Flex items size to their content, so the columns come out ragged and that last lonely card refuses to line up. Flex never promised you equal columns — it promised to distribute leftover space along one axis, and it kept that promise. Equal columns and a clean two-dimensional structure were never its job.

The job belongs to grid. Here's the entire answer to the catalog, and we'll spend the rest of the lesson earning the right to read it fluently:

```tsx
<div className="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3 gap-6">
  {products.map((product) => (
    <ProductCard key={product.id} product={product} />
  ))}
</div>
```

By the end of this lesson that line will be obvious, and you'll have four more grid patterns in your pocket: the page shell that frames every dashboard (header, sidebar, main, footer), the one-liner that centers a hero on the screen, the dashboard with one tile bigger than the rest, and the card grid that reflows with no breakpoints at all. More than that, you'll be able to look at any layout and decide — flex or grid — on sight. That decision is the real payoff, and it's what makes this whole chapter's two-primitive split click into place.

:::note
You'll notice `md:` and `lg:` prefixes on that grid. Those are responsive variants — "two columns at tablet width and up, three at desktop width and up." The full breakpoint system gets its own treatment in the next chapter; for this lesson, just read `md:grid-cols-2` as "more columns once the screen is wide enough." The grid story is meaningless without them, so we're using them a chapter early.
:::

## A grid is a container of tracks

Flexbox gave you a frame worth keeping: set `display: flex` on a parent and it becomes a *container*; its direct children become *items* the algorithm arranges. Grid reuses that exact frame, and the symmetry is the fastest way in.

Set `display: grid` (Tailwind `grid`) on an element and it becomes a <Term definition="An element with display: grid (or inline-grid). It defines a set of rows and columns — the tracks — and its direct children become grid items that occupy cells.">grid container</Term>. Every direct child becomes a <Term definition="A direct child of a grid container. It occupies one or more cells of the grid, auto-placed unless it declares its own position. Grandchildren are not grid items.">grid item</Term> — same as flex, no per-child opt-in. But where a flex container only defines a single direction and lets content widths fall where they may, a grid container defines an explicit two-dimensional scaffold up front: a set of columns and a set of rows. Each column or row is a <Term definition="A single column or row of a grid. Defined by grid-template-columns / grid-template-rows. Tracks have a size; items occupy the cells they form.">track</Term>. Where a column track and a row track cross, they form a <Term definition="The intersection of one column track and one row track — a single box of the grid that an item can occupy.">cell</Term>. Items drop into cells, left to right and top to bottom, filling row by row — unless an item speaks up and asks for a specific spot.

That's the whole shift. Flex *distributes* space along one axis and lets the items size themselves. Grid *defines* a structure on two axes and the items snap into it. You design the grid; the content obeys.

The container defines its tracks with two properties: `grid-template-columns` and `grid-template-rows`. In Tailwind, the everyday form is `grid-cols-*` and `grid-rows-*`. Here's the simplest grid that does something useful — six cards, three equal columns, a gap between them:

```tsx
<div className="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4">
  <Card>One</Card>
  <Card>Two</Card>
  <Card>Three</Card>
  <Card>Four</Card>
  <Card>Five</Card>
  <Card>Six</Card>
</div>
```

`grid-cols-3` declares three column tracks. You never declared rows, so the grid creates them as needed: six cards into three columns means two rows, made automatically. The cards flow in source order — one, two, three across the first row, then four, five, six across the second — each landing in the next free cell. `gap-4` sets the space between tracks in both directions at once. No widths on the cards, no per-item rules; the structure lives entirely on the parent.

That figure shows what "auto-placement" means: with no instructions, item *n* lands in cell *n*. Most grids you write never need more than this — you declare the columns and let the items fall in.

Before the responsive tricks, it's worth knowing the menu of *sizes* a track can take, because everything later is a variation on it. A track's size can be:

- **A fixed length** — `200px`, `16rem`. The track is exactly that wide, full stop. The reach for a sidebar whose width is part of the design.
- **The `fr` unit** — a fraction of the leftover space. The workhorse for "this column grows with the container," and the next section is entirely about it.
- **A content keyword** — `auto` (size to the content), or the escape hatches `min-content` and `max-content` (the narrowest or widest the content can be), and `fit-content()`. You'll rarely write `min-content`/`max-content` in component layouts; they earn their keep mostly in data tables where a column should hug its longest value. Name them, move on.
- **`minmax(min, max)`** — a track that won't go below `min` or above `max`. This one is quietly everywhere, and you'll see why in a moment.

One more thing belongs here, not as a concept but as a reflex: when a grid item isn't where you expected, don't guess at utilities — open the overlay. Chrome's Elements panel puts a small `grid` badge next to any grid container; click it and Chrome draws the track lines straight onto the page. The Layout tab toggles line numbers, line names, and area names independently. Reading the overlay turns "why is this card in the wrong place" from a guessing game into a glance. It's the same instinct as the flex overlay from the last lesson: inspect the structure the browser actually built before you start changing classes.

## The fr unit and why grid-cols-3 already shrinks

Fixed-width columns are easy to picture. The interesting unit is `fr` — and it hides a detail that, once you see it, explains the single most common grid bug.

The <Term definition="fr (fraction) — a grid track unit equal to one share of the leftover space after fixed and content-sized tracks are subtracted. Three 1fr columns split the remaining width into thirds.">`fr` unit</Term> means *one fraction of the leftover space*. The grid first subtracts every fixed and content-sized track from the container's width, then splits whatever remains among the `fr` tracks in proportion to their numbers. Three `1fr` columns each take a third of the leftover. A `200px 1fr` pair gives a fixed 200-pixel sidebar and a main column that swallows everything else — the classic two-column app layout in a single track definition. Reuse the phrasing from the last lesson if it helps: `fr` is *leftover space as a layout tool*, the same idea flex's `flex-1` expressed in one dimension.

So `grid-cols-3` is three equal `1fr` columns, right? Almost — and the "almost" is the whole point. Here's what Tailwind's `grid-cols-3` actually compiles to:

```css
grid-template-columns: repeat(3, minmax(0, 1fr));
```

Not `repeat(3, 1fr)`. Three columns of `minmax(0, 1fr)`. Read that minmax: each column can be as small as `0` and as large as one fraction of the leftover space. That `0` lower bound is doing critical work, and to see it you need to know the default it's overriding.

A plain `1fr` track has an implicit minimum: it won't shrink below the *minimum content size* of what's inside it. Drop a long unbreakable string — a URL, an API key, a `font-mono` token — into a `1fr` column and the column refuses to go narrower than that string, even if that blows the whole grid wider than its container and forces a horizontal scrollbar. This is the exact same trap flexbox set in the last lesson, where a `flex-1` item wouldn't shrink past its content until you added `min-w-0`. Grid has the identical problem — and Tailwind's numeric `grid-cols-*` utility quietly fixes it for you by baking in the `minmax(0, …)` lower bound. The `0` says "you're allowed to shrink below your content," so the column clamps to its share of the space and the long string wraps or clips instead of bursting the layout.

The watch-out lives right here, in the rule it qualifies. The trap only bites when you bypass the utility and hand-write the track template:

```tsx
<div className="grid grid-cols-[repeat(3,1fr)] gap-4">
```

That bracket form is raw `repeat(3, 1fr)` with no `minmax`, so each column keeps its content-width floor and a single long word can overflow the grid on a narrow viewport. Plain `grid-cols-3` doesn't have this problem because it already wrote the `minmax(0, …)` for you. The lesson: the utility does the right thing; only the hand-written `1fr` form bites. ## Tailwind's grid utilities at a glance

Before the patterns, here's the compact map of what's available, grouped by the job each utility does. You don't need to memorize it — skim it once so the pattern sections read fluently, and come back to it as a lookup.

| Job | Utilities | What they do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Define tracks | `grid-cols-*`, `grid-rows-*` | Numeric (`grid-cols-3`) for equal `minmax(0,1fr)` tracks, or bracket form (`grid-cols-[200px_1fr]`) for explicit sizes. |
| Spacing | `gap-*`, `gap-x-*`, `gap-y-*` | One gutter between tracks, both axes at once — or split per axis. The spacing default inside any grid; no per-item margins. |
| Span tracks | `col-span-*`, `row-span-*`, `col-span-full` | Make one item cover several tracks (or the whole row). |
| Place by line | `col-start-*`, `col-end-*`, `row-start-*`, `row-end-*` | Pin an item to specific gridlines. |
| Implicit tracks | `auto-cols-*`, `auto-rows-*` | Size the rows/columns the grid creates automatically. |
| Auto-placement | `grid-flow-row`, `grid-flow-col`, `grid-flow-dense` | Direction the auto-placer fills cells; `dense` backfills gaps. |

Two notes worth carrying forward. `gap` is the only spacing tool you need inside a grid — it sets row and column gutters together, adds space *between* items without leading or trailing edge collisions, and reflows correctly when the item count changes. (The full case for why `gap` retired the old sibling-margin tricks is its own lesson later in this chapter; here, just take it as the default.) And `grid-flow-dense` carries the same accessibility caveat as flexbox's `*-reverse`: it reorders items *visually* to backfill gaps, but the DOM source order — and therefore keyboard tab order and screen-reader order — stays put. Reach for it only when visual order and reading order genuinely don't need to match.

## Responsive card grids without media queries

Back to the catalog. The intro's answer used breakpoint variants — `grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3` — to say "exactly one column on mobile, two on tablet, three on desktop." That's the right tool when the design specifies counts. But there's a second strategy that needs no breakpoints at all, and choosing between the two is itself a small design decision worth making deliberately.

The breakpoint-free form leans on a function you can read almost as a sentence:

```css
grid-template-columns: repeat(auto-fit, minmax(16rem, 1fr));
```

"Create as many equal columns as fit, each at least `16rem` wide, each growing to fill the leftover space." The grid measures its own width and decides the *column count itself* — four columns on a wide screen, two on a tablet, one on a phone — and it recomputes as the container resizes. No <Term definition="A defined screen-width threshold where the layout changes — Tailwind's md, lg, xl prefixes. The next chapter covers the full set.">breakpoints</Term>, no <Term definition="A CSS rule (@media) that applies styles only when a condition like a minimum viewport width is met. The mechanism behind responsive breakpoints.">media queries</Term>, no `md:` anything. In Tailwind it's the bracket form: `grid-cols-[repeat(auto-fit,minmax(16rem,1fr))]`.

Here are the two strategies side by side. The tabs aren't just two ways to write the same grid — they're a decision about *what the design actually specifies*:

One more knob lives inside that `auto-fit`, and it has a near-twin that trips people up: `auto-fit` versus `auto-fill`. Both create as many tracks as fit. The difference shows up when there aren't enough items to fill the last row. `auto-fit` *collapses* the empty trailing tracks to zero width, so the present items stretch to fill the whole row. `auto-fill` *keeps* those empty phantom tracks at their minimum width, so a half-empty last row leaves visible gaps where the missing cards would be. For a card grid you almost always want `auto-fit` — items filling the available width reads as intentional. Reach for `auto-fill` only in the rare case where you specifically want the empty slots reserved, like a fixed gallery whose blanks should stay blank. State it as: `auto-fit` is the default; `auto-fill` is the "I want the gaps held open" exception.

One forward pointer. `auto-fit` reacts to the *container's* width, but it's reacting to the viewport in practice because the container usually fills it. There's a component-scoped cousin — container queries (`@container`) — that lets a component respond to *its own* width regardless of the viewport, which is what you reach for when the same card component lives in both a wide main column and a narrow sidebar. That's a next-chapter topic; just file the name away.

## Page shells with named template areas

Every dashboard you've ever used is the same skeleton: a header across the top, a sidebar down one side, the main content filling the rest, a footer at the bottom. This is the <Term definition="The fixed chrome around the scrolling content of an app — typically header, sidebar, main, and footer. The skeleton nearly every dashboard shares.">app shell</Term>, and grid has a feature built almost exactly for it: named template areas.

The idea is that you draw the layout as a little map of names, and then each child says which named region it belongs to. The map reads like ASCII art — you can see the layout in the source. Here's the catch you need to know before the syntax makes sense: core Tailwind v4 ships *no* dedicated utility for grid areas. Plugins exist, but a from-scratch 2026 SaaS stack doesn't pull in a plugin for something the bracket form already does cleanly. So the canonical v4 approach is the arbitrary value — you write the CSS property name inside brackets, and Tailwind passes it straight through. Let me step you through the full shell:

<div className="grid h-dvh grid-cols-[200px_1fr] grid-rows-[auto_1fr_auto] gap-4 [grid-template-areas:'header_header''sidebar_main''footer_footer']">
  <header className="[grid-area:header]">Acme</header>
  <aside className="[grid-area:sidebar]">Nav</aside>
  <main className="[grid-area:main]">Dashboard</main>
  <footer className="[grid-area:footer]">© 2026</footer>
</div>
`}>
  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"grid-cols-[200px_1fr]" "grid-rows-[auto_1fr_auto]" "h-dvh"`} color="blue">
    First the tracks. `grid-cols-[200px_1fr]` is a fixed 200-pixel sidebar column and a main column that takes the rest. `grid-rows-[auto_1fr_auto]` is a header sized to its content, a body that grows to fill, and a footer sized to its content. `h-dvh` makes the shell as tall as the viewport so the footer sits at the bottom (the `dvh` unit gets its own treatment in the next lesson).
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"[grid-template-areas:'header_header''sidebar_main''footer_footer']"`} color="blue">
    Now the map. Read it as a 2×3 grid of names: row one is `header header` (the header spans both columns), row two is `sidebar main`, row three is `footer footer` (footer spans both). The template *is* the layout — you can see it at a glance.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"'header_header'" "'sidebar_main'" "'footer_footer'"`} color="orange">
    The underscores are spaces. Inside a Tailwind bracket value you can't type a literal space, so Tailwind reads `_` as a space — `'header_header'` is one row of two `header` cells. The row strings sit directly adjacent with no separator: `'header_header''sidebar_main'` is two rows, not one.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"[grid-area:header]" "[grid-area:sidebar]" "[grid-area:main]" "[grid-area:footer]"`} color="blue">
    Each child claims its region with `[grid-area:name]`. The names here must match the names in the template exactly, and the template has to be rectangular — every row the same number of cells. Get a name wrong and the child auto-places somewhere unexpected instead.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta={`"gap-4"`} color="blue">
    `gap-4` spaces every region from its neighbors — the same gutter tool, now between named areas.
  </AnnotatedStep>
</AnnotatedCode>

</TabbedContent>

Only the container changed between those tabs — its track template and its `grid-template-areas` string. Every child kept the exact `[grid-area:…]` it already had; not one of the four region elements was touched. That's the payoff of naming regions: the whole shell rearranges declaratively, by editing one string, and on a phone you'd wrap that one change in an `md:`-style variant.

Named areas are worth it when the two-dimensional arrangement genuinely earns a named template — a real sidebar-plus-header-plus-footer shell. For a simpler skeleton — just a header, a main, and a footer with no sidebar split — you often don't need areas at all. A plain multi-column grid with `col-span-full` on the header and footer (covered in a moment) reads cleaner and saves you the template string. As always, reach for the heavier tool only when the layout asks for it.

## Subgrid for cross-card alignment

Here's a subtle alignment problem that bites real card grids. Each card in a row has its own internal structure — a title, an image, a body, a footer button pinned at the bottom. The cards are equal *width* thanks to the grid. But the title on one card runs two lines while its neighbor's runs one, and now that card's image starts lower than its neighbor's, and the whole row goes out of step. The cards line up on the outside but their insides are ragged.

The fix is `subgrid`. Normally a card that's itself a grid defines its own independent rows. With `grid-rows-subgrid` (or `grid-cols-subgrid` for the column direction), the card *adopts its parent grid's tracks* instead of inventing its own. Every card's title row, image row, and body row lands on the *same shared lines* — so titles align with titles and images align with images across the entire row, no matter how long any individual card's content is.

The contrast is the whole lesson here, so look at it both ways:

    <SubgridBeforeAfter variant="without" />
    <Fragment slot="caption">
      Each card sizes its own rows. A longer title pushes the image, body, and button below it down — so the image tops stair-step and the buttons never line up across the row.
    </Fragment>
  </TabbedItem>
  <TabbedItem label="With subgrid">
    <SubgridBeforeAfter variant="with" />
    <Fragment slot="caption">
      `grid-rows-subgrid` makes each card adopt the parent's rows — titles align with titles, images with images, buttons with buttons, no matter how long any one title runs.
    </Fragment>

Two things make subgrid work, and missing either is the usual mistake. First, the card has to *span* the parent's relevant tracks — it's a grid item, so if the shared structure is four rows, the card spans four rows (e.g. `row-span-4`). Second, the card declares `grid-rows-subgrid` so its own children place onto those inherited lines instead of fresh ones. Span plus subgrid: the card borrows the parent's tracks and hands them to its children.

Safe to ship without a fallback.">Baseline</Term> — every current major browser supports it — so there's no fallback to write and no polyfill to pull in. ## Centering and aligning the whole grid

Grid has its own alignment vocabulary, and it's the two-axis cousin of the `justify-*` and `items-*` you learned on flex. There's one pair people reliably confuse, so we'll pin the distinction down with a picture. But first, the single most useful grid one-liner in CSS — the one you'll reach for to center anything:

```tsx
<div className="grid place-items-center min-h-dvh">
  <SignInCard />
</div>
```

That's a sign-in card dead-center on the screen, both axes, three utilities. `place-items-center` is the shorthand for `align-items: center` *and* `justify-items: center` together — it centers each item *within its cell*. `min-h-dvh` makes the grid at least as tall as the viewport (that viewport unit is the next lesson's territory; here it just means "fill the screen height"). One grid, one item, centered. This is the auth-hero and the empty-state reach, and it beats every flexbox centering incantation for sheer brevity.

Now the confusion. There are two *different* alignment questions in a grid, and they sound alike:

- **Where does each item sit *inside its cell*?** That's `place-items-*` (longhands `items-*` and `justify-items-*`). It moves the content around within the box the track gives it.
- **Where does the whole *track group* sit inside the container, when the tracks don't fill it?** That's `place-content-*` (longhands `content-*` and `justify-content-*`). If your three `200px` columns only use 700 pixels of a 1000-pixel container, `place-content-center` centers that whole block of tracks, leaving equal margins on the sides.

The difference is invisible until you see it, so here it is:

<Figure>
  <PlaceItemsVsPlaceContent />
  <Fragment slot="caption">
    Same oversized container on both sides. Left, `place-items-center`: the grid fills the container with three equal columns and rows, and each small item is centered *within its cell*. Right, `place-content-center`: the tracks are small and don't fill the container, so the whole block of tracks centers as a unit, leaving equal empty margin all around. `place-items-*` positions each item within its cell; `place-content-*` positions the whole track group within the container.
  </Fragment>
</Figure>

For the everyday case — full-screen centering of a single card — `grid place-items-center min-h-dvh` is all you need, and `place-content` is the tool you reach for in the rarer case where the tracks are smaller than their container. If you ever need to override the alignment of just *one* item, `place-self-*` does it for that item alone.

## Placing items across tracks

Auto-placement handles most grids. But sometimes one item needs to break the pattern — a featured dashboard tile twice the size of the rest, a header that should span every column. That's explicit placement, and it comes in two flavors: spanning and pinning.

**Spanning** is the one you'll use constantly. `col-span-2` makes an item occupy two column tracks; `row-span-2` makes it two rows tall; together they make a tile that's big in both directions. `col-span-full` is the clean way to make an item — a header, a footer, a full-width banner — stretch across *every* column of the grid regardless of how many there are. Here's a stats dashboard where the first tile is the hero:

```tsx
<div className="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4">
  <StatTile className="col-span-2 row-span-2">Revenue</StatTile>
  <StatTile>Users</StatTile>
  <StatTile>Churn</StatTile>
  <StatTile>MRR</StatTile>
  <StatTile>Trials</StatTile>
</div>
```

The Revenue tile takes a 2×2 block in the top-left; the four smaller tiles flow into the cells around it. One utility on one item, and the dashboard has a focal point.

A small watch-out: if you ask for `col-span-3` in a grid that only has two columns, the span *clamps* to the columns available — no overflow, but not the layout you drew either. Match your spans to your track count.

**Pinning** is the precise form, for when "span N" isn't enough and you need an item at exact coordinates. Grid lines are numbered from `1` at the start edge, and `col-start-2 col-end-4` places an item from line 2 to line 4 (covering columns 2 and 3). To use start/end fluently you have to picture the lines, which are *edges*, not tracks — an N-column grid has N+1 vertical lines:

<Figure>
  <GridLineNumbers />
  <Fragment slot="caption">
    Grid lines are numbered from 1 — an N-column grid has N+1 vertical lines. `col-start-2 col-end-4` spans the columns *between* lines 2 and 4, not lines 2 and 4 themselves.
  </Fragment>
</Figure>

There's one more form worth a name-check and no more: you can give your gridlines *names* in the track definition (the bracket form, e.g. `[grid-template-columns:[sidebar-start]_200px_[sidebar-end_main-start]_1fr_[main-end]]`) and then place items against those names. It's genuinely nice in a big shell where the line names match your layout language, but it's advanced and rarely worth the verbosity in component code. Know it exists; reach for span counts first, line numbers when you need precision, named lines only when a complex shell earns them.

## Flex or grid — the decision

This is the payoff the whole chapter has been building toward. You now have two layout primitives, and the experienced move isn't knowing each one — it's knowing *which one* on sight, before you write a single class. Here's the rule, stated cleanly:

- **Flex is one dimension.** A single row or a single column of variable-content items where you want the algorithm to distribute the leftover space. Navs, toolbars, button clusters, form rows, vertical stacks. One axis, content-sized items, the algorithm sorts the slack.
- **Grid is two dimensions.** A rows-and-columns structure where items snap to tracks: columns must be equal or exactly counted, sections must align across items, or you want the column *count* to respond to width. Card grids, page shells, dashboards.

And the synthesis that dissolves the "which is better" debate entirely: **most SaaS UIs are a grid of flex compositions.** Grid lays out the page regions and the card galleries — the big two-dimensional structure. Flex arranges the contents *inside* each region and each card — the nav bar in the header, the title-and-price row inside a product card, the footer buttons. They don't compete; they nest. Grid for the skeleton, flex for the flesh.
