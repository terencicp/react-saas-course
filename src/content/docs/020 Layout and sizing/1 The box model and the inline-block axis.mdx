You give a card a fixed width, add some padding, draw a border, and check the result: `w-64 p-4 border`. It renders exactly 256 pixels wide. Not 256 plus the padding, not 256 plus the border — exactly 256. If you ever wrote CSS before this course, that should feel slightly wrong, because the rule you half-remember says padding *adds* to width. This lesson installs why it doesn't: the four boxes every element is made of, the `border-box` model that makes widths compose by addition, the spacing scale that feeds every `p-*` and `m-*` you write, and the inline/block axis that gets you right-to-left layouts for free.

Most of this is cashing in a single line from *Preflight, the deliberately blank canvas* — the one where Tailwind set `box-sizing: border-box` on every element before you wrote a thing. You inherited that line; now you'll see what it bought you. By the end you'll be able to read any combination of `p-*`, `m-*`, and `border` and predict the rendered size in your head, and you'll reach for logical utilities like `ps-*` by reflex.

## The four boxes

Every element the browser lays out is not one rectangle but four, nested one inside the next. Learning their names — and which one your `width` actually targets — is the whole foundation, so let's name them from the inside out.

<BoxModelDiagram />

The **content box** is the innermost rectangle — where your text and child elements actually render. Under the legacy sizing model, this is the box your `width` and `height` target. Hold that thought; it's the source of the surprise we're about to undo.

The **padding box** wraps the content. Padding is *inside* space — breathing room between your content and the element's edge — and it takes on the element's background color. When you set `bg-white p-4`, the white fills the content *and* the padding.

The **border box** is the styled, visible edge. Every `border-*` utility draws here, on the ring between the padding and the outside world.

The **margin box** is the outermost band: transparent space *outside* the border that pushes neighboring elements away. Margin is the odd one out. It's the only box that doesn't take the background — it's always see-through — and it's the only one that participates in a quirk called margin collapse, which you'll meet shortly.

That last contrast is the one worth burning in, because it's the intuition every other rule hangs off: **background and border paint the content, padding, and border together; margin is always transparent and always outside.** That's exactly why padding *feels* like "space on the inside" and margin *feels* like "space on the outside" — the background stops at the border, and everything past it is margin.

## Why `w-64 p-4 border` is still 256px wide

Now the surprise from the top of the lesson, and the rule that resolves it. There are two ways a browser can interpret the `width` you set, and which one is active changes your arithmetic completely.

Under the old default, called `content-box`, `width` sizes *only the content box*. Padding and border are then added *on top* of it. So that same `w-64 p-4 border` card — 256px wide, 16px of padding on each side, a 1px border — would render at `256 + 32 + 2 = 290px`. You asked for 256 and got 290. This is the model that most stale tutorials assume, and — worth saying plainly — it's the model most AI-generated CSS still reaches for, because it learned from a decade of pages written before the better default won. If you paste in a snippet and an element comes out mysteriously too wide, this is the first thing to suspect.

Under `border-box` — your project's default, set for you by <Term definition="Tailwind's small built-in CSS reset, loaded automatically by the Tailwind import. Among other things, it sets box-sizing: border-box on every element.">Preflight</Term> — `width` sizes the *border box*. Content, padding, and border all fit *inside* the number you declared. `w-64 p-4 border` is 256px, full stop; the content box quietly shrinks to absorb the 32px of padding and the 2px of border. You name the outer size, and the browser does the subtraction.

That difference looks academic until you put it in production terms. Under `border-box`, **widths compose by addition** — and the most common layout bug in all of legacy CSS simply doesn't exist for you. Picture a child set to `w-full` (100% of its parent) with `p-6` of padding. Under `content-box`, that element is `100% + 48px` — wider than its parent, spilling out and triggering a horizontal scrollbar. Under `border-box`, it's exactly 100%, padding folded inside, no overflow. This is *why* `border-box` won and why every modern framework switched its global default to it.

Don't take the rule on faith — feel it. In the following playground, drag the padding slider up and watch the two readouts. Under `border-box`, the rendered outer width refuses to move past what you declared. Then flip the box-sizing toggle to `content-box` and drag padding again: now the rendered width climbs right past your declared number.

You'll notice the one thing you never did in any of this: write the `box-sizing` rule. You inherited it from Preflight, globally, on every element. That's the intended state, and it's worth protecting.

<Aside type="caution">
Never add your own global `* { box-sizing: content-box }` reset, or otherwise override Preflight's setting. It would silently revert every Tailwind width you've calculated this lesson — `w-full` would start overflowing again across the whole app. The only time you'll meet `content-box` in practice is when a third-party widget assumes it internally, which is rare, and even then it's scoped to that widget, not your project.
</Aside>

## The spacing scale feeds every box-model utility

So far the numbers in `p-4` and `m-2` have probably looked like arbitrary labels you memorize. They aren't. Every spacing utility in Tailwind is arithmetic over a single variable, and once you see the formula, the whole scale stops being magic.

`p-*`, `m-*`, `gap-*`, `space-*`, the inset utilities — all of them compile to `calc(var(--spacing) * n)`, where `n` is the number in the class name. In Tailwind v4 the default `--spacing` is `0.25rem`, which is 4px. So `p-4` is `calc(0.25rem * 4)` = `1rem` = 16px, and `p-2` is `0.5rem` = 8px. The number is a multiplier, not a pixel count.

That indirection is the point. Because every utility routes through one variable, you can rescale your entire spacing system by editing a single line in `@theme` — the same `@theme` block where you defined your color tokens in *Custom properties and the three-tier token model*. This is the spacing tier of that same design-token substrate.

```css
@theme {
  --spacing: 0.25rem;
}
```

Change that `0.25rem` to `0.2rem` and every `p-*`, `m-*`, and `gap-*` in the app tightens at once, in proportion, with no find-and-replace. That single source of truth is what keeps a UI feeling consistent. It also encodes a convention you'll see everywhere: the **4px grid**. Material, Carbon, Tailwind, shadcn — every major design system spaces things in multiples of 4. Staying on that grid is what makes a layout look intentional without anyone consciously deciding it does.

Tailwind does let you escape the scale with an arbitrary value like `p-[17px]` for a genuine one-off. Reach for it sparingly. A codebase peppered with `p-[17px]`, `mt-[13px]`, `gap-[5px]` is a codebase with no system — the experienced reflex is to stay on the scale, and if the scale genuinely doesn't fit your design, change the scale's base rather than litter magic numbers. Off-grid sizing is a smell, not a tool.

## Margin collapse is a smell, not a tool

Margin has one behavior that catches everyone the first time, and it's the reason this section exists at all. You're not going to learn margin collapse so you can use it — you're going to learn it so that when it bites you in someone else's CSS, you recognize the teeth marks instead of losing an afternoon. Read this as a thing to *spot*, never a thing to *reach for*.

The rule shows up in two shapes. The first is **adjacent siblings**: when two block elements stack vertically, the bottom margin of the upper one and the top margin of the lower one don't add — they *collapse* to the larger of the two. Put an `mb-8` element above an `mt-4` element and they sit 32px apart, not 48px. The browser keeps the bigger margin and throws the smaller one away.

<MarginCollapseDiagram />

The second shape is **parent and first or last child**: a block parent's top margin can collapse together with its first child's top margin (and its bottom with its last child's bottom). The child's margin appears to *escape* the parent and push the whole parent down instead — which is how you get a phantom gap above a section that you can't find by inspecting the parent, because the margin causing it lives on the child.

Two facts scope this trap tightly, and they're the facts that make the modern reflex work. Margin collapse happens **only between block-level elements in <Term definition="The default top-to-bottom, left-to-right layout of block and inline elements, before any flex, grid, or positioning changes how they're arranged.">normal flow</Term>, and only on the vertical (block) axis.** It never happens between flex items, it never happens between grid items, and it never happens horizontally. Ever.

That scoping *is* the senior move: don't fix margin collapse, sidestep it. The instant a parent is a flex or grid container, its children stop collapsing — and you space them with `gap` instead of margins, so there are no margins left to collapse in the first place. The reflex you'll build over this chapter is `flex flex-col gap-4`, not a stack of `mb-*` utilities. You get the full treatment of `gap` in a later lesson of this chapter; for now just register the trade: legacy code stacks blocks with margins and pays the collapse tax, your code uses `gap` and never sees it.

## The inline and block axis

Here's a quiet shift in how you'll name directions, and it's the one that earns you right-to-left support for free. So far you've thought in physical terms — left, right, top, bottom. CSS underneath thinks in *logical* terms, tied to how text actually flows, and matching that model is what makes a layout mirror correctly in Arabic or Hebrew without a second stylesheet.

CSS names two axes for the box model. The <Term definition="The axis text flows along — horizontal in English, the direction characters advance across the page.">inline axis</Term> is the direction text flows: in English that's left-to-right, so the inline axis runs horizontally. The <Term definition="The axis blocks stack along — vertical in English, perpendicular to the inline axis.">block axis</Term> is perpendicular — the direction block elements stack, which in English is top-to-bottom, so it runs vertically.

The reason these names exist instead of just "horizontal" and "vertical" is that they *follow the writing system*. In a <Term definition="Right-to-left — the writing direction of Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, and others, where text and layout flow from the right edge toward the left.">RTL</Term> language the inline axis runs right-to-left; in a vertical writing mode the axes rotate ninety degrees. "Inline start" means *wherever a line of text begins* — the left edge in English, the right edge in Arabic. The physical edge changes with the language; the logical concept doesn't.

That's exactly why CSS grew **logical properties**: `padding-inline-start` instead of `padding-left`, `margin-inline-end` instead of `margin-right`. They resolve against the writing direction, so when the document flips to `dir="rtl"`, a layout authored in logical properties mirrors itself automatically — your "start" padding moves from the left edge to the right with zero code changes.

Tailwind ships these as first-class utilities, and these are the ones you'll actually type:

- `ps-*` / `pe-*` — padding inline-start / inline-end (the logical twins of `pl-*` / `pr-*`).
- `ms-*` / `me-*` — margin inline-start / inline-end.
- `pbs-*` / `pbe-*` and `mbs-*` / `mbe-*` — the block-axis forms, for the rarer vertical-writing or block-direction cases. Worth knowing they exist; you'll reach for them seldom.

There's a logical *inset* family too — `inset-s-*`, `inset-e-*`, `inset-bs-*`, `inset-be-*` — that belongs to this same system and that replaced the older `start-*` / `end-*` shorthands. It's for positioned elements, so its behavior waits for the position-and-inset lesson later in this chapter; just file it under "same logical idea, applied to position."

To see why any of this is worth the slightly-less-familiar names, watch one single utility produce two mirrored results. The following tabs render the *same* box with the *same* `ps-8` class. The only thing that changes between them is the document's writing direction.

So the decision, stated as a default: **ship logical utilities from day one in any project that might ever be localized to a right-to-left language.** It costs you nothing over physical utilities and hands you RTL support the moment you flip a `dir` attribute. In a project that will only ever be English (or only ever LTR languages), physical utilities are perfectly fine and the later migration to logical is purely mechanical. This is exactly why the course's conventions read "logical properties (`ps-*`, `pe-*`, `inset-s-*`) over physical (`pl-*`, `pr-*`) so RTL works for free" — and now you know what the "for free" is buying.

## `mx-auto`, the one centering case where margin still earns its weight

Step back and look at how little margin you've actually needed. In 2026 you write `padding` constantly (space inside an element) and `gap` constantly (space between siblings), and `margin` almost never. The conventions go further and forbid sibling margins outright — that's `gap`'s job, and you just saw why margins between siblings invite collapse anyway. So it's fair to ask: when does margin still earn a spot in your code at all?

The durable answer is centering one specific kind of element. `mx-auto` — which compiles to `margin-inline: auto` — centers a block element that has a defined width inside its parent. The mechanism is worth understanding rather than memorizing: when both left and right margins are `auto`, the browser takes whatever horizontal space is left over after the element's width and splits it equally between the two sides. Equal margins on both sides means the element sits dead center.

That "defined width" part is load-bearing. A full-width block has no leftover space to distribute, so `mx-auto` on its own does nothing — you have to constrain the width first. The canonical 2026 pattern pairs the two as a unit:

```tsx
<article className="mx-auto max-w-3xl">
  <h1>Pricing</h1>
  <p>Pick the plan that fits your team.</p>
</article>
```

`max-w-3xl` caps the article's width; `mx-auto` centers what's left. You'll write this exact pairing for centered content columns and reading surfaces constantly. (`max-w-3xl` and the rest of the sizing model get their own lesson later in this chapter — here it's just `mx-auto`'s required partner.)

Now the boundary, because `mx-auto` is easy to overuse. It is specifically for a standalone block sitting in normal flow with no flex or grid parent to do the centering instead. The moment the parent *is* a flex or grid container, centering becomes that container's job — `justify-center` or `place-items-center` on the parent — and reaching for `mx-auto` on the child is the wrong tool. You'll learn those container-centering utilities in the flexbox and grid lessons later in this chapter. The rule of thumb: `mx-auto` for a lone block in flow, flex/grid centering for everything inside a flex/grid parent.

<Aside type="note">
Margins can also go *negative* — `-mt-4`, `-mx-2` — and that's legal and occasionally exactly right: an image bleeding flush to a card's edge, a hero element pulled up to overlap a banner. But a negative margin reached for to *fix* spacing is almost always a sign the layout structure underneath is wrong. Treat it as a deliberate, rare effect, not a patch.
</Aside>

## Debugging layout with the box-model panel

You now have a vocabulary for every band of an element — and that vocabulary pays off the next time a layout is "off by a few pixels." The slow way to find the culprit is to start guessing and bisecting CSS. The fast way is to open DevTools and *read the box model directly*, because the browser will tell you the exact resolved numbers if you ask.

Hover any element in the Elements panel and the browser paints a color-coded overlay on the page: content in blue, padding in green, border in yellow, margin in tan-orange. Look back at the diagram that opened this lesson — those are the *same four colors*, on purpose. You already know how to read the overlay; you've been reading it since the first figure.

For the exact numbers, the **Computed** panel shows the box-model diagram: the content's measured size in the middle, surrounded by the padding, border, and margin values on each of the four sides. That's where you confirm what `border-box` actually resolved to — inspect your `w-64 p-4 border` card and you'll see `256` in the center with `16` of padding and `2` of border folded inside it, exactly as the model promises.

That's the payoff of the whole lesson: "why is this element wider than I expect?" and "where is this phantom gap coming from?" become questions you answer in seconds by reading the panel, instead of editing CSS at random and hoping.
