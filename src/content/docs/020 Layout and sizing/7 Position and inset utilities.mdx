You're building a SaaS dashboard and the design calls for a small "Saved" toast that appears in the bottom-right corner of the screen, floating above everything, no matter where the user has scrolled. Reasonable ask. So you reach for what you know — flex, grid, a bit of margin — and none of it works. `margin: auto` won't push the toast to the bottom, because the parent isn't tall enough to push against. Flex aligns things *inside* a container, but you want this thing to ignore its container entirely and stick to the glass.

The 2026 answer is three utilities: `fixed bottom-4 right-4 z-50`. That string pins a toast to the bottom-right of the viewport in every real SaaS app shipping today. But two things in it should bother you. Why `fixed` and not `absolute`? And what is that `z-50` doing? By the end of this lesson you'll own all five `position` modes, you'll know exactly what each one anchors against, and you'll build badges, sticky headers, toasts, and drawers without ever hitting the "why is it in the wrong corner" bug that eats an afternoon the first time it happens.

Here's the one connection to make before we start. Every element you've laid out so far — flex items, grid items, stacked blocks — has lived in <Term definition="The browser's default layout: each block element takes a new line top-to-bottom, each inline element flows left-to-right, and every element reserves a slot that pushes its siblings around.">normal flow</Term>. Flow is the thing that makes a paragraph push the next paragraph down, and a flex item make room for its neighbors. `position` is how a small, specific set of elements opt *out* of flow — and the senior framing you should carry through this entire lesson is that it stays a small set. Most of your layout is still flex and grid. You reach for `position` only for the handful of elements that genuinely need to escape: a badge on a corner, a header that sticks while you scroll, a toast pinned to the screen.

## Five ways an element can be positioned

There are five values for the `position` property, and the fast way to never confuse them is to stop thinking of them as five vocabulary words and start thinking of them by a single question: **what is this element positioned against?** Once you know what each one is measured *from*, the offsets (`top`, `left`, and friends, which we'll get to) stop being mysterious.

Here are the five, framed that way:

- `static` — the default. The element goes exactly where normal flow puts it, and offsets don't apply at all. Every element you've styled so far has been `static`; you've just never had to say so.
- `relative` — stays in flow (its original slot is preserved, siblings don't move), but offsets now shift it *visually* from that slot. Its real job in 2026 isn't the nudge, though — it's to **become an anchor** for `absolute` children inside it. Hold that thought; it's the most important sentence in the lesson.
- `absolute` — leaves flow entirely. It reserves no space, so its siblings collapse together as if it were deleted, and it's positioned against the nearest *positioned* ancestor. This is the badge-and-overlay tool.
- `fixed` — also leaves flow, but it's positioned against the **viewport** — the browser window itself — and it stays put while the page scrolls. This is the toast and floating-action-button tool.
- `sticky` — the hybrid. It behaves like `relative` (in flow, holding its slot) until you scroll it to a defined offset, then it behaves like `fixed` — but only *within its parent's bounds*. This is the sticky-header tool, and it's the trickiest of the five, so it gets its own section later.

Tailwind gives you one utility per value, named identically to the CSS: `static`, `relative`, `absolute`, `fixed`, `sticky`. No surprises there.

The single most useful split inside those five is whether the element is <Term definition="In flow means the element reserves space and pushes its siblings around; out of flow means it reserves no space, so siblings lay out as if it weren't there.">in flow or out of flow</Term>. `static`, `relative`, and `sticky`-before-it-sticks all stay in flow — they hold their slot. `absolute` and `fixed` are out of flow — they reserve nothing, and the layout closes up around them. Get that binary solid before anything else, because it's what explains why dropping an `absolute` element into a row doesn't shove the row's other items aside: there's no slot to shove against.

## Feel the modes on one box

Reading definitions only gets you so far with `position`, because the whole point is *motion* — the way a box's relationship to its neighbors changes the instant you change its mode. So before we go deeper, play with it.

The playground below has one highlighted box sitting among three plain sibling boxes inside a bordered parent. The select changes the highlighted box's `position`; the two sliders set its `top` and `left` offsets. Switch between the modes and watch what happens to the *siblings*:

- On `static`, the box sits in its normal slot and the offsets do nothing — drag the sliders all you want, it won't budge. That's the default, and it's why you never had to think about position until now.

## What an absolute element is positioned against

This is the heart of the lesson, and it's the concept that turns "positioning is fiddly and unpredictable" into "positioning is mechanical and obvious." When you give an element `position: absolute` and then set `top` and `left`, the browser has to measure those offsets from *something*. That something is called the <Term definition="The rectangle an out-of-flow element's offsets are measured from — for an absolute element, the padding box of its nearest positioned ancestor.">containing block</Term>.

The rule for an `absolute` element is precise: its offsets are measured from the nearest ancestor that is *itself* positioned — meaning the nearest ancestor with `position` set to `relative`, `absolute`, `fixed`, or `sticky`. The browser walks up the DOM tree from the element, parent by parent, looking for the first one that's positioned. That one becomes the containing block. And here's the catch that bites everyone: if it walks all the way up and finds *no* positioned ancestor, it falls back to the <Term definition="The viewport-sized rectangle the browser uses as a last-resort containing block when nothing in the ancestor chain is positioned.">initial containing block</Term> — which is, near enough, the whole viewport.

That fallback is the source of the single most common position bug in real code, and you will hit it. The setup looks like this: you're building a product card, and you want a small "New" badge in its top-right corner. You drop an `absolute` badge inside the card, set `top-2 right-2`, and the badge lands in the top-right corner of the *entire page* instead of the card. You stare at it. Nothing about your markup looks wrong.

The reason is now obvious if you think about the rule: nothing in the card is positioned. The badge's `absolute` made the browser walk up looking for a positioned ancestor, it found none, and it anchored the badge to the viewport. The fix is a single class — `relative` on the card — which gives the badge a positioned ancestor to find before it climbs any higher.

Install this as a reflex, because you'll type it a thousand times: **`relative` parent plus `absolute` child is the atomic unit of corner-positioning.** Whenever you want to pin something to a corner or fill a region of an element, the parent gets `relative` and the child gets `absolute`. The `relative` parent reserves no offsets of its own — its entire job is to be the anchor. Seniors don't even pause on this; the moment they write `absolute` on a child, their fingers have already added `relative` to the parent.

The diagram below makes the "walk up the tree" mechanic literal. On the left, an `absolute` child sits inside a plain card with nothing positioned around it — follow the arrow and it shoots straight past the card, past everything, up to the viewport edge. On the right, the same child sits inside a card marked `relative` — now the arrow stops at the card, because the card is the first positioned ancestor the browser finds.

There's one exception to the "`fixed` anchors to the viewport" rule, and it's worth naming once so you recognize it when it bites — but we're not going to unpack it here, because it belongs to a later lesson. A `fixed` element normally ignores its ancestors and anchors to the viewport. *Except* when one of its ancestors has a `transform`, `filter`, or `perspective` set, in which case that ancestor silently becomes the containing block instead, and your `fixed` modal ends up positioned relative to some transformed parent halfway down the page. This is the same trap that the lesson on stacking context and `z-index` (later in this chapter) unpacks for a different symptom, and the fix in both cases is the same: portal the element out to `<body>` so it has no troublesome ancestors — a technique you'll meet in the chapter on portals and modals. For now, just file the recognition: **a `transform` on an ancestor can capture a `fixed` child.**

:::note
The whole containing-block model is visible in DevTools. Select a positioned element in the Elements panel and the layout overlay highlights the box its offsets resolve against — the fastest way to confirm an `absolute` child is anchored where you think it is.
:::

## Offsets — the inset utility family

Now that you know what an element is positioned *against*, you need the vocabulary for *where* on that containing block to place it. That's the `inset-*` family, named for the CSS <Term definition="Shorthand for the four offset properties — top, right, bottom, left — as a single group.">inset</Term> shorthand that groups the four offset properties (`top`, `right`, `bottom`, `left`). They all draw from the same `--spacing` scale you've used for padding and margin all chapter, so `top-4` is `1rem`, exactly like `p-4`.

Let's build the family up in tiers, smallest to broadest.

**Single edge.** `top-*`, `right-*`, `bottom-*`, `left-*` each set one offset. `top-0` pins the element's top edge to the containing block's top; `right-4` holds it `1rem` in from the right edge. Beyond the spacing scale they also take fractions and `top-full` (100% of the containing block), plus arbitrary values for genuine one-offs. The detail worth dwelling on is **negative offsets** — `-top-4`, `-left-2` — which pull the element *outside* its containing block. That's not a hack; it's the canonical move for a badge that overhangs a card corner. `-top-2 -right-2` lifts the badge up and out so it straddles the edge instead of sitting tucked inside it.

**Axis pairs.** `inset-x-*` sets left and right together; `inset-y-*` sets top and bottom together. `inset-y-0` is the everyday reach for "stretch this from the top of the containing block to the bottom" — you'll see it in the side-drawer pattern in a moment.

**All four at once.** `inset-*` sets all four offsets, and the one you'll reach for constantly is `inset-0`: top, right, bottom, and left all `0`, which means "fill the containing block exactly." Paired with `absolute` or `fixed`, `inset-0` is *the* full-cover pattern — it's how you lay a backdrop over a card or a dimming overlay over the whole screen.

<InsetUtilityReference />

**Logical offsets.** There's one more tier, and it's the one a senior reaches for by default. Physical offsets like `left-*` and `right-*` are baked to the screen's left and right — which is wrong the moment your app renders in a right-to-left language like Arabic or Hebrew, where "start" is the right edge, not the left. The logical forms fix that: `inset-s-*` and `inset-e-*` set the *inline-start* and *inline-end* offsets (the start and end of the text-flow direction), and `inset-bs-*` and `inset-be-*` set the *block-start* and *block-end* (top and bottom in normal writing). Under `direction: rtl`, `inset-s-*` automatically flips to the right edge — no media query, no duplicate styles. This is the same logical-property idea you met with `ps-*` and `pe-*` for padding earlier in the chapter, now extended to offsets.

The code conventions make logical properties the default for any project that might ever ship in an RTL language — `inset-s-*` over `left-*`, every time, so RTL works for free. In an LTR-only project, physical offsets are acceptable and the migration to logical is mechanical if you ever need it, but starting logical costs you nothing and saves a rewrite later.

:::note
If you've seen `start-*` / `end-*` in older Tailwind code or AI output, those were the original shorthands for logical insets — they still compile, but they're deprecated in favor of the `inset-s-*` / `inset-e-*` family, which is consistent with the block-axis pair. Author the `inset-*` forms.
:::

## Sticky: in flow until it isn't

`sticky` earns its own section because it confuses more people than the other four combined, and almost always for one of three specific reasons. Get the model right and the confusion evaporates.

Here's the model. A `sticky` element behaves exactly like a `relative` one — it sits in its normal slot, in flow, holding its space — *until* scrolling would carry it past the offset you set. The instant it would scroll past `top-0`, it stops and sticks at that offset, behaving like `fixed`. But — and this is the part that separates it from `fixed` — it only sticks **within its parent's bounds**. Once you scroll far enough that the parent itself leaves the screen, the sticky element leaves with it. It never escapes its parent. That's why a `sticky` section header stays pinned while you read that section, then slides away as the next section's header takes over: each header is sticky within its own section's box.

When `sticky` "doesn't work," it's nearly always one of three preconditions missing. Walk this checklist:

1. **No offset set.** `position: sticky` with no `top`, `bottom`, `left`, or `right` does nothing — it has no idea *where* to stick. This is the most common one. The fix is `top-0` (or whichever edge you want it to pin to).
2. **No scrollable ancestor.** Sticky needs *something* to scroll — either the page itself or an ancestor that scrolls on the relevant axis. If nothing scrolls, there's no scroll for it to react to, and it just sits there like a `relative` element. (Scroll containers are the next lesson's whole topic; for now, know that `sticky` depends on one.)
3. **Parent too short.** Sticky only sticks *within its parent's height*. If the parent is barely taller than the sticky element itself, there's no room to scroll inside it, so the element appears to do nothing. It's working — there's just nowhere to stick to.

So when a teammate says "sticky is broken," the senior reflex isn't to fiddle with values — it's to check those three: is there an offset, is there a scroll container, and is the parent tall enough? One of them is almost always the answer.

## Choosing a position mode

Five modes is enough to need a decision procedure, and the senior one collapses to just two questions asked in order. The walker below is that procedure — work through it the way you'd reason about a real element on a real screen. The value isn't in any single answer at the bottom; it's in the *order* of the questions, because that order is how an experienced developer narrows from "this thing needs to move" to "this is the mode" in a couple of seconds.

    `sticky top-0`. Needs an offset and a scroll container, and the parent has to be taller than the element.
  </Leaf>

  <Leaf id="leaf-absolute" verdict="absolute">
    `absolute` inside a `relative` parent. Use `inset-0` to fill the parent, or single edges (with negative offsets) to pin a corner.
  </Leaf>
</StateMachineWalker>

## The platform is taking over floating UI

One last orientation before you go, because the ground here is shifting in a way that changes what a senior reaches for. For years, tethering a tooltip to a button or a dropdown to its trigger meant reaching for a JavaScript positioning library — code that measured the trigger, measured the viewport, and computed coordinates on every scroll and resize. In 2026 the browser platform is absorbing that job, and two features are why.

The first is **CSS Anchor Positioning**. It lets an element position itself relative to *any* other element on the page — not just its containing-block ancestor — by naming an anchor (`anchor-name`) and pointing at it (`position-anchor`). That's the native answer to "tether this tooltip to that button," and it's usable today: it reached Baseline in 2026 (Chrome 125+, Firefox 147+, Safari 26+).

The second is the **Popover API** — the native `popover` attribute on an element plus a `popovertarget` button to toggle it (and a `showPopover()` method in JS). It gives you top-layer popovers, dropdowns, and menus with light-dismiss (click outside to close) and focus handling built in — no positioning library, no manual `z-index`. It renders in the <Term definition="A browser-managed layer that paints above all ordinary page content and escapes every stacking context — where popovers and dialogs render so they're never trapped behind something.">top layer</Term>, which is exactly why it sidesteps the `z-index` traps you'll study later this chapter.

Here's the senior takeaway, and it's the reason this is orientation and not instruction: you will almost never write these from scratch. shadcn's Popover, Dropdown, and Tooltip components (which you'll meet in the chapter on the shadcn component surface) wrap both features for you, and they also solve the `fixed`-under-`transform` trap by portaling to `<body>`. So the job here isn't to memorize the APIs — it's to recognize the names, know the platform is doing the heavy lifting, and reach for the wrapped component instead of hand-rolling positioning math.
