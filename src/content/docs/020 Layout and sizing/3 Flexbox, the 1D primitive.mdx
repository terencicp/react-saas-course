Open any SaaS app and the first thing on the screen is the same shape: a top bar with a logo pinned to the left, a sign-in button pinned to the right, and everything sitting on one tidy horizontal line. In 2026 the entire bar is three utilities — `flex items-center justify-between` — and a web developer writes that string without thinking. By the end of this lesson you'll read it and know exactly what each of the three does, and you'll build four more layouts that lean on the same handful of ideas.

In the last lesson you flipped `flex` on as a display mode: it changed an element's inner formatting context so its children stopped stacking and started lining up in a row. That was the switch. This lesson is about the machine behind the switch — the algorithm that takes a row (or column) of children with unpredictable content widths and decides who gets how much space, where the leftover slack goes, and how everything aligns. The whole thing rests on one idea, and it's the idea most people skip: a flex container has two axes. Get the axes concrete and `justify` versus `items` stops being a coin flip. Learn one more utility, `min-w-0`, and your flex rows stop blowing out their container. Those two moves are most of the difference between someone who has heard of flexbox and someone who ships it.

## A flex container has two axes

Setting `display: flex` (Tailwind `flex`) on an element makes it a <Term definition="An element with display: flex (or inline-flex). It lays its direct children out along an axis and distributes space between them.">flex container</Term>. Every direct child automatically becomes a <Term definition="A direct child of a flex container. It participates in the flex layout — it can grow, shrink, and be aligned. Grandchildren are not flex items.">flex item</Term> — there's no per-child opt-in, no class you add to the children. Turn the parent into a flex container and its kids are along for the ride.

A flex container lays its items out along two perpendicular directions, and this is the load-bearing fact of the entire lesson. The <Term definition="The primary axis a flex container lays its items along. Horizontal by default in a row; rotates to vertical with flex-col. Follows the writing direction, so it runs right-to-left in an RTL language.">main axis</Term> is the direction the items flow — by default that's a horizontal row, running left to right in a left-to-right language. (It follows the writing direction, so in a right-to-left language the same row runs right to left — a detail we'll trust rather than chase here.) The <Term definition="The axis perpendicular to the main axis. Vertical in a default row, horizontal in a column.">cross axis</Term> is the one perpendicular to it — vertical, in a default row.

Here is the reflex to build, and it's worth saying out loud before you touch a single utility: **every flex property targets one axis.** Properties that distribute items *along* the flow work the main axis. Properties that position items *across* the flow work the cross axis. So the question is never "which utility do I want" first — it's "which axis am I moving things on," and the utility follows. Name the axis, then pick the property. Almost every flexbox mistake a newcomer makes is reaching for the property before naming the axis.

The reason "name the axis first" matters so much is that the axes are not fixed to "horizontal" and "vertical." The main axis is wherever the layout flows. Flip a row into a column and the main axis rotates to vertical, dragging the cross axis to horizontal with it. In the figure below, switch between the Row and Column tabs and watch the two arrows rotate together. The boxes barely move; what changes is which arrow is labelled "main."

## Direction and wrapping

Which direction the main axis runs is yours to choose. `flex-row` is the default — items flow left to right. `flex-col` rotates the main axis to vertical so items stack top to bottom, which you just saw in the column tab. That's the choice you'll make on essentially every flex container: row or column.

There are also `flex-row-reverse` and `flex-col-reverse`, and these are worth knowing about mostly so you know *not* to reach for them casually. They flip the *visual* order of the items, but they leave the source order — the order in the HTML, the order the keyboard tabs through — untouched. So a `flex-row-reverse` bar looks like it runs right to left, but pressing Tab still walks the items left to right in the DOM. That mismatch is an accessibility bug: a sighted keyboard user sees focus jumping backwards across the screen. When you genuinely need one item to move, the better tool is `order-*` on that single item, which the layout and the source agree to disagree about in a more contained way. Treat the `-reverse` utilities as recognition-only and move on.

:::caution
`flex-row-reverse` reverses what you *see*, not the *source order* the keyboard and screen readers follow. Reordering for visual reasons while leaving focus order untouched is a real accessibility defect, not a cosmetic one.
:::

By default a flex container forces all its items onto a single line, shrinking them if it has to rather than letting any wrap to a new line. `flex-wrap` turns that off: items that don't fit flow onto the next line. You'll meet wrapping properly when you build wrapping grids later in this chapter; for now just know `flex-wrap` exists and that once items wrap, `gap-y-*` becomes the gap *between the rows*. We'll come back to spacing in a moment.

## Sizing items: grow, shrink, basis

Here's the production question this section answers: when the container is wider than its items need — or narrower — who absorbs the difference? A nav bar is rarely the exact width of its contents. Something has to stretch to fill the slack, or give up space when there isn't enough. Flexbox lets you decide, per item, exactly how that negotiation goes.

Three properties on each flex item drive it. <Term definition="flex-grow — how much an item grows to absorb leftover free space, relative to its siblings. 0 means don't grow.">`flex-grow`</Term> is how eagerly an item expands to eat free space. <Term definition="flex-shrink — how much an item gives up space when the container is too small, relative to its siblings. 0 means refuse to shrink.">`flex-shrink`</Term> is how willingly it gives space back when there isn't enough. <Term definition="flex-basis — an item's starting size along the main axis before grow and shrink apply. auto means start from the item's content size.">`flex-basis`</Term> is the size each item starts from before any growing or shrinking happens. The `flex` property is shorthand for all three at once. You should know these three exist, because every flex utility you write compiles down to them — but in real code you'll almost never set them by hand. Instead you reach for three named shorthands that cover the cases that actually come up.

- `flex-1` — grow *and* shrink, starting from a basis of zero. Because every `flex-1` item starts from nothing and then grows equally, they end up sharing the available space in equal portions regardless of their content. This is your reach for the thing that should fill the leftover room: a search input next to a button, the main content column next to a sidebar.
- `flex-auto` — grow and shrink too, but starting from the item's *content* size. It hands out leftover space proportionally on top of what each item already needs, so a longer item stays wider than a shorter one. Subtle, but it's the difference between "equal columns" and "columns sized to their content, then stretched to fill."
- `flex-none` — don't grow, don't shrink. The item is exactly its content (or declared) width, full stop. Reach for it when an element's size *is the design* and must not move.
- `shrink-0` — keep growing behavior at the default but switch off shrinking. This is the everyday reach for sidebars, fixed-width avatars, icons, badges — anything that should hold its size while its neighbors flex around it.

That last one comes with a warning that bites people: **flex items shrink by default.** Put an avatar with a fixed `w-12` into a tight row and flexbox will happily squash it narrower than 48px to make everything fit, distorting it. Adding `shrink-0` is what tells flexbox to take that width literally. When an avatar or icon looks subtly crushed in a row, a missing `shrink-0` is the usual culprit.

## Aligning items on both axes

This is where the two-axis investment pays off. Alignment is the same move twice — pick the axis, pick a value — and once you've internalized which axis is which, the utility names read themselves.

On the **main axis**, `justify-*` distributes the items along the flow. The values: `justify-start` (the default, packed at the start), `justify-end`, `justify-center`, `justify-between` (first and last item flush to the edges, the rest spread evenly between them), `justify-around` (equal space around each item), and `justify-evenly` (equal space between every item and the edges). `justify-between` is the nav-bar workhorse — it's what pushes the logo to one edge and the sign-in button to the other with no manual spacing.

On the **cross axis**, `items-*` positions the items across the flow. `items-stretch` is the default, and it's worth pausing on: it's *why* a flex child fills the full height of a row even when its content is short. Unless you tell it otherwise, every flex item stretches to fill the cross axis. The other values pull items off that stretch: `items-start`, `items-end`, `items-center`, and `items-baseline`.

That last one, `items-baseline`, is a detail an experienced eye catches and a newcomer doesn't. Picture a row mixing a large heading and a small timestamp beside it. `items-center` centers the two *boxes*, and because the boxes are different heights, the actual text ends up looking misaligned — the big text and small text don't sit on the same line. `items-baseline` aligns the *text baselines* instead, so the words sit on a common line the way they would in a sentence. Whenever you align text of different sizes in a row, baseline is usually what you actually want.

Two more utilities round out alignment, both recognition-level. `self-*` (`align-self`) overrides the cross-axis alignment for a *single* item — set the row to `items-center` but pin one avatar to the top with `self-start`. And `content-*` (`align-content`) positions the *lines* of a wrapped container relative to each other; you'll rarely reach for it, because `gap-y-*` usually handles the spacing between wrapped rows on its own. Know the names exist; don't go looking for them.

## Spacing items with gap

You've seen `gap` in every figure so far without comment, and that's intentional — in this course `gap` is simply how you space things inside a flex or grid container. Every flex container with more than one item gets a `gap-*`.

What makes `gap` the right tool is precise: it adds space **between** items and nowhere else. There's no leading gap before the first item or trailing gap after the last, so it never collides with the container's own padding — the items sit snug against the padding edge with even gaps only in the interior. `gap-x-*` and `gap-y-*` set the two axes independently when you need asymmetry, and as noted earlier, `gap-y-*` is the gap between rows once items wrap.

You met the rule that sibling margins are forbidden back when we covered the spacing scale, and `gap` is the reason that rule costs you nothing. The full comparison — why `gap` beats the old margin and `space-x` tricks — gets its own treatment later in this chapter. Here, just assume `gap` and use it.

## The min-w-0 fix every flex row needs

This is the single most common flexbox bug you'll hit in real work, and it's the part of this lesson most worth remembering. It shows up in week one of a real job, it's baffling the first time, and the fix is one utility.

The setup is ordinary. You build a row with `flex`: an avatar on the left, a flexible middle region with `flex-1` holding some text — a file name, an email subject, a URL — and a button on the right. It works fine until the text gets long. Then, instead of the text truncating with an ellipsis the way you'd expect, the *whole row* blows wider than its container. The middle region refuses to shrink, the button gets shoved off the edge or the row spills out of the card. `flex-1` was supposed to make the middle flexible — so why won't it shrink?

Here's the why, and it's not obvious. Flex items default to `min-width: auto` — a rule that says "never shrink an item below its <Term definition="min-content width — the narrowest an element can get without its content overflowing. Roughly the width of its single longest unbreakable run of text, like its longest word or an unbroken URL.">min-content</Term> width." Min-content is, roughly, the width of the longest unbreakable thing inside — the longest word, or a long URL with no spaces to break on. So even though `flex-1` says "shrink freely," that `min-width: auto` floor quietly overrides it and clamps the item at the width of its longest word. The item *can't* go narrower, so it pushes everything else out instead. Images and form controls carry their own implicit min-content widths, so the same blowout happens with an `<img>` or an `<input>` in a flex row.

The fix is to lower the floor. Add `min-w-0` to the flexing item and you override that `min-width: auto`, letting the item shrink below its content width — at which point the text finally truncates or wraps as you intended. Pair it with `truncate` (a text utility that clips the overflow to a single line with an ellipsis) for the classic one-line-with-ellipsis treatment. So the real, complete recipe for "a flexible text region in a row" isn't `flex-1` — it's `flex-1 min-w-0`, with `truncate` when you want the ellipsis. The `flex-1` alone is the trap.

The before-and-after is the clearest way to see it. The first tab is the broken row — `flex-1` only, long string spilling. The second adds `min-w-0 truncate` and the row behaves.

## Five flex layouts you'll reach for

Everything so far converges here. The point of this lesson isn't that you can recite flex properties — it's that an experienced developer composes layouts from a small, settled vocabulary of patterns rather than inventing CSS from scratch each time. These five cover the overwhelming majority of one-dimensional layout you'll write in a SaaS app. Learn them as units. Each tab below pairs a live rendering with its class string, so you can see the pattern and the code together and inspect either in devtools.

<TabbedContent syncKey="five-flex-layouts">
  <TabbedItem label="Nav bar" caption="Logo one side, actions the other, everything centered. The app's top bar — justify-between does the pushing.">
    <FlexLayout pattern="nav" />

    ```tsx "justify-between" "items-center"

    </header>
    ```
  </TabbedItem>

  <TabbedItem label="Form row" caption="A fixed-width label and an input that fills the rest. shrink-0 holds the label, flex-1 min-w-0 fills and stays shrinkable.">
    <FlexLayout pattern="form" />

    ```tsx "shrink-0" "flex-1 min-w-0"
    <div className="flex items-center gap-3">
      <label className="shrink-0">Workspace URL</label>
      <input className="flex-1 min-w-0" />
    </div>
    ```
  </TabbedItem>

  <TabbedItem label="Card stack" caption="The everyday vertical list. flex-col rotates the main axis down; gap spaces the rows.">
    <FlexLayout pattern="stack" />

    ```tsx "flex-col"
    <div className="flex flex-col gap-3">
      <Card>Invite teammates</Card>
      <Card>Connect a bank account</Card>
      <Card>Set your billing currency</Card>
    </div>
    ```
  </TabbedItem>

  <TabbedItem label="Toolbar spacer" caption="Push two clusters apart using leftover space as a tool — an empty flex-1 div between them, no margins.">
    <FlexLayout pattern="toolbar" />

    ```tsx "flex-1"
    <div className="flex items-center gap-2">
      <button>Bold</button>
      <button>Italic</button>
      <div className="flex-1" />
      <button>Discard</button>
      <button>Publish</button>
    </div>
    ```
  </TabbedItem>

  <TabbedItem label="Pinned footer" caption="Footer sticks to the bottom no matter how short the body is. The same spacer trick, rotated to the column axis.">
    <FlexLayout pattern="footer" />

    ```tsx "flex flex-col h-full" "flex-1"
    <div className="flex flex-col h-full">
      <header>Usage this month</header>
      <div className="flex-1">{/* body */}</div>
      <footer>Resets June 30</footer>
    </div>
    ```
  </TabbedItem>
</TabbedContent>

A couple of those layouts lean on a sizing utility — `h-full` on the pinned-footer card, and you'll want widths like `max-w-*` on a real nav bar. Those belong to sizing, which is its own topic later in this chapter; here they're just along for the ride so the patterns render. And the toolbar shows a quiet but powerful idea worth naming: a `flex-1` spacer is *leftover space used as a layout tool*. An empty growing div between two clusters shoves them apart with no manual margins at all. The pinned footer is the same trick rotated to the column axis — `flex-col` plus a `flex-1` body region pushes the footer to the bottom regardless of how much the body holds. Once you see leftover space as something you can place and grow on purpose, a lot of "how do I push this down/over there" questions answer themselves.

## DevTools: the flex overlay

When a flex layout looks wrong, the experienced move isn't to guess utilities — it's to look at what the browser is actually doing. Open the Elements panel in Chrome or Firefox and you'll see a small `flex` badge next to any flex container. Click it and the browser draws a colored overlay right on the page: the main and cross axes, the gaps between items, and the bounds of each item.

This is the two-axis model made literal. The overlay *draws the two axes this whole lesson has been about* — so when items are sitting somewhere you didn't expect, you can read off which axis they're aligned on instead of cycling through `justify` and `items` values hoping one sticks. Saw an item that won't shrink? The overlay shows it pinned at its content width. Wondering why everything's bunched at one edge? The overlay shows the main axis and where the free space went. Reach for it the moment a flex layout surprises you.
