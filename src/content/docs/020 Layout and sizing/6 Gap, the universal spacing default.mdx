You have a vertical stack of cards, or a row of buttons, and you need even space between them. It's the most ordinary layout task there is, and the answer has changed three times in seven years. Around 2018 you put `margin-bottom` on every card *except the last one* — and that "except the last one" was a real chore, a `:last-child` rule or a per-item bookkeeping you had to remember every time. Around 2021 Tailwind shipped `space-y-*`, which automated the chore but kept the underlying trick. Today, in 2026, you do none of that. You put `flex flex-col` (or `grid`) on the parent and add one `gap-*`. The parent declares the layout, `gap` does the spacing, and not a single child carries a spacing rule of its own.

```tsx del={5}
<ul className="flex flex-col">
  {invoices.map((invoice) => (
    <li
      key={invoice.id}
      className="mb-4 last:mb-0 rounded-lg border p-4"
    >
      {invoice.title}
    </li>
  ))}
</ul>
```

```tsx ins={1}
<ul className="flex flex-col gap-4">
  {invoices.map((invoice) => (
    <li key={invoice.id} className="rounded-lg border p-4">
      {invoice.title}
    </li>
  ))}
</ul>
```

That's the whole shift in two snippets: the spacing rule moves off every child and onto the parent, once. You've actually been writing `gap-*` for two lessons already — the flexbox and grid lessons used it as the assumed default without stopping to justify it. This lesson is where that debt gets paid. By the end you'll (1) reach for `gap` automatically inside any flex or grid container, (2) know the one narrow case where `margin` still earns its place, and (3) recognize the legacy patterns on sight when an old codebase — or an AI trained on a decade of old codebases — hands them to you.

This builds directly on the box model from *The box model and the inline/block axis*: you already know `padding` and `margin`, you know the `--spacing` scale that feeds them, and you've met margin collapse. This lesson slots `gap` between padding and margin and, in doing so, quietly narrows margin's job down to almost nothing.

## Padding, gap, margin — one spatial model

Before the utilities, the model — because once this lands, "where does this spacing go?" stops being three competing options and becomes one obvious answer per situation. The three spacing tools you have are not interchangeable. Each one answers the *same* question — "where does this space sit relative to the element?" — with a *different* answer.

- **Padding** is space *inside* the element, between its border and its content. It pushes the element's own content inward. You met it last as the inner band of the box model.
- **Gap** is space *between* sibling items inside a flex or grid container. It belongs to the parent, not to the children — and it sits *only between* items. It never adds a leading edge before the first item or a trailing edge after the last one.
- **Margin** is space *outside* the element, pushing it away from neighbors that are *not* its flex or grid siblings.

That middle one is the whole lesson, so look closely at where it lives.

<SpatialModelDiagram />

Here is the takeaway an experienced developer carries out of this, stated flatly: in 2026 you write `padding` and `gap` constantly, and `margin` almost never. Padding handles every "space inside," gap handles every "space between siblings," and that covers the overwhelming majority of real layouts. Margin is reserved for one narrow job — pushing an element away from something *outside* its container — plus the `mx-auto` centering case you already met. If you ever catch yourself writing `margin` *between siblings inside a flex or grid container*, that is the smell. The fix is always the same: delete the margin and put `gap` on the parent.

:::note
A quick vocabulary anchor for the rest of the lesson: <Term definition="The direct children of the same parent element. A flex or grid container's siblings are the items it lays out — its grandchildren are not.">siblings</Term> means the direct children of one parent. `gap` spaces siblings; it has nothing to say about grandchildren or about the parent's own outer edges.
:::

## Gap, the universal spacing default

Now the property itself, properly. `gap` is a single declaration you set on the parent of a flex, grid, or multi-column container, and it spaces all the children at once. Tailwind's `gap-*` compiles straight to it — `gap-4` becomes `gap: calc(var(--spacing) * 4)`, riding the *same* `--spacing` scale every `p-*` and `m-*` uses. That shared scale is the point: your spacing *between* elements stays in lockstep with your spacing *inside* them, because both resolve through the one variable you can tune in `@theme`.

The defining behavior — the one that makes `gap` the right call and not just a convenient one — is that it adds space *between* items and nowhere else. There is no gap before the first child and none after the last. Edge spacing isn't `gap`'s job; that's what the parent's own `padding` is for. So the canonical container is two utilities working as a pair:

```tsx
<div className="flex flex-col gap-3 p-4">
  <Row />
  <Row />
  <Row />
</div>
```

`p-4` insets every edge of the container uniformly; `gap-3` opens an even channel between the rows. Neither one steps on the other, and no child carries a spacing rule. This separation is exactly why `gap` survives changes that break the legacy patterns: the spacing is computed from the *layout*, not pinned to individual children.

That robustness shows up most clearly when items wrap or when the count changes. Add a fifth child to a `gap-3` column and it inherits the spacing for free — there's no `:last-child` exception to update, no per-item margin to add. Let a row wrap to a second line and `gap` spaces *both* axes automatically: the horizontal channel between items on a row, and the vertical channel between the rows themselves. Hold onto that wrapping behavior; it's the first thing we'll watch the legacy approach fail at in the next section.

For the rare case where you want different spacing on each axis, `gap` splits into two: `gap-x-*` controls the horizontal channel (column-gap) and `gap-y-*` the vertical one (row-gap). You'll reach for these mostly in wrapping layouts where rows should sit closer together than the items within a row, or the reverse. Worth knowing the plain `gap-*` you write every day is really CSS shorthand for `row-gap` plus `column-gap` set to the same value — but you'll write the Tailwind utility, not the longhand.

One stale worry to put down before we go further. Older tutorials warned that `gap` "doesn't work in flexbox" — that was true years ago and is the reason you'll still see the legacy tricks in old code. It hasn't been true since 2021. `gap` on flex containers ships in every major browser and clears the high nineties of global support. Use it without fallbacks. There is no caveat to carry.

## Why gap wins over the legacy patterns

This is the section that should leave you *convinced*, not just informed — because the payoff is that you'll fix these patterns on reflex the moment one shows up in a file you're editing. Every legacy approach shares one mechanism, and every failure below is a symptom of that single mechanism, so name it first.

Tailwind's `space-x-*` and `space-y-*` utilities compile to a selector that targets *every child except the last one* and gives it a margin — `space-y-4`, for instance, puts `margin-bottom` on each child but the final one. The exact selector is `:where(& > :not(:last-child))`; you'll never type it, but its *shape* is what matters. The spacing is computed **per child, by DOM position** — "are you the last child or not?" That single fact is the seed of every break. It makes the spacing fragile to *which* child happens to be structurally last and to the *source order* of the children. `gap`, by contrast, is one property on the *parent*, with no per-child selector and no position arithmetic at all. Watch each failure mode fall out of that difference.

**First, wrap breakage.** `space-x-*` puts a horizontal margin on each item except the last, and a margin knows nothing about where rows break. Let the items wrap to a second line and the spacing falls apart: items don't line up between rows, and there is no vertical spacing between the rows whatsoever, because the trick only ever added *horizontal* margins. `gap` handles both axes the instant items wrap — even horizontal channel, even vertical channel, no extra code. This is precisely the job `gap-x` and `gap-y` unify into one property.

**Second, and most important for the work you'll actually do: hidden and reordered children.** This is where the per-child mechanism turns into a real bug in a real React component. Because `space-y-*` margins live on "every child except the last," the moment you hide the *last* child with the `hidden` class — `display: none`, the way you toggle a row off without unmounting it — it keeps its place in the DOM as the structurally-last child, so it still soaks up the selector's skip while taking no margin and no space. The row that is *now* last on screen is no longer the structurally-last one, so the selector still hands it a bottom margin meant to sit *between* rows. You get a phantom gap hanging below the visible list, spacing against nothing. Reorder children with `order-*` and you hit the mirror image: the DOM-last child, which the selector quietly skips, is no longer the *visually* last one, so a separator goes missing exactly where you wanted it. `gap` is immune to both, because it spaces the items that are actually laid out — a `display: none` child is pulled out of layout entirely, so no space is ever reserved for it. There is no "last child" for it to get wrong.

    </div>
    **Phantom trailing gap.** The hidden row stays in the DOM as the structural last child, so the now-visually-last `Billing` row keeps the bottom margin meant to sit *between* rows — a gap hangs below the list, spacing against a row no one can see.
  </CodeVariant>

  </CodeVariant>
</CodeVariants>

**Third, margin collapse.** `space-y-*` uses real margins, and real margins *collapse* — the box-model quirk from the box-model lesson, where two adjacent vertical margins merge into the larger of the two instead of adding. So when a child has its own vertical margin, the `space-y` margin can collapse against it and silently under-space the list: you asked for 16px and got 12px, with nothing in the markup to explain the difference. `gap` is not a margin and never collapses. The spacing you ask for is the spacing you get, every time. That's not just a historical footnote — it's a live reason to prefer `gap` today.

**Fourth, right-to-left layouts.** A physical sibling margin like `margin-right` is pinned to the right edge and doesn't flip when the document switches to right-to-left, even though the layout around it does — so the spacing ends up on the wrong side. `gap` is direction-agnostic: it spaces along the layout's axis whichever way that axis runs, so an RTL flip just works. This is the same logical-vs-physical thread the box-model lesson opened with `ps-*` and `pe-*`; `gap` gives you that direction-independence in spacing for free.

So the rule, stated flatly: `space-x-*` and `space-y-*` survive only for the rare parent you genuinely can't turn into a flex or grid container without side effects — and that parent is much rarer than it sounds, because the clean fix is almost always just adding `flex flex-col` (or `flex`) and one `gap`. The old `* + *` "lobotomized owl" selector you might spot in hand-written CSS, and `space-y` itself, are **recognition-only**: dead for new code, alive in legacy and in AI output trained on it. When you see them, you now know both why they're there and what to replace them with.

## Borders between items with divide

`gap` adds *invisible* space between items. Sometimes you want a *visible* line between them instead — the hairline rules between rows in a settings panel, the thin dividers between clusters in a toolbar. That's a different question, and `gap` has no answer for it. `divide-*` does.

`divide-y-*` and `divide-x-*` draw a border *between* direct children — `divide-y` puts a `border-bottom` on each child except the last, so the rules land *between* the items rather than wrapping the whole container. (Yes, that's the same all-but-the-last mechanism as `space-*`; hold that thought for the watch-out below.) You add `divide-color-*` to tint the lines, and the whole thing composes with the parent's own `border` and `rounded-*` to produce the classic bordered list card — one rounded, bordered container with hairlines separating the rows inside it:

```tsx "divide-y" "divide-slate-200"
<ul className="divide-y divide-slate-200 rounded-lg border border-slate-200">
  <li className="px-4 py-3">Account</li>
  <li className="px-4 py-3">Notifications</li>
  <li className="px-4 py-3">Billing</li>
</ul>
```

Notice there's no `gap` here — the rows sit flush and a hairline separates them. But the two are not rivals; they compose. `divide` draws a line, `gap` adds space, and plenty of designs want *both*: items held a little apart *and* a rule drawn between them. They answer different questions, so reach for whichever the design asks for, or both together. The key framing to carry: **`divide-*` is not a substitute for `gap`.** When you want separation that's visible, you want `divide`; when you want separation that's just breathing room, you want `gap`.

:::caution
Because `divide-*` rides the same per-child "all but the last" mechanism as `space-*`, it carries the same sensitivity: hide the last child with the `hidden` class and the now-visually-last item keeps a divider it shouldn't have; reorder with `order-*` and the visually-last item still draws its line. It's accepted anyway, because a *visible separator between dynamic items* is a far rarer need than spacing, and there's simply no `gap`-style equivalent that draws borders. Worth knowing so it doesn't surprise you — not worth avoiding `divide` over.
:::

## When margin still earns its place

That leaves margin, and the loop on the spatial model closes here. The point of this section is to scope margin's surviving role precisely enough that you stop reaching for it out of habit. Here's the decision, mechanical enough to apply without thinking:

- **Use `gap`** between siblings inside a flex or grid container. This is roughly ninety percent of all spacing you'll write.
- **Use `margin`** to push an element away from something *outside* its flex or grid container — or in a context that has no flex/grid parent to own a `gap` in the first place. The honest rare cases are `mx-auto` for centering a lone block (from the box-model lesson) and pushing one element away from a non-sibling neighbor.
- **Never** use `margin` between siblings inside a flex or grid container. That's `gap`'s job, and a sibling margin there only invites the collapse and RTL bugs you just saw.

That's the senior mental shift in one line: padding and gap cover almost everything, and margin is the exception, not a co-equal third tool. Newcomers reach for margin first, because it's the spacing property they learned first. Experienced developers reach for `gap` first and treat a sibling margin as something to explain.
