You scaffold a fresh Tailwind app, drop in a chunk of plain, honest, semantic HTML — the kind you'd write on any other day — and the page comes back looking *wrong*.

```tsx
<div className="card">
  <h1>Upgrade to Pro</h1>
  <ul>
    <li>Unlimited projects</li>
    <li>Priority support</li>
  </ul>
  <button>Start trial</button>
</div>
```

The `<h1>` isn't a heading. It's the same size and weight as the list items next to it — body text wearing a heading's tag. The `<ul>` has lost its bullets and its indent; the two features sit flush against the left edge like loose sentences. And the `<button>` doesn't look like a button at all — no border, no fill, no native chrome, just a run of plain text sitting inline with everything else. You wrote correct markup and the browser rendered something that looks unstyled in a way it never does anywhere else.

There's a sentence almost everyone says out loud at this exact moment, and you may have just thought it: *Tailwind broke my HTML.*

It didn't. Nothing broke. The instant you added the `@import "tailwindcss"` line back in the last chapter, Tailwind quietly ran a small, deliberate reset called <Term definition="Tailwind's small built-in CSS reset, loaded automatically by the Tailwind import, that normalizes away the browser's default styles.">Preflight</Term> — and Preflight *deleted the browser's default styling on purpose.* The big bold heading, the bullets, the native button look: those were never yours. They came from the browser, and Tailwind stripped them so you could start from nothing.

This lesson is the flip from "broke my HTML" to "blank canvas." By the end you'll see that flattened look as a surface you *want* — know exactly what got deleted and why, recognize it on sight in DevTools, and know the two narrow situations where an experienced engineer deliberately brings some defaults back.

Two earlier lessons left a debt that comes due here. In *How the browser picks a winning rule* you saw that Preflight lives in `@layer base` — the reason a bare `<h1>` has nothing for your `text-2xl` to fight. And in *What flows down the DOM tree* you met Preflight's one `font: inherit` rule, the thing that re-opens form controls to your typography. Both were named in passing and left unexplained. This is where they, and every other reset Preflight ships, get explained in full.

## Preflight is the base layer, loaded for free

Before we list what Preflight strips, pin down *what it is and where it lives*, because that single fact turns the whole list from trivia into consequences.

Preflight is a small set of CSS rules Tailwind v4 ships. You never wrote them. You wrote one line — `@import "tailwindcss"` — and that line does more than pull in your utilities. Expanded, here's what it actually loads:

<div data-mark-color="green">

```css {3}
@layer theme, base, components, utilities;
@import "tailwindcss/theme.css" layer(theme);
@import "tailwindcss/preflight.css" layer(base);
@import "tailwindcss/utilities.css" layer(utilities);
```

</div>

Look at the third line. Preflight is `preflight.css`, and it loads into `layer(base)` — the same `base` layer you sorted your own element resets into last lesson. That placement is everything. Recall the layer gate from *How the browser picks a winning rule*: a declaration in a later-declared layer beats one in an earlier layer, no matter how specific either selector is, and the gate fires before specificity even gets a turn. Preflight sits in `base`. Your utilities sit in `utilities`, the last layer declared. So:

**Preflight lives in `base`; your utilities live in `utilities`; the layer gate hands utilities the win every single time.**

That's *why* you never fight Preflight, and why you'll never need `!important` against it. You don't out-specify it, you don't escalate, you don't reach for any hammer. You write the utility, and the layer order — decided before specificity is consulted — gives it the victory automatically. Preflight is structurally beneath everything you write. It cannot win a conflict with one of your utilities, by design.

So why ship a reset at all? Because the browser's defaults are a liability for a design system. They're forty years of accumulated decisions, they differ between browsers, and not one of them is on *your* scale — a default heading isn't on your type scale, a default margin isn't on your spacing scale, a default button doesn't read your color tokens. Preflight normalizes all of that away so that **every pixel of visual weight on the page comes from a utility you wrote** — predictable, tokenized, and byte-for-byte identical in every browser. Without a reset, your design system would be in a fistfight with the browser's defaults on every element. Preflight ends the fight before it starts by clearing the field.

## What Preflight strips, element by element

Now the catalog — and the chapter framing asks for it *named, not exhaustively walked*. You don't need to recite Preflight's stylesheet. You need to predict the flattened look of a common element and recognize each reset for what it is. So read every item below through one lens: this is the browser default being **subtracted**, and in each case the replacement is the same — *now it's a utility's job.*

The fastest way to feel that subtraction is to watch it happen. That arc — start full, clear to blank, repaint deliberately — is the whole thesis of the lesson in one gesture. Now the catalog behind it. Here are Preflight's headline rules in one place, the literal CSS Tailwind ships, with each reset tied to what it deletes and which earlier debt it repays.

<AnnotatedCode lang="css" maxLines={18} code={`
*,
::before,
::after {
  box-sizing: border-box;
  border: 0 solid;
  margin: 0;
  padding: 0;
}

h1, h2, h3, h4, h5, h6 {
  font-size: inherit;
  font-weight: inherit;
}

ol, ul, menu {
  list-style: none;
}

button, input, select, textarea {
  font: inherit;
  color: inherit;
}
`}>
  <AnnotatedStep meta="{6-7}" color="blue">
    Margins and padding, gone everywhere — every element, plus `::before` and `::after`. No default space above an `<h1>`, between paragraphs, around lists or `<figure>` or `<hr>`. Spacing stops being an accidental browser value and becomes yours, declared with `gap` and `p-*` and `m-*` on your `--spacing` scale. (The course prefers `gap` to sibling margins — more in the box-model chapter.)
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta="{4-5}" color="blue">
    Two resets in one rule. `box-sizing: border-box` makes every element measure its padding and border *inside* its declared width — the sane model, owned in full by the next chapter. And `border: 0 solid` is load-bearing in a way that surprises people: it pre-sets every border to zero-width, *solid* style. Your `border` utility only sets *width* — so it renders a visible line **only because** Preflight already set the style to `solid`. Strip Preflight and a bare `border` paints nothing, because a border's default style is `none`. (No color is pinned here, so a fresh border still starts at the current text color — `currentColor`, the thread from *What flows down the DOM tree* — until you set an explicit `border-*` color.)
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta="{10-12}" color="blue">
    Headings flattened — the debt from *How the browser picks a winning rule*. A bare `<h1>` is set to inherit its size and weight, so it renders at body size and body weight. There is no large default for `text-2xl font-bold` to fight, because Preflight already neutralized it. A heading's size and weight are now *entirely* whatever utility you put on it — nothing more, nothing less.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta="{15-16}" color="blue">
    Lists unstyled — no bullets, no numbers, and the margin/padding reset already took the indent. This feels like loss, but look at what a real SaaS UI is made of: nav menus, command palettes, option lists, dropdowns — almost all of them are semantically `<ul>` and visually *not* bulleted. This is what you want roughly nine times in ten. The tenth, a genuinely bulleted prose list, is `prose` — which is coming up.
  </AnnotatedStep>

  <AnnotatedStep meta="{19-21}" color="blue">
    Form controls re-inherit your typography — the debt from *What flows down the DOM tree*. This is the exact rule that re-opens the inheritance rebels: `font: inherit; color: inherit` makes a `<button>` stop wearing its native OS typography and take your body font and color instead. The controls rejoin the cascade you set up on `<body>`.
  </AnnotatedStep>
</AnnotatedCode>

That `AnnotatedCode` covers the four highest-value resets — margins, box-sizing and borders, headings, form controls — plus lists. A few more are worth a sentence each, because you'll see their effects but rarely think about them.

**Images and media go block-level and constrained.** Preflight sets `img, svg, video, … { display: block; vertical-align: middle }` and `img, video { max-width: 100%; height: auto }`. The first kills the mysterious sliver of space under an inline image (the inline baseline gap); the second stops an image from overflowing its container. Two of the most common image annoyances, gone in one move.

**Links lose their underline, and their color inherits.** A bare `<a>` comes out body-colored rather than browser-blue, with no underline — a clean slate you'll style per design with something like `text-primary underline-offset-*`, or get for free from a component later.

And a few you can file under "real but low-stakes": tables get `border-collapse`, `[hidden]` is enforced as `display: none`, and a placeholder's color resolves to the current text color at 50% opacity rather than a hardcoded grey. Recognize them if they come up; don't memorize them.

One reset people *expect* to find here and won't: Preflight does **not** set `cursor: pointer` on buttons. Tailwind v4 removed that — a bare `<button>` uses the browser's default `cursor: default`, matching native behavior. If a button feels unclickable, the cursor is *your* job: you add `cursor-pointer`, or, in practice, shadcn's `<Button>` handles it for you. It is not a Preflight rule, so don't go looking for it in `@layer base`.

:::note
A button with a pointer cursor isn't a Preflight default in v4 — it's a utility you add (`cursor-pointer`) or one your component library sets. Don't expect to find `cursor: pointer` in the reset.
:::

### Is it the reset, or is it a bug?

This is the one skill the whole lesson is built to install: when an element comes out flattened, decide *correctly* whether that's Preflight doing its job or an actual mistake. Most of the time it's the reset, and the right response is "add a utility." Occasionally it's a real bug. Tell them apart.

    The other two are real bugs. ## Reading Preflight in DevTools

You already own the instrument for this — the Styles panel, from the last two lessons. Here it's pointed at one specific sighting.

Select any flattened element, open the **Styles** panel, and scroll to the bottom. There, beneath every other rule, you'll find Preflight's rule sitting in a section labeled `@layer base`. That label *is* the recognition. Seeing the rule in the base layer tells you three things at once: it's a reset, it's structurally beneath everything, and any utility you add — which lands in `@layer utilities`, above it — will win cleanly. You're not looking at a culprit. You're looking at confirmation that the reset is exactly where it should be.

<PreflightInBaseLayer />

Attach one sentence to that sighting and carry it: **the reset is doing its job; the utility is my job.** That phrasing is deliberate, and it's the same muscle from the last two lessons — when something looks off, you read where each value came from instead of guessing.

And it tells you when the problem *isn't* Preflight. Suppose your `text-2xl` is genuinely there in `@layer utilities` and the heading is *still* body-sized. Preflight being visible in `base` is not the bug — it's behaving exactly as shown. So you trace it the way *How the browser picks a winning rule* taught: open Computed, expand `font-size`, read which rule won and which layer it came from. Maybe an unlayered rule of your own is sitting above `utilities` and stealing the win. Preflight in `base` is the innocent bystander; the trace will point you at the real culprit.

## When you bring defaults back, do it on purpose

Here's the discipline, stated once and then unpacked. An experienced engineer does **not** strip Preflight to undo the flattening. They lean into the blank canvas and paint with utilities — and in exactly two situations, they make a narrow, scoped addition that brings some browser defaults back. Two carve-outs, both tightly bounded. Everything outside them is a wrong fix.

### Carve-out 1: `prose` for content you don't author element by element

Picture the case. Your app renders a blog post, a changelog, a help article — written in Markdown, or pulled from a CMS — and what reaches your component is a *blob* of HTML: `<h2>`, `<p>`, `<ul>`, `<blockquote>`, `<code>`, `<a>`, all generated, none of it carrying your classes. You can't put a utility on each element, because you never wrote the elements. And for once you actually *want* the typographic defaults back — real heading sizes, bulleted lists, styled links, sensible spacing — because long-form reading is exactly where those defaults earn their keep.

That's what the `@tailwindcss/typography` plugin is for. You install it in your CSS next to the import:

```css
@import "tailwindcss";
@plugin "@tailwindcss/typography";
```

and then you wrap the rendered content in the <Term definition="The @tailwindcss/typography plugin's class that restyles raw, unclassed HTML content with a tokenized, themeable set of typographic defaults.">`prose`</Term> class:

```tsx
<article className="prose dark:prose-invert max-w-prose">
  {/* rendered Markdown lives here */}
</article>
```

For recognition, the family you'll meet: `prose` is the base; `prose-sm` and `prose-lg` size it; `prose-invert` flips it for dark mode; `max-w-prose` caps the line length for readability and `max-w-none` removes that cap. Here's the reframe that matters, though: **`prose` is not "undoing Preflight."** It doesn't strip the reset or fight it. It's a scoped, tokenized typographic system applied to exactly the subtree that needs it — the one place where you legitimately can't reach the elements one by one. It even themes through `@theme` tokens like `--prose-body` and `--prose-headings`, so it stays on your design system rather than escaping it. (You'll cash this in later, in the empty-state chapter; here, just recognize it as *the* right tool for content you don't own.)

### Carve-out 2: a scoped `@layer base` override for a third-party widget

The second case: you embed something you don't control — a payment provider's iframe host element, an embedded rich-text editor, a legacy script that injects its own DOM — and it *expects* browser defaults and visibly breaks without them. You bring the defaults back, but **scoped to that widget's container**, and you keep the override inside `@layer base` so it stays beneath your utilities and doesn't leak into the rest of the app:

```css
@layer base {
  .third-party-widget :where(h1, h2, h3) {
    font-size: revert;
    font-weight: revert;
  }
}
```

Two details here are payoffs from earlier in this chapter, landing together. `revert` — from *What flows down the DOM tree* — rolls the property back to the user-agent value, which is precisely "give me the browser default again," exactly the right tool for the job. And the `@layer base` plus `:where()` choice is straight from *How the browser picks a winning rule*: keeping the override in `base` means your utilities still win everywhere they apply, and wrapping the selector in `:where()` holds its specificity at zero so it never starts a specificity war. This is the rare moment the layer discipline and the revert keyword pay off at the same time — a scoped, polite override that brings defaults back without breaking the system anywhere else.

### The three fixes to reject

With the two legitimate moves in hand, the wrong ones are easy to name — and each is a reflex worth catching in yourself before you reach for it.

**Don't strip Preflight globally** to "fix" the flattened look. You'd lose `box-sizing: border-box`, the border-style reset (your borders would stop rendering — that's the load-bearing dependency you just met), and the form-control inheritance — and you'd hand yourself back the browser-divergent defaults you were trying to escape. The flattening you wanted to fix is local; the cost of stripping the reset is everywhere.

**Don't `@apply h1 { … }` or write a global `h1 { font-size: … }`** to "restore" headings. That re-introduces an element rule fighting your utilities — the exact anti-pattern *How the browser picks a winning rule* warned against. If you want heading-sized headings on markup you own, just put the utilities on them. If it's content you don't own, that's `prose`. There's no third option that involves resurrecting a global element rule.

**Don't reach for `!important`.** There is nothing here to beat. Preflight is in `base`, your utility is in `utilities`, and the utility already wins the layer gate before importance or specificity is even consulted. An `!important` here is the bug smell from the cascade lesson with no bug underneath it — you'd be forcing a win you already have.

There's a genuinely rare third case worth knowing exists, at recognition depth only: omitting Preflight *entirely*. That's legitimate when you ship utilities into an environment that already has its own reset — a widget mounted inside a third-party site, or a CMS with its own base styles — where running a second reset would collide with the first. Tailwind lets you import its layers selectively to handle that. But for a standalone SaaS app, the one you're building toward, **Preflight always loads.** The how is out of scope; the recognition is the point.

### Match the situation to the fix

Four situations, four correct moves. The skill is reaching for the right lever — and noticing when the right lever is "do nothing but add a utility."

## Preflight and the components you'll actually ship

Step back and notice something about everything you've seen in this lesson: you've been styling bare `<button>`, `<input>`, and `<h1>` elements. On a real project, you almost never do. shadcn's `<Button>`, its `<Input>`, your own components — they're all built *on top of* the blank canvas Preflight provides. They assume the reset ran, and they add tokenized styling on a surface where nothing competes. The reason a `<Button>` looks identical in every browser is that Preflight cleared the browser's opinion first, and the component painted its own on the clean surface underneath.

So here's the reframe to close on, and it's the same shape as the last two lessons. Preflight isn't a quirk to work around — it's the **clean foundation every later styling decision in this course stands on.** You don't fight the blank canvas; you paint on it, with utilities and with components. And the one time you bring defaults back, you do it the way an experienced engineer does: scoped and on purpose — `prose` for content you don't own, a narrow `@layer base` override for a widget that genuinely needs the defaults — never globally, never with `!important`.

The thread runs straight into the next lesson. The same `@layer` and inheritance machinery that makes Preflight predictable is what makes *design tokens* predictable too — a value set high in the tree, flowing down, overridable in a subtree. That's *Custom properties and the three-tier token model*, and it's where the blank canvas gets the system that paints it.
