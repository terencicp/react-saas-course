Your app is live, but the address gives it away. `your-project.vercel.app` is a fine URL for the team — you've been sharing the project alias since the first deploy — but it's the wrong URL to put in front of a customer. It's unbranded, it isn't yours, and to anyone who's seen a few of them it reads as "not a real product yet." Going live, properly, means a domain you own, resolving to your app, served over HTTPS with a green padlock in the address bar.

If your mental image of "get a certificate and serve HTTPS" comes from older tutorials, brace for that image to be wrong. There's a version of this task that involves buying a certificate, generating a signing request with OpenSSL, uploading files, and setting a calendar reminder so the thing doesn't expire at 2 a.m. and take the site down. None of that is the 2026 reality. On Vercel, the entire certificate side is **two DNS records and zero certificate work** — you point your domain at Vercel, and Vercel provisions, installs, and renews the certificate for you, forever, for free. Lead with that relief, because it changes how you should approach the rest of this lesson: the hard part isn't the lock, it's the pointer.

This lesson delivers four things, in order: the DNS records you set, the certificate Vercel handles for you, the one decision you make once and live with (apex versus `www`), and the one pitfall that bites teams who put Cloudflare in front of Vercel. Underneath all four is the question an experienced engineer is actually asking while doing this: *what's the minimum I have to configure, what does the platform handle on its own, and what's the failure mode while DNS is still mid-flight?*

## A live domain is a pointer plus a lock

Before any clicking, install the model, because everything else is just a special case of it. A live custom domain is two independent concerns that people new to this conflate into one:

- **The pointer — DNS.** The domain name has to *resolve* to Vercel's edge: when a browser looks up `app.example.com`, the answer has to lead to Vercel. That answer is a <Term definition="Domain Name System — the internet's name-to-address lookup; it turns a name like example.com into the address a browser actually connects to.">DNS</Term> record you create at your registrar. This half is your job, and you do it once.
- **The lock — TLS.** Browsers won't serve a modern site over plain HTTP; the domain needs a valid certificate to be reachable over HTTPS. That's <Term definition="Transport Layer Security — the protocol behind HTTPS; it encrypts the connection and proves the server is who it claims to be; the 'lock' in the address bar.">TLS</Term>, proven by a <Term definition="A signed file that proves a domain owns its TLS identity — it's the thing that expires and has to be renewed.">TLS certificate</Term>. This half is Vercel's job, and it's fully automatic.

You already own half of this model. In the first lesson of this chapter you learned that "production" isn't a deployment — it's a *pointer*, an alias Vercel re-aims at whichever immutable deployment is current. A custom domain just adds a *second* pointer, one hop upstream of the first. DNS points your domain at Vercel's edge; Vercel's alias points its edge at the live deployment. A single request from a browser travels through both pointers in series.

<TwoPointersInSeries />

Reusing the pointer metaphor is the point: you don't have to learn a new mental model, you have to extend one you already trust. Keep the two halves separate in your head for the rest of the lesson — when something goes wrong, it's almost always the *pointer* that's misconfigured or still propagating, never the lock, because the lock isn't yours to misconfigure.

## Apex versus www: pick one canonical

Here's the one genuine decision in this lesson, and it's worth pausing on because it's effectively irreversible. Not technically — you *can* flip it later — but flipping it after launch is a migration with redirect and SEO cost, the kind of thing nobody wants to schedule. So decide once, deliberately, now.

The choice is which form of your domain is the real one:

- **Apex** — `example.com`, the bare domain with nothing in front. The shortest, cleanest expression of your brand. The modern default for a marketing site.
- **`www`** — `www.example.com`. Historically easier to configure (you'll see exactly why in the next section), and some large sites still prefer it for that reason.

The rule that matters more than the choice itself: **pick one as canonical and redirect the other to it.** Never serve your site at both addresses as if they were equals. If both `example.com` and `www.example.com` independently serve the same content, search engines see two different sites with identical pages — duplicate content, which dilutes your ranking — and inbound links fragment across two addresses instead of accumulating on one. The fix is a permanent redirect (a 301 or 308) from the non-canonical form to the canonical one. Vercel does this redirect for you automatically once you've added both forms and marked one as the primary.

For this course, the default is **apex canonical** — `example.com` is the real address, and `www.example.com` permanently redirects to it. That's the cleanest brand URL, and it's the convention you'll see assumed from here on. State it, set it, move on.

One forward-looking note so the shape of a real SaaS doesn't surprise you later. The apex isn't the only name you'll eventually serve. A common split is the marketing site on the apex, the application itself on `app.example.com`, and documentation on `docs.example.com` — three subdomains, three purposes. And further out, multi-tenant products give each customer their own subdomain, `acme.example.com`, `globex.example.com`, provisioned dynamically. That per-tenant routing is its own substantial topic for later in the course; name it, file it, and don't let it complicate the decision in front of you now. Today you're wiring one domain.

Before the mechanics, make the decision concrete.

## The two DNS records, and why apex is the awkward one

This is where first-timers get stuck, so go slowly and lead with *why*, not with a record to copy. The whole confusion comes from one quirk: the cleanest kind of DNS record is forbidden at exactly the address most people want to use.

Start with the easy case. For `www` — or any subdomain at all — you create a <Term definition="Canonical Name — a DNS record that aliases one name to another name, rather than to an address.">CNAME</Term>. A CNAME says "this name is really an alias for that other name; go look *that* one up instead." You point `www.example.com` at the target Vercel gives you, and you're done. CNAMEs are standard, supported by every registrar, and they track changes automatically — if Vercel ever moves the underlying address, the CNAME still resolves correctly because it points at a *name*, not a fixed address.

Now the <Term definition="The bare domain with no subdomain — example.com itself; also called the zone root.">apex</Term>. Here's the quirk. The DNS specification forbids a CNAME at the apex. The reason is structural: the zone root has to coexist with a couple of mandatory records that define the zone itself (its `SOA` and `NS` records), and the rules say a CNAME can't share a name with any other record. So `example.com` already has records living on it that it can't give up, which means it categorically cannot also be a CNAME. The clean tool is off the table at exactly the address you most want to use.

There are two ways around it:

1. **An `A` record.** An <Term definition="Maps a name directly to an IP address.">A record</Term> points the apex straight at Vercel's IP. This always works, everywhere. The catch is that you're now hardcoding a specific address, and that address is Vercel's to change. Vercel publishes the current value in your dashboard — so you copy it from there, you don't memorize it. To make one IP serve every visitor on earth quickly, Vercel uses <Term definition="One IP address announced from many locations at once; the network routes each visitor to the nearest one.">anycast</Term>, which is how a single A-record address can be fast from anywhere.
2. **An `ANAME` / `ALIAS` record.** This is a <Term definition={"Behaves like a CNAME but is allowed at the apex — it resolves to the target's address behind the scenes; also called 'CNAME flattening'."}>special record type</Term> that some registrars offer. It gives you CNAME-like behavior at the apex: you point it at Vercel's *name*, and it auto-tracks any address change, just like a real CNAME would. When your registrar supports it, prefer it — it's the A record's benefits without the hardcoded address.

So the rule of thumb for the apex: use `ANAME`/`ALIAS` if your registrar offers it; fall back to the `A` record if it doesn't.

One thing matters more than any specific value here, and it's the durable lesson of this whole section: **never treat a particular Vercel IP or CNAME target as authoritative.** Vercel issues these per project now and rotates them — the value for your project may not match a value you saw in someone else's tutorial, and a value that's correct today may change. The instruction that survives is *copy whatever Settings → Domains shows you for this specific project.* Everything below is a clearly-labeled example, never a string to memorize.

In practice you don't go hunting for these values — Vercel hands them to you. The flow is short.

<Steps>

1. In your project on Vercel, go to **Settings → Domains** and enter the domain you want to add (start with the apex, `example.com`).

2. Vercel shows you the **exact record or records** to create — the type, the name, and the value, scoped to your project. This is the value you copy; it's the authoritative one, not anything from a tutorial.

3. Create those records at your **registrar** (wherever you bought the domain — that's where DNS lives).

4. Wait. Vercel polls your DNS in the background and flips the domain's status from **Invalid Configuration** to **Valid Configuration** once it sees the record. You don't click anything to make this happen.

</Steps>

## Propagation, and the automatic certificate

You created the record, the dashboard still says Invalid Configuration, and nothing seems to be happening. This is the moment that makes people thrash — re-saving the record, deleting and re-adding it, second-guessing the value. Don't. Almost always, nothing is broken; you're just inside the wait.

The wait is called <Term definition="The delay between changing a DNS record and the change being visible everywhere, while cached copies of the old record expire.">propagation</Term>. When you change a record, the new value isn't instantly true across the internet. Resolvers all over the world have cached the *previous* answer, and they'll keep serving it until their cache expires. How long they hold it is governed by the record's <Term definition="Time To Live — how long a resolver is allowed to cache a record before it must look it up again.">TTL</Term>. So "saved at the registrar" to "Vercel sees it" can be seconds or it can be hours, and the ceiling is set by the *old* record's TTL still living in caches around the world. Vercel polls and updates the status pill on its own; your job during this window is to wait, not to edit.

There's an experienced-engineer move that makes future changes painless: **lower the TTL before you make a change, raise it back after.** Think of TTL as a dial you turn down before surgery and up after. A day before you plan to change or cut over a record, drop its TTL to something small like 300 seconds. Now caches only hold the old value for five minutes, so when you flip the record the change propagates fast and the cutover window is short. Once everything's stable, raise it back to an hour or more so resolvers cache efficiently again and you're not generating needless lookups. You can't shrink the wait for *this* change retroactively — the old TTL already set it — but you can set up the *next* one to be quick.

Now the part this lesson promised in its first paragraph. The moment your DNS resolves to Vercel, Vercel automatically requests a TLS certificate from <Term definition="A free, automated certificate authority — it issues the short-lived certificates that browsers trust, and Vercel renews them for you.">Let's Encrypt</Term>, installs it on your domain, and **auto-renews it before it expires.** No signing request. No file upload. No cron job to remember. No renewal outage at 2 a.m. The entire historical certificate ritual collapses into a single instruction: *wait for the status to go green.* That's it. (Uploading your own custom certificate is an Enterprise-only feature, and you will almost certainly never need it — the automatic path is the path.)

There's one piece of HTTPS that is *not* fully automatic, and it's worth planting now so it doesn't surprise you later. Vercel automatically redirects plain HTTP to HTTPS on custom domains — visit `http://example.com` and you'll be bounced to `https://`. But the header that tells browsers to *never even try* plain HTTP again — `Strict-Transport-Security`, or <Term definition="HTTP Strict Transport Security — a response header instructing browsers to only ever use HTTPS for this domain, even if the user types http://.">HSTS</Term> — is an application-level response header, and that one is your job, not the platform's. You don't write it here. It lives in `next.config.ts` alongside your other security headers, and you'll set it and verify it in the launch-checklist lesson at the end of this chapter. For now, hold one clean distinction: **the HTTP→HTTPS redirect is automatic; the HSTS header is yours, and it comes later.**

<Aside type="caution">
If it's been a few minutes and the dashboard still says Invalid Configuration, that's propagation, not a bug. Resist the urge to re-save the record — re-saving doesn't speed anything up and can reset timers. Instead, check from *outside* your own machine using the `dig` command in the next section, which queries a public resolver directly and bypasses whatever stale answer your laptop has cached. Wait, verify externally, and let Vercel's poller catch up.
</Aside>

## Putting Cloudflare in front without breaking TLS

This is the pitfall, and it's the kind that's invisible until someone audits it — which makes it exactly the sort of thing an experienced engineer carries around as a checklist item. Set the framing straight first, though, because the framing has shifted.

In 2026, Vercel itself *discourages* putting a Cloudflare proxy in front of Vercel. Proxying through Cloudflare blinds Vercel's own Firewall and bot protection to real client traffic — Vercel sees Cloudflare's IPs, not your visitors' — it adds a network hop of latency, and it complicates caching. Vercel's built-in Firewall is the default edge-security answer for a 2026 SaaS, and you should reach for it first. So treat this section as conditional: *the default is not to run Cloudflare in front of Vercel.* Teams still do it deliberately for specific reasons — an existing investment in Cloudflare's WAF rules, multi-origin edge logic Vercel doesn't cover — and **if** you're one of them, here's the one rule that keeps the setup from being silently insecure.

When you front Vercel with Cloudflare, you move your domain's nameservers to Cloudflare and let it proxy requests through to Vercel (the "orange cloud" / proxy-on mode). The thing to understand is what that does to the lock. You no longer have one TLS connection — you have **two TLS hops** end to end:

- **Browser → Cloudflare.** Encrypted with Cloudflare's own edge certificate.
- **Cloudflare → Vercel.** A second, separate connection, secured by Vercel's origin certificate — the Let's Encrypt one from the previous section.

Two hops, two independent certificates, two separate terminations. The whole pitfall is that beginners model this as one connection with one lock, when it's really two connections each needing their own lock. And whether that second hop is actually locked depends entirely on one Cloudflare setting: the **SSL/TLS encryption mode**. It has three settings that matter, and only one is correct:

- **Flexible** — the Cloudflare → Vercel hop is **plain, unencrypted HTTP.** The browser still shows a padlock, because the *first* hop (browser → Cloudflare) is encrypted — but your traffic crosses the public internet between Cloudflare and Vercel in the clear. Worse, Vercel is HTTPS-only and will try to redirect the plain request to HTTPS, and you get a redirect loop. Wrong and insecure.
- **Full** — the origin hop is encrypted, but Cloudflare does **not validate** Vercel's certificate. The traffic is encrypted, but Cloudflare will accept *any* certificate on the far end, which means the connection is spoofable. Better than Flexible, still not safe.
- **Full (strict)** — the origin hop is encrypted **and** Cloudflare validates Vercel's certificate. This is the only mode where the connection is genuinely encrypted and verified end to end.

<TwoHopTlsPath />

The reason this is a genuine senior diff: from the browser, the padlock looks identical in all three modes. Flexible, Full, Full (strict) — the user sees the same green lock every time. Only Full (strict) is actually end-to-end secure, and the difference is completely invisible unless someone deliberately inspects that second hop. That's why it slips into production and sits there: nothing looks wrong.

<Aside type="danger">
Cloudflare on "Flexible" in front of Vercel is the classic looks-fine-but-insecure setup — a padlock in the browser, plaintext on the wire behind it. If you run Cloudflare in front of Vercel, the SSL/TLS mode must be **Full (strict)**. Anything less is shipping a security hole that no casual check will catch.
</Aside>

Then practice spotting the trap, because spotting it is the whole skill.

One operational footnote while you're moving domains around. If the domain you're adding is already attached to a *different* Vercel project, Vercel may ask you to prove you control it by adding a `TXT` verification record at your registrar. Add it, let Vercel verify, and then leave it in place — Vercel re-checks it periodically, so deleting it later can break the domain's attachment. It's an edge case, not part of the main flow, but it surprises people the first time they see it.

## Proving the domain is ready

A domain isn't "done" because the dashboard turned green and it loaded once in your browser. "Done" is something you can *prove*, from outside your own machine, with a handful of commands — and that proof is the durable artifact you walk away with. This is the same checklist-as-gate idea that runs through this whole chapter, and the launch-checklist lesson at the end will lean directly on it.

Here are the five checks. The first four are commands with a clear pass condition; the fifth is the human one.

<div data-mark-color="blue">

```bash "@1.1.1.1" "-sI" "-sIL"
dig +short example.com @1.1.1.1        # returns Vercel's IP, not your old host's
curl -sI https://example.com           # HTTP/2 200, no certificate error
curl -sI http://example.com            # 308 (or 301), Location: https://example.com
curl -sIL https://www.example.com      # follows the redirect down to the apex
# then open it in a browser: padlock present, click around
```

</div>

Each command earns its place, and the *why* is the part worth internalizing:

<Steps>

1. **DNS resolves to Vercel.** <Term definition="A command-line tool for looking up DNS records directly.">`dig`</Term> with `@1.1.1.1` asks Cloudflare's public <Term definition="The DNS server that answers a lookup — 1.1.1.1 is Cloudflare's free public one.">resolver</Term> for the answer, deliberately *bypassing* your own machine's cache. This is the single most important habit in the list: querying from outside your laptop dodges the stale answer your own resolver may still be holding, which is the number-one source of "it works for me but not for them." If this returns Vercel's address, the pointer is real.

2. **HTTPS serves a valid certificate.** `curl -sI` makes a request and prints only the response headers (`-I` is headers-only — you don't need the page body, just the status line and that there's no certificate error). A clean `200` with no TLS complaint means the lock is in place.

3. **HTTP redirects to HTTPS.** The same `curl -sI` against the plain `http://` address should come back `308` (or `301`) with a `Location` header pointing at `https://`. This proves Vercel's automatic HTTP→HTTPS redirect is doing its job.

4. **The canonical redirect works.** `curl -sIL` against `www.example.com` — the `-L` follows redirects all the way down — should land on the apex. This is the proof that your apex-versus-`www` decision is actually wired: the non-canonical form really does redirect to the canonical one.

5. **Browser sanity check.** Load it like a human would, confirm the padlock, click around. Automated checks prove the plumbing; a real load proves the experience.

</Steps>

The thread tying these together is one habit: **verify from outside your own machine.** Your laptop lies to you — it has caches, it has its own resolver, it may have visited the old site five minutes ago. A public resolver and a plain `curl` see what your customers see. Learning to check from the outside, not from the comfortable inside, is the part of this lesson that outlives Vercel.

A closing word for the case where this domain is already live somewhere else and you're cutting it over to Vercel, because that's a real operation with real downtime risk. Lower the TTL a day ahead so the cutover propagates in minutes, not hours. Prepare the Vercel records *before* you flip anything, so the new target is ready the instant you change the pointer. Expect a window of partial propagation where some visitors hit the new site and some still hit the old one — and remember the certificate provisions only *after* DNS resolves to Vercel, so there's a short beat between "DNS points here" and "HTTPS works here." Do the cutover during low traffic. And keep the old DNS records on hand: reverting the DNS pointer is your rollback for a domain cutover gone wrong — the same recovery instinct that runs through this chapter, applied to the pointer instead of the deployment.
